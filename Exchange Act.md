# Crossroads Exchange Act of 2025: Draft 1.0

Posted to r/Bulwarkomics
Draft: 1.0 | Date: June 29, 2026
Author: Thunderfish2

**Cross-reference.** Companion to the Monetary Act 9.6, Credit Union Act 5.6, FCL Structure Act 3.2, and Industry Act 4.2. The fiat currency framework, the issuance anchor (§1.5), the Reference Rate Anchor (§1.5A), the Crisis-Management section (§1.8), the CGCI foreign channel (Monetary §6), the corporate-governance requirements (Monetary §4.1), the Informal Economy Guarantee, asymmetric scrutiny, and the PPK/CED data architecture are governed by those acts. This act establishes the venue on which their tradable instruments are listed and the single rulebook that governs all trading. *(The FCL Act 3.2 and Industry Act 4.2 are pre-fiat in places — DAI/BAR language, older cross-references — and will need their own sync pass; the structural provisions this act relies on, FCL §2.5.5 and Industry §2.5, survive that cleanup.)*

**Note on figures.** Illustrative projections from a 2025 base; the design is in the structure and ratios, not the precise values.

## Scope note — Draft 1.0 (design decisions)

This act consolidates capital-market provisions that previously lived as separate, disconnected venues. Explicit record of what it decides:

- **One exchange, many boards, one rulebook.** A single Crossroads Exchange with segregated boards by instrument, a shared anti-speculation spine, and one clearing/registry backbone. Standalone act so no sibling act has to absorb a cross-cutting institution.
- **Three trading boards:** co-op investor shares (the former FCL Co-op Capital Index), credit-union special shares (the former CUSM), and **domestic** corporate equity (new). Plus the CGCI as a foreign-capital *channel*, not a trading board.
- **Corporate board scoped to domestic §4.1 firms only,** with an optional **49% outside-ownership cap** and the §4.1 disciplines as listing conditions. **Foreign firms are not listed here** — they are handled by the Industry Act §2.5 co-op-conversion ramp plus the CGCI channel.
- **Anti-speculation spine:** ownership trades, control does not; prices anchor to PPK/CED-audited value or par; minimum holds, daily caps, and trading bands dampen churn; a public backstop bid sits under every board.
- **Prohibition on synthetic structuring:** no tranching, no securitization that repackages or obscures underlying risk, no derivatives on listed instruments, no shorting, no leverage/margin. Diversification is achieved only through **transparent pass-through index funds**.
- **Off the exchange:** the Citizens' Metals ETF stays a wallet-native fund; the **sovereign metals reserve is never listed**; there is no debt board (Crossroads issues no bonds and runs no repo — Monetary §1.5A).

## Abstract

Establishes the Crossroads Exchange, the national secondary market for ownership stakes in the cooperative, credit-union, and corporate sectors. The Exchange is a single venue with segregated boards under one rulebook built to do the opposite of a speculative market: it lets capital find productive homes and exit when needed, with genuine price discovery, while structurally frustrating short-term speculation. Every board trades **cashflow claims, never control**; prices anchor to audited value; minimum holding periods, daily price caps, and trading bands dampen volatility; and a public backstop bid sits beneath each board so no holder is ever trapped — and no panic runs unchecked. Tranching, derivatives, shorting, and leverage are prohibited outright. The Exchange gives New Crossroads real, deep capital markets that remain entirely in character: legible, ownership-based, anti-takeover, and transparent.

---

## Section 1: Purpose and Scope

*Rationale:* New Crossroads grew tradable instruments in four places — co-op investor shares, credit-union special shares, the foreign CGCI channel, and the metals ETF — each with its own rules. A single exchange with one rulebook makes them a coherent national capital market rather than a patchwork, without surrendering the anti-speculation character that defines the system.

- **The Exchange** is a single national venue with **segregated boards by instrument** (Section 3), a **shared rulebook** (Section 2), and **one clearing, settlement, and registry backbone** (2.6).
- **What the Exchange lists:** co-op investor shares (3.1), credit-union special shares (3.2), and domestic corporate equity (3.3).
- **What flows through but does not trade as a board:** foreign capital, via the CGCI channel (3.4), during the Industry Act §2.5 conversion ramp.
- **What is deliberately *not* on the Exchange (3.5):** the Citizens' Metals ETF (a wallet-native fund), the sovereign metals reserve (a strategic state asset, never a security), and any debt instrument (Crossroads issues no bonds and runs no repo — Monetary §1.5A).
- **What never exists on the Exchange (Section 2.5):** tranched or securitized repackagings, derivatives, short positions, and leveraged/margin positions.

## Section 2: The Shared Rulebook (applies to every board)

### 2.1 Cashflow trades; control does not

The founding rule. On every board, only **non-controlling cashflow claims** may trade. Governance never circulates.

- Worker/member **base shares** (FCL §2.2, CU §3.1) are non-sellable and **never list**.
- Credit-union **special shares** are non-voting (CU §3.2).
- FCL **investor shares** are voting-capped (FCL §2.3) and only the non-voting economic interest trades.
- Domestic **corporate** shares trade subject to the 49% outside-ownership cap and the §4.1 voting disciplines (3.3).

A buyer on any board acquires the **economics** of an enterprise, never the power to control it.

### 2.2 Audited-value pricing

Prices anchor to **PPK/CED-audited value** — book value for co-op and corporate boards, par for the standardized special-share board — refreshed on the audit cycle (quarterly for elite/institutional listings). Market movement happens **within bands around the audited anchor** (2.3), not in free float. This keeps price discovery real but tethered to audited fundamentals rather than sentiment.

### 2.3 Dampening (the anti-speculation mechanics)

Each board carries dampening rules calibrated to its instrument; the spine is common:

- **Trading bands.** Special shares: 90%–110% of par (CU §3.3). Co-op investor shares: ±10% daily movement around audited book (FCL §2.5.5). Corporate equity: a daily limit band set by the Exchange Authority.
- **Minimum holding periods.** Co-op investor shares: 12-month minimum hold (FCL §2.5.5). Special shares: 3-year sale lock-up from issue (CU §3.2). Corporate equity: subject to the §4.1 0.5% short-term trade tax on sales under 365 days, collected at point of sale.
- **No intraday churn engine.** There is no high-frequency or algorithmic market-making tier; settlement and registry update on a defined cycle (2.6), not in microseconds.

These are not frictions to be optimized away — they are the point. The Exchange rewards the patient holder and frustrates the fast trader by design.

### 2.4 Public backstop bid

A public bid sits beneath every board so a holder is never wholly trapped — and, equally, so the band caps any panic:

- Co-op investor shares: the **CCIF** buys unlisted shares at **90% of audited book** if no buyer is found within 90 days (FCL §2.5.5).
- Special shares: the **90% floor** of the trading band, plus the co-op-index / member-buyback last-resort paths (CU §3.3).
- The backstop is a haircut exit, not a guarantee of par — it removes the trap without removing the risk.

### 2.5 Transparency and the prohibition on synthetic structuring

*Rationale:* the 2008 collapse was not caused by bundling as such; it was caused by **opacity plus tranching that manufactured fake safety plus leverage stacked on top** — complexity that hid correlated risk. New Crossroads' entire design is the opposite: trust through transparency, risk priced in plain sight. The Exchange therefore forbids the financial engineering that hides risk.

On all boards, the following are **prohibited**:

- **Tranching and synthetic seniority** — no slicing of a pool into "senior/junior" layers that manufacture a safety rating the underlying does not have.
- **Securitization that repackages or obscures the underlying** — no instrument whose holder cannot see exactly which issuers' risk they hold.
- **Derivatives on listed instruments** — no options, futures, swaps, or synthetic exposure.
- **Short selling** — no positions that profit from decline via borrowed shares.
- **Leverage / margin** — listed positions may not be bought on borrowed money or pledged to manufacture leverage.

Why this matters specifically here: the special-share board's price *is* the system's distributed bank-soundness signal (Section 6; Monetary §1.8). Tranching or wrapping it in synthetic structures would blind that signal — the very instrument built so risk could be seen would become a place where risk is hidden. The prohibition protects the crisis early-warning system, not just market integrity.

**Diversification is permitted only through transparent pass-through index funds (Section 4)** — which hold the actual underlying shares, disclose exactly what they hold, and add no synthetic seniority or leverage.

### 2.6 Settlement, clearing, and the share register

- **Settlement currency:** BWC, on a defined settlement cycle (not intraday).
- **A trade is a transfer, not a redemption.** On the special-share and co-op boards, a sale changes only the **share register** — who the dividend/patronage is owed to — and leaves the issuing entity's balance sheet untouched (CU §3.2, two doors). Cash moves wallet-to-wallet, off the issuer's books.
- **The share register is the load-bearing record:** the Exchange maintains the current holder of each listed instrument so dividends, patronage, and ownership route correctly. For converting foreign firms, the register also tracks the moving ownership split (3.4).
- **Accrued-distribution rule:** on transfer, the seller's accrued portion of the current dividend/patronage period is settled in the price or paid to holder-of-record on a set date, so the buyer does not collect distributions the seller earned.

### 2.7 Asymmetric scrutiny and privacy

The Exchange inherits the PPK/CED architecture and asymmetric scrutiny (Monetary §5, CU §6.6):

- **Individual trades are private.** The CED logs **aggregate board volume** (quarterly, system-wide) and per-issuer ownership splits; it does not expose who traded what below the disclosure thresholds.
- **Identity reaches a position only via the warranted Judicial Link Box.** Holdings are pseudonymous; identity is never merged with trading data outside a warrant.
- **Elite/institutional disclosure applies:** holdings above $10M are publicly disclosed (pseudonymously); listed issuers above $10M revenue file public annual reports. Ordinary citizen positions stay private.

## Section 3: The Boards

### 3.1 Co-op Board (formerly the FCL Co-op Capital Index)

- **Lists:** non-voting FCL **investor shares** (FCL §2.3, §2.5.5). Worker/member base shares never list.
- **Pricing:** audited book value, ±10% daily band, 12-month minimum hold.
- **Backstop:** CCIF at 90% of book if no buyer within 90 days.
- **Also the destination for:** stabilized **Freedom Shares** after a jubilee (Monetary §2.2, CU §5.4), and the **growing worker/co-op stake of converting foreign firms** as they co-op-ify (3.4).
- **Operated by:** the CCIF Alliance Network under Co-op Department and Central Council oversight (its existing steward).

### 3.2 Credit-Union Capital Board (formerly the CUSM)

- **Lists:** credit-union **special shares** past their 3-year lock-up (CU §3.2, §3.3) — uninsured, loss-absorbing equity, subordinate to all depositors.
- **Homogeneous coupon:** every tradable special share pays the standardized **reference + 2%** floating dividend (CU §3.2), so the board prices only **credit risk**, not yield — capital cannot chase a higher coupon because none exists.
- **Pricing:** par, 90%–110% band; trades above 105% require CLS notification (no approval).
- **Transfer, not redemption:** a sale changes only the register; the issuing credit union keeps all its capital (CU §3.3).
- **Disclosure:** each credit union's **member-vs-investor equity split** is published quarterly. No cap on outside ownership; transparency is the discipline.
- **Operated by:** the Exchange under Regional Board oversight.

### 3.3 Domestic Corporate-Equity Board

*Rationale:* the corporate board is the only board trading real voting equity, so it is the one place the anti-speculation and anti-concentration spine must be enforced as **listing conditions** — otherwise a casino reopens next to the patient-capital boards. Scoped to **domestic** corporations only; foreign firms are handled in 3.4.

- **Lists:** equity of domestic corporations subject to Monetary §4.1.
- **49% outside-ownership cap (the control rule).** A listed corporation may place **at most 49% of its equity** on the Exchange; **insiders — workers and/or founders — retain at least 51%.** Control cannot be bought off the market: no hostile takeovers, no activist capture. This makes the corporate board consistent with the FCL voting caps and the credit-union non-voting rule — ownership of cashflow trades, control stays home.
  - *Votes follow the §4.1 framework:* one share, one vote with the long-term tenure multiplier (2 votes/share at 5+ years, 3 at 10+), and **no separate share classes** (§4.1). The 51% insider equity floor plus the tenure multiplier together keep working control with insiders without resorting to dual-class structures.
- **Listing conditions (inherited from §4.1):** the 0.5% short-term trade tax (sales under 365 days), the quarterly-guidance ban (annual guidance only), worker board representation by headcount, and the stock-buyback limits. A corporation lists **only** by accepting these — there is no "clean" corporate listing that escapes the disciplines.
- **Capital-intensive exception.** A genuinely capital-hungry firm (the corporate analog of the FCL Heavy Industry Clause) may petition to exceed the 49% outside cap — never below a hard **33% insider floor** — with **Central Council approval + a Community Benefit Agreement + a Labor Peace Agreement**, renewable every 5 years. This mirrors the FCL Heavy Industry mechanism and is the pressure valve for firms a 49% cap cannot finance.
- **Pricing:** audited book anchor with a daily limit band set by the Exchange Authority.
- **Approval to list:** Regional Board → relevant Department → Central Council (simple majority); the exception requires final Central Council approval.

### 3.4 Foreign Capital — the CGCI channel and the conversion handoff (not a trading board)

*Rationale:* the Industry Act §2.5 already resolves the foreign-control problem better than any listing cap could: foreign-owned factories convert to a worker co-op model over time (**20% by year 1, 40% by year 10**). So a foreign firm is never a free-floating foreign listing that needs special-casing — it is a co-op-in-progress. The Exchange handles it through structures it already has, in sequence.

- **During the conversion ramp (years 0–10):** the firm's still-foreign-owned capital sits in the **CGCI** (Monetary §6) — non-voting, structurally illiquid (50-year leases, cash-export ban), the foreign channel. It is **not** a corporate-board listing.
- **The growing worker/co-op stake** (20% → 40%+) lists as **co-op investor shares on the Co-op Board** (3.1) under the normal co-op rules.
- **The registry handoff (the one seam to watch):** because the firm's ownership is *moving* — CGCI-held shrinking, co-op-held growing — the share register (2.6) tracks the drifting split over the ramp, and there is a defined handoff point at which CGCI-channel capital converts into co-op-board shares. The Exchange Authority publishes the conversion schedule per firm so the two instruments connect rather than falling into a gap.
- **No corporate-board special-casing.** Because foreign firms flow through CGCI + the Co-op Board, the corporate board (3.3) never has to solve "who holds the 51% of a wholly-foreign subsidiary." That case simply isn't on it.

### 3.5 Off the Exchange (by design)

- **Citizens' Metals ETF:** stays a **wallet-native fund** — bought, held, valued, and redeemed directly through the wallet (CU §3.4). It needs no trading venue and is not listed, so the Exchange remains unambiguously a market for ownership stakes.
- **Sovereign metals reserve:** the SMSWF holdings and the metal backing the cash flecks are a **strategic state asset and the system's catastrophe floor — never a security, never listed, never traded.**
- **Debt:** there is no debt board. Crossroads issues no sovereign bonds and runs no repo (Monetary §1.5A); the tradable universe is ownership and (off-exchange) the metals ETF — never debt or rates.

## Section 4: Index Funds (the diversification vehicle)

*Rationale:* a holder who wants to spread risk across many issuers — rather than betting on one credit union or one co-op — needs a diversification vehicle. The safe way to provide it is a **plain, transparent, pass-through index fund**: it holds the actual underlying shares, discloses exactly what it holds, adds no synthetic seniority, and carries no leverage. This delivers the benefit the bundling instinct reaches for (don't sink with one wobbly issuer) without any of the opacity-plus-fake-safety that Section 2.5 prohibits.

- **Special-Share Index:** an equal- or book-weighted fund holding the actual special shares of many credit unions, pass-through, fully transparent. A holder sees precisely which credit unions' risk they own; nothing is repackaged or re-rated. Because it holds the real shares, it does **not** blind the bank-soundness signal (Section 6) — the underlying prices remain visible.
- **Co-op Index:** the analogous pass-through fund over co-op investor shares (an evolution of the existing CCI index logic).
- **Rules:** index funds are themselves transparent and pass-through only — no fund-of-funds leverage, no synthetic replication, no tranching of the index. They list and trade under the same Section 2 spine.

## Section 5: Governance and Oversight

*Rationale:* consistent with the system's anti-concentration ethos, the Exchange is a **thin clearing/registry utility**, not a powerful central operator. Power stays distributed across the existing stewards.

- **Exchange Authority:** a thin utility operating the clearing, settlement, and share-registry backbone, under **Central Council oversight**. It sets daily limit bands and publishes conversion schedules; it does not own listings or take proprietary positions.
- **Board stewards:** the **CCIF Alliance Network** operates the Co-op Board (its existing role); **Regional Boards** oversee the Credit-Union Capital Board and verify corporate listing eligibility; the **Co-op Department** approves co-op and professional-FCL listings.
- **Listing approval chain:** Regional Board → relevant Department → Central Council (simple majority), mirroring the FCL Heavy Industry approval path. The capital-intensive corporate exception (3.3) requires final Central Council approval plus the Community Benefit and Labor Peace Agreements.
- **Audit & data:** the CED logs aggregate board volume and per-issuer ownership splits, all pseudonymized; system auditors (shared with the FCL/CU audit corps) review listings and may not request individual cash records. Identity reaches a position only via the Judicial Link Box.
- **Enforcement:** violations of the Section 2.5 prohibitions (attempting to tranche, create derivatives, short, or leverage listed instruments) are void as transactions and carry fines via the relevant Department, with appeal to the Special Arbiter Panel (SAP).
- **Amendments:** require a 75% Central Council vote (matching the FCL Act standard for structural change).

## Section 6: Crisis Linkage

The Credit-Union Capital Board is not only a market — it is an instrument of the crisis-detection system (Monetary §1.8, CU §6.8).

- Because special shares are homogeneous, national, and uninsured, the board's price **is** a distributed, real-time **bank-soundness signal**: pressure toward the 90% floor is read as institutional stress.
- The Exchange therefore has a standing duty to **keep that board transparent and un-engineered** — the Section 2.5 prohibition on tranching/derivatives is, in part, what preserves the signal's legibility.
- The Exchange feeds **aggregate, pseudonymized** board data to the CED for the §1.8 detection telemetry (CUSM price pressure, volume, dispersion) — institutional plumbing only, never citizen surveillance.
- In a declared systemic-freeze trigger, the Exchange continues to operate within its bands; the public backstop bids (2.4) and the automatic RLP→SWF liquidity line (CU §6.4, §6.8) are what arrest a panic — not a trading halt that would destroy price information.

## Design Philosophy (Informative)

New Crossroads built markets the way it built everything else — by inverting the assumption.

- **It is a market for ownership, never debt.** There are no bonds to trade, no repo, no rates. The entire tradable universe is a slice of someone's productive enterprise. A trader here can never speculate on sovereign debt or interest rates, because those instruments do not exist.

- **Cashflow circulates; control stays home.** You can buy the economics of a co-op, a credit union, or a corporation — never the power to govern it. Worker base shares never trade, special shares never vote, corporate insiders keep 51%. The market moves money toward good enterprises without ever letting money capture them.

- **Prices are tethered to audited truth.** Every board anchors to PPK/CED-audited value and moves only within a band. Price discovery is real but disciplined — sentiment cannot run away from fundamentals, because the audit is the anchor.

- **It rewards patience and frustrates speculation — on purpose.** Minimum holds, daily caps, a short-term trade tax, and a public backstop bid make this a place for capital to find a home and exit when it must, not a casino. The frictions are not flaws to optimize away. They are the design.

- **It refuses the one trick that blew up the old world.** No tranching, no synthetic seniority, no derivatives, no shorting, no leverage. The 2008 collapse was complexity hiding correlated risk; this system's entire premise is risk in plain sight. Diversification comes from transparent pass-through index funds that show you exactly what you own — never from engineering that hides it. And because the special-share board's price is the country's bank-soundness signal, keeping it un-engineered keeps the early-warning system alive.

- **Foreign capital is welcomed and domesticated, not special-cased.** A foreign factory behind the tariff wall does not get a free-floating listing — it becomes, over ten years, partly worker-owned, and its shares migrate from the foreign channel onto the co-op board as it co-op-ifies. The market doesn't bend its rules for foreign money; the foreign money bends toward the market's rules.

- **The metals reserve never touches the floor.** The citizens' investment ETF is a fund in a wallet; the sovereign reserve is the catastrophe floor under the whole system. Neither is a security, and the reserve is never within conceptual reach of a trading board.

Crossroads has real, deep capital markets — and they are unmistakably its own: ownership-based, anti-takeover, audited, patient, transparent, and quietly load-bearing for the crisis system. A place to invest, not a place to gamble.

That is the Exchange Act.
