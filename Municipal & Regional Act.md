# Crossroads Municipal and Regional Funding Act of 2025: Draft 1.0

**Posted to r/Bulwarkomics**
**Draft:** 1.0 | **Date:** June 27, 2026
**Author:** Thunderfish2

> **Cross-reference.** Companion to the **Government Act 4.18** (Regional Boards administer; Special Auditors enforce), the **Monetary Act 9.5** (fund mechanics mirror the credit-union RLP/DIP model; fiat BWC; SWF interaction), the **Credit Union Act 5.4** (mutual-fund design), the **Housing Act**, and the **Infrastructure Act 2.3** (cost-of-service and 155% recovery principles). Data uses PPK/CED (no CWD). The currency is fiat.

> **Note on figures.** Illustrative projections from a 2025 base; the design is in the structure and ratios, not the precise values.

## Overview

This act answers a question the national fiscal model leaves open: **how do the cities and regions that actually spend the money fund themselves?** It establishes a hybrid of the American and Canadian models, corrected for their known failure modes:

- **Municipalities self-fund** through a bounded revenue base (commercial/industrial and speculative land taxes, VAT, fees, licenses, capped development fees) — **but may never tax residential land and may never carry debt.**
- **Cost-of-service taxation** ends the sprawl-subsidy trap: entities pay in proportion to the infrastructure burden they impose, so dense development is rewarded and big-box sprawl pays its true cost.
- **Two mutual equalization funds** (Municipal Monetary Fund, Regional Monetary Fund) let rich municipalities and regions subsidize poor ones — peer mutual insurance, not central handouts — modeled on the credit-union RLP/DIP system.
- **Conditionality is formula-driven, not preference-driven:** to draw sustained support, a recipient's discretionary spending may not exceed what its net contributors sustain for themselves. The fund sets the *envelope*; the recipient keeps sovereignty over the *contents*. A domestic equalization mechanism with the IMF's discipline but none of its predation.

## Section 1: Core Principles

1. **No subnational debt.** No municipality or region may borrow or carry deficit debt. The mutual funds (Section 3) are the shock absorber instead of borrowing — eliminating the moral hazard of bail-out-financed overspending.
2. **No residential land tax — ever.** No municipality or region may tax residential land or the primary home. Taxing the home a citizen owns turns ownership into perpetual rent to the state and undermines the homeownership keystone (Housing Act). This is a constitutional protection, on par with the Informal Economy Guarantee.
3. **Cost-of-service fairness.** Taxes and fees are levied in proportion to the infrastructure burden an entity imposes — density rewarded, sprawl pays its true cost. No entity is taxed below its cost-of-service rate.
4. **Mutual equalization, not central charity.** Rich municipalities and regions subsidize poor ones through peer mutual funds, governed jointly by contributors and recipients, with conditions set by objective solvency math, not political preference.
5. **Self-funding within bounds.** Municipalities and regions raise their own revenue within the bounded base below; the funds backstop genuine deficits, not chronic overspending.

## Section 2: Municipal & Regional Revenue Base

### 2.1 Permitted Municipal Revenue

| Source | Notes |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Commercial & industrial property/land tax** | The anchor. Assessed on a **cost-of-service** basis (Section 2.3), not raw building value |
| **Speculative / undeveloped land tax (premium rate)** | Vacant and speculatively-held land taxed at a **higher** rate — discourages hoarding, pushes land into use, keeps lots affordable for homebuyers (Georgist tilt where it belongs) |
| **Local VAT / sales tax** | On goods and services within the municipality |
| **License & permit fees** | Business permits, trade licenses, event permits |
| **Service fees** | Utility access surcharges, waste, recreation, parking |
| **Capped development fees** | Cost-recovery only (Section 2.4) |
| **Fines & penalties** | Bylaw enforcement |

### 2.2 Prohibited Municipal Revenue

- **Residential land or primary-home tax** — absolutely prohibited (Section 1.2).
- **Below-cost tax breaks** — a municipality may not tax any commercial entity below its cost-of-service rate to attract or retain it (Section 2.3).
- **Debt / borrowing** — prohibited (Section 1.1).
- **Development fees as disguised revenue** — fees may only recover servicing cost (Section 2.4), never substitute for the forbidden residential tax.

### 2.3 Cost-of-Service Assessment (The Anti-Sprawl Rule)

*Rationale:* the "growth Ponzi scheme" bankrupts municipalities — they subsidize the arrival of low-density big-box sprawl, then inherit the perpetual maintenance liability of spread-out infrastructure while dense, efficient downtowns are taxed harder. This section inverts that math by taxing entities for what they actually cost the commons to serve.

- **Assessment basis:** commercial and industrial property is taxed in proportion to the **infrastructure burden per acre it imposes** — the linear road, water, sewer, and service cost required to serve it — not merely its building value. Low-density, edge-of-town development that requires extensive new servicing pays **more** per acre to serve; dense, infrastructure-sharing development pays **less**. Density becomes the tax advantage it deserves.
- **No below-cost rate:** a municipality **may not** tax any commercial entity below its demonstrable cost-of-service rate, and **may not** offer abatements, breaks, or special deals that shift the burden onto existing taxpayers. This bans the race-to-the-bottom giveaway that lures big-box chains at the existing downtown's expense.
- **No interregional poaching:** municipalities may not bid down taxes below cost-of-service to poach a business from another municipality — a negative-sum game only the chain wins. Detected poaching is a Municipal Fund governance violation.
- **Full-lifecycle cost recovery:** development that imposes a long-term maintenance liability must fund that liability — either through the ongoing cost-of-service rate, or a required **maintenance reserve** for the infrastructure it necessitates. *If you require the city to build and maintain a mile of road to serve you, you fund that mile for its life — not just on opening day.*
- **Not anti-business — pro-honest-pricing:** any chain is welcome; it simply pays the true cost of the infrastructure it requires, like everyone else. A business model that only works when the taxpayer eats its infrastructure liability is a subsidy, not a business — and the subsidy ends. This quietly favors dense, walkable, infrastructure-efficient development over subsidized sprawl.
- **Enforcement:** the Special Auditors (Government Act §4.3) may investigate a municipality's assessment schedule and any below-cost deal on their own initiative; an inflated cost basis or a sweetheart deal is exactly the books-cooking they are empowered to subpoena, charge, and (with judicial ratification) freeze assets over.

### 2.4 Development Fees (Capped — the Canadian Fix)

*Rationale:* uncapped development charges have become a major hidden driver of housing cost — an open-ended revenue grab paid by the young homebuyer. This section caps them at cost recovery.

- **Cap:** development fees may recover the **actual marginal administrative and infrastructure cost of servicing the development, plus a 50% margin — capped at 150% of demonstrable cost.** Not a profit center, not general revenue.
- **Marginal cost only:** fees recover the **new** servicing the development requires — not a share of the city's *existing* infrastructure, and not the general capital plan. New development pays for the new servicing it imposes; it does not subsidize the city's books.
- **Demonstrable cost required:** a municipality must document the actual servicing cost; the fee cannot exceed 150% of that documented figure. Auditable by the Special Auditors on developer or homebuyer complaint.
- **No residential dodge:** development fees may not be inflated to recoup the forbidden residential land tax — the lifetime cost of a home may not be loaded into a fee at the door to evade the annual-tax prohibition.

### 2.5 Regional Revenue & Resource Ownership

Regions fund themselves through regional commercial/industrial assessment coordination, regional infrastructure access fees (155% cost-recovery, Infrastructure Act 2.3), SWF allocations for designated programs, and — primarily — **their own resource revenue.** Regions are likewise barred from residential land tax, below-cost deals, and debt.

**Resource ownership (the Canadian model, corrected).** Resources developed within a region are **regionally owned and managed.** Regions levy their own **royalties, stumpage, and severance/extraction fees** as primary own-source revenue — this is a constitutional revenue *right*, not a reimbursement from the center.

**The national cut (split at source, not reimbursement).** On the same resource activity, the national government takes a **defined cut at source**, comprising:
- The **3% resource surcharge** (Monetary Act §2.1), which flows to the SWF, and
- **Normal corporate/co-op profit tax** on resource firms' profits (Monetary Act §4).

The remainder of the royalty is the region's. Two levels, two streams, one activity — no reimbursement, no dependency. This splits the ~$274B national resource line explicitly: the surcharge-plus-profit-tax portion stays national (SWF); the royalty portion becomes regional own-source revenue.

**Resource wealth flows through the Regional Monetary Fund.** Because regions keep their own royalties, **resource-rich regions become the natural net contributors to the Regional Monetary Fund (Section 3), and resource-poor regions the natural recipients** — equalization arising organically from resource ownership, exactly as in Canada. A resource-rich region's wealth is shared through the RMF (within the donor-cap guardrail, §3.3), and a resource-poor region draws on it (within the solvency-band conditions, §3.2). The resource right and the equalization fund are two halves of one mechanism.

*(Fiscal-model note: granting regions their royalties reduces the national surplus by the royalty portion of the ~$274B line. This is intended — it makes resource-rich regions genuinely wealthy and the RMF's natural donors. The national SWF take is preserved via the surcharge and profit tax. The exact split is set deliberately so the national books retain the surcharge/tax portion while regions hold the royalty; re-derive in the consolidated fiscal pass.)*

## Section 3: The Mutual Equalization Funds

Two mutual funds equalize within their tiers, modeled on the credit-union RLP/DIP backstop (Credit Union Act 5.4): pay-in, equalize-within-tier, no bailout without conditions, no cross-tier raiding.

### 3.1 Structure

| Fund | Contributors | Equalizes | Analogue |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Municipal Monetary Fund (MMF)** | Every municipality pays in (% of own-source revenue) | Among municipalities | Credit-union RLP/DIP for cities |
| **Regional Monetary Fund (RMF)** | Every region pays in (% of own-source revenue) | Among regions | Credit-union RLP/DIP for regions |

- **No cross-tier raiding:** municipalities help municipalities; regions help regions. The MMF and RMF are separate pools.
- **No national bailout:** the national SWF is the lender of last resort *only* on cross-regional emergency certification (5/20 Regional Boards + Central Council 6/11), preserving the no-bailout ethos.
- **Contributions** are a defined percentage of each unit's own-source revenue, set so the funds stay solvent across the cycle; returns stay in the pool.

### 3.2 Drawing From the Fund — The Conditionality Ladder

Support escalates in conditions with the depth of the draw. *Bad luck gets help with no strings; chronic overspending gets adult supervision* — the same surgical logic as the credit-union jubilee (Credit Union Act §5.4), applied to government.

| Draw level | Condition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Occasional / small** (one bad year, shock) | **No conditions.** It is insurance, not a bailout — a single-year deficit draws freely. |
| **Sustained** (multi-year deficit) | **The solvency-band test:** the recipient's **per-capita discretionary spending may not exceed the median (within a defined band) of its net contributors.** The fund sets the spending *envelope*; the recipient chooses *what* to cut to fit it. |
| **Chronic / deep** (structural, repeated) | **Supervised recovery plan:** the recipient writes a plan, the fund approves it; like a surgical jubilee for governments — misfortune is helped, chronic mismanagement gets oversight until solvent. |

**The formula, not the preference (this is what makes it non-predatory):** the condition is objective math, never a policy choice. The fund cannot say "axe daycare." It can only say: *"Your per-capita discretionary spend exceeds the median of the regions subsidizing you — bring it within band to qualify. The program is yours to keep; but something must give, because you are spending beyond what your funders sustain for themselves. You choose what."* The fund enforces the ceiling; the recipient keeps sovereignty over priorities. This is the entire difference from the IMF: solvency conditionality without policy dictation.

**Worked example:** A region wants a universal daycare program but runs a sustained deficit and draws from the RMF. Its per-capita discretionary spending is above the median of its net-contributor regions. The RMF does not target daycare. It tells the region: bring discretionary per-capita spend within band. The region may keep daycare and cut elsewhere, or cut daycare — its choice. The donor regions are not forced to fund, from their own restraint, a luxury they do not themselves sustain; the recipient is not told which luxury to drop. Both grievances resolved.

### 3.3 The Three Guardrails

1. **Joint governance.** Each fund's board is **contributors and recipients jointly** (weighted so neither dominates), confirmed by Regional Boards (11/20), with appeal to the SAP. The rich do not dictate to the poor (no Alberta-runs-Quebec), nor the reverse. A **dual-sector grandmaster** perspective (Government Act §3.4.5) is well-suited to the board for cross-cutting balance.
2. **Taper, don't cliff.** Support phases out as a recipient improves its own revenue — the recipient keeps a defined share of the upside from growing its tax base. No 100% clawback, no poverty trap, no "why bother growing."
3. **Donor cap & recipient floor.** No contributor may be drawn beyond a defined percentage of its surplus (donor protection); no municipality or region may fall below a basic-services floor regardless of its finances (recipient protection). Both grievances pre-empted.

### 3.4 Contributor Standing & Appeals (Symmetric Justice)

*Rationale:* Canada's equalization festers because only the rule exists — the donor has no recourse and no visibility. New Crossroads gives the **contributor** standing too, so the fund is legitimate to both sides. The recipient can appeal conditions it finds unjust (§3.2); the contributor can appeal draws it finds unjust — both on **objective grounds only**, both ending at the SAP.

**A net contributor may:**
- **Challenge eligibility / gaming:** formally petition that a recipient is underreporting its own revenue to draw more, misclassifying discretionary spending as essential to dodge the solvency band, or running chronic deficits that should have escalated to supervised recovery. Such a challenge **triggers a Special Auditor review** of the recipient's books (Government Act §4.3).
- **Challenge the band calculation:** contest that the net-contributor median was computed incorrectly, or that its own contribution is being drawn beyond the donor cap (§3.3).
- **Trigger chronic-recipient review:** force escalation of a region that has drawn for repeated years without improvement to the supervised-recovery tier.

**A contributor may NOT:**
- **Dictate the recipient's line items.** A contributor may challenge *whether* a recipient qualifies and *whether* it is within the band — **never what it cuts.** "This recipient is gaming the formula / spending beyond the band / drawing beyond my cap" is appealable; "this recipient shouldn't fund daycare" is never appealable. The fund sets the envelope; the recipient chooses the contents — and no donor may override that. The same principle that protects the recipient from politicized conditions (math, not preference) now protects the recipient from donor dictation, symmetrically.

**Mechanism:**
1. **First instance:** the contributor petitions the jointly-governed fund board (where donors already hold seats).
2. **Investigation:** an eligibility/gaming challenge compels a Special Auditor review of the recipient's books.
3. **Appeal:** either side — contributor or recipient — escalates an adverse or disputed ruling to the **SAP**, whose decision is binding (the dual-sector grandmaster on the SAP is well-suited to a cross-regional fiscal dispute).
4. **Systemic:** disputes implicating many regions or the equalization formula itself go to the **EGA**.

**Transparency right.** Every contributor may see, annually, exactly what it paid in, what each recipient drew, and the band calculation that justified each draw — at the **regional/municipal-government level** (institutional transparency per the asymmetric-scrutiny principle; citizen-level data stays pseudonymized). Solace requires not just that the rule exists, but that the donor can *verify* it is enforced — that the subsidized region actually adjusted its spending to the band. The black-box opacity that breeds grievance in real equalization systems is prohibited.

*The result for a net contributor (the "Alberta" case): you cannot be made to fund, on your own restraint, a recipient that out-spends you; if you suspect gaming you can compel an audit; if you are over-drawn you can appeal; and you can see, every year, that the recipient adjusted. You pay — but never blindly, never beyond your cap, and never for a luxury your own people forgo.*

### 3.5 Anti-Gaming

- Contributions and draws are tracked (pseudonymized fiscal aggregates) via the CED; municipal/regional identity and governance via the PPK-linked Government Act bodies.
- The Special Auditors may investigate any municipality or region suspected of underreporting revenue to draw more, overstating cost-of-service, or offering below-cost deals — on their own initiative.
- Disputes over conditionality or eligibility go to the SAP; systemic multi-region crises to the EGA.

## Section 4: Governance & Administration

- **Municipalities** set their own budgets within the bounded base; they may not run debt; they administer their own revenue and pay into the MMF.
- **Regional Boards** (Government Act §3.1) oversee the municipalities in their region, administer regional revenue, and pay into the RMF — exercising their state-legislature function.
- **MMF / RMF boards** (jointly governed, §3.3) administer contributions, draws, and conditionality; confirmed by Regional Boards; appeal to the SAP.
- **Special Auditors** (Government Act §4.3) enforce against gaming, below-cost deals, inflated assessments, and disguised development fees — independently and on their own initiative.
- **National SWF** is lender of last resort only on cross-regional emergency certification — never a routine backstop.

## Design Philosophy (Informative)

This act fixes the quiet bankruptcy of the modern municipality — and it does so with principles already woven through the rest of the system.

- **You cannot tax the home.** The one tax forbidden is the one that turns an owner back into a tenant. Everything the system does to capitalize a citizen by 30 would be undone by a state that could tax the home out from under them. The home is sovereign ground.

- **You pay what you cost.** Sprawl is not banned; it is simply made to pay its own way. The big-box store on the edge of town that needs a mile of new road pays to serve that mile — forever — instead of handing the bill to the dense downtown that pays for itself. End the subsidy, and the market quietly chooses efficiency. No business that pays its true cost is turned away; only the ones that were never really profitable without the taxpayer's help.

- **No debt, but never alone.** A municipality cannot borrow its way into a crisis the nation must rescue — but it is never left to drown either. The mutual funds are the cooperative answer to the bailout: cities insure cities, regions insure regions, the way credit unions insure each other.

- **Help without dictation.** When a poor region draws on the rich, the fund enforces only one thing: you may not spend, on your subsidizers' restraint, what they will not spend on themselves. *What* you cut is always yours to choose. It is the discipline of the IMF without its colonialism — solvency, not sovereignty, is the price of help.

- **Someone is always watching the books.** The independent prosecutors can open any municipality's or region's accounts on their own initiative — so the cost-of-service rules, the fee caps, and the fund conditions are real, not aspirational.

No taxed homes, no subsidized sprawl, no subnational debt, no predatory conditions, and no books that cannot be audited. That is how the cities and regions of New Crossroads pay their way.
