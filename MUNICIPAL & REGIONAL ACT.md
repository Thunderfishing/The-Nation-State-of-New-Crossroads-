## Section 1: Principles

1. **The region is the county.** New Crossroads has three tiers of government — federal, regional, municipal — and no more. There is no county layer. Each Regional Board (Chamber Act §3 — 20 boards, 11 members each) subdivides its territory into administrative districts covering every square kilometre. Municipalities self-govern within their boundaries; rural districts are administered by the Regional Board through district offices. (Municipal & Regional Funding Act §2.7.)

2. **The community owns the building; a co-op runs the service.** Every school, hospital, clinic, and community facility is owned by the community (the Local School Board, the Regional Health Board, the co-op council) and operated by a professional co-op (the Teacher FCL, the Doctors' FCL, the maintenance co-op). The community controls the asset; the co-op controls the profession. Neither answers to the other on the other's domain.

3. **No sovereign in the region.** The Sovereign Functions Act §8.5 bars the sovereign from domestic regulation — prices, rates, entry, licensure, policing, and sector policy. **There is no national police force.** Policing is a regional function, delivered by regional constabularies answering to the Regional Board, not the Sovereign Council.

4. **No debt at any level.** Regions and municipalities fund themselves from own-source revenue, equalized by the MMF/RMF, and may draw revolving capital loans from the mutual funds (Municipal & Regional Funding Act §3.7). They may not issue bonds, borrow externally, or carry debt of any kind.

5. **Self-funding by design.** The subnational tier runs on ~$134B of own-source revenue against ~$112B of spending — a ~$22B surplus that flows into the MMF/RMF pools. No federal transfer, no block grant, no revenue-sharing formula. The regions live on what they raise.

## Section 2: Regional Revenue (~$97B system-wide)

| Source | ~Annual | Notes |
|---|---:|---|
| **Regional income tax** | ~$40B | Own rate, same base and threshold as federal (income above $100,000, formal and informal alike). The threshold is the CED-visibility line and is not a regional choice |
| **Regional VAT** (~2% harmonized) | ~$30B | Harmonized with federal 5% and municipal ~1% on **one base, one form, collected once.** DLB zero-rated at all levels |
| **Resource royalties** (60% of 12%) | ~$22.1B | Own-source — the region owns the resource. Frostpeak mining regions are natural net contributors to the RMF; resource-poor regions are net recipients |
| **Regional access fees** | ~$5B | The regional share of infrastructure tolls and track access charges (Infrastructure Act §4.2) |
| **Total** | **~$97B** | |

**Distribution across regions is highly unequal, and this is by design.** Region 3 (South Valley City / Frostpeak) collects more royalty revenue than Regions 14, 16, 17, and 20 combined. The RMF equalizes — resource-rich regions are net contributors, resource-poor regions are net recipients — so that every region can deliver the same services regardless of what's underground.

## Section 4: Community Health Facilities — the Hospital Pattern

**Hospitals and clinics follow the same architecture as schools.** The community owns the building; a professional co-op operates the service; the MMF funds capital when the community can't afford it; and no mandatory fee may be charged as a condition of care (the healthcare premium system, Healthcare Act §4, is the funding mechanism — not facility fees).

### 4.1 Structure

| Level | Facility | Owned by | Operated by | Funded by |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Regional** | 1 mega-hospital per region (co-located at the SMR hub) | Regional Health Board | Doctors' and Nurses' FCL (professional co-op) | Healthcare premiums + FCL commons (~$19B health slice) + bottom-tier subsidy (~$45B) |
| **District** | Super-hospitals (major population centres) | District or municipal health board | Professional FCL | Same funding streams |
| **Local** | Clinics (community level) | Local health board or co-op council | Professional FCL or independent practitioners | Same + MMF capital lending for construction |

### 4.2 Capital Funding

- **New construction:** MMF/RMF capital lending (Municipal & Regional Funding Act §3.7) — revolving loan at reference + 0.5% (~4%), repaid over 10–20 years. The same mechanism as school buildings.
- **Routine maintenance:** regional revenue (§3 above, ~$12B line).
- **Major equipment:** the professional FCL funds medical equipment from its own revenue (healthcare premiums flow to the operating co-op, not the building owner). The facility owner provides the shell; the co-op provides the tools.
- **Volunteer maintenance corps:** the same sweat-equity model as schools (Education Act §3.2) — community members and apprenticeship-track students perform routine maintenance, earning journeyman hours.

### 4.3 Governance

- The **Regional Health Board** (a subcommittee of the Regional Board, not a separate body) oversees the mega-hospital and coordinates district/local facilities.
- The **NHB** (National Health Board, Chamber Act §7.1) sets standards, accredits facilities, and publishes outcome data — but **does not own or operate any facility.** It is a regulator, not a provider.
- The **professional FCL** (doctors, nurses, allied health) controls clinical decisions, hiring, and treatment protocols. The building owner cannot override clinical judgment — same separation as the Teacher FCL/Local School Board split in Education Act §3.2.

> **Why this works, and why it mirrors the school model.** A hospital is a building with professionals in it. The building is a community asset — it sits on community land, serves community members, and should be governed by the community. The medicine inside it is a professional discipline — it should be governed by professionals. Mixing the two produces either a community that overrides doctors (dangerous) or doctors who own the building (a cartel). The split prevents both. The community decides where the hospital is and what it costs to maintain. The co-op decides what happens inside it.

| As % of GDP | Revenue | Spending |
|---|---|---|
| Subnational | 2.2% | 1.9% |
| Federal (for comparison) | 7.7% | 6.7% |
| **Combined** | **~9.9%** | **~8.6%** |

> **A $6T economy run by a state that spends 8.6% of GDP.** The combined federal + subnational spending is **~$515B** — less than 9% of the economy. For comparison: the US is ~36%, the UK ~44%, France ~57%, the OECD average ~40%. New Crossroads is not a small state by accident. It is a small state by constitution — capped, barred from debt, prohibited from accumulation, and forced to return its surplus as a citizen dividend. **The economy is large because the state is thin, not despite it.**

## Design Note

Every real federation struggles with the same question: who pays for the school in the poor district? The answer is usually one of three bad options: the national government funds it (creating dependency), the rich district funds it (creating resentment), or nobody funds it (creating inequality).

New Crossroads has a fourth option: **the mutual fund lends to it, and the community repays.** The school is not a gift from the capital or a charity from the wealthy region. It is a loan from a pool the community itself contributes to — drawn when needed, repaid over time, the corpus surviving for the next community that needs it. The rich region contributes more to the pool (because its royalties are larger), but it contributes to **the pool**, not to **the poor region** — and the pool's governance is joint, not unilateral.

The hospital works the same way. The clinic works the same way. The bridge works the same way. **Every community asset follows the same pattern: community-owned, co-op-operated, mutual-fund-capitalized.** And no bill — not a tax bill, not a fee, not a mandatory charge — ever falls on a family as a condition of using the service their community built.
