# Crossroads Municipal and Regional Funding Act

---

**Note on figures.** Illustrative projections from a 2025 base; the design is in the structure and ratios, not the precise values.

---

## Overview

This act answers a question the national fiscal model leaves open: **how do the cities and regions that actually spend the money fund themselves?** It establishes a hybrid of the American and Canadian models, corrected for their known failure modes:

- **Municipalities self-fund** through a bounded revenue base (commercial/industrial and speculative land taxes, VAT, fees, licenses, capped development fees) — **but may never tax residential land and may never carry debt.**
- **Cost-of-service taxation** ends the sprawl-subsidy trap: entities pay in proportion to the infrastructure burden they impose, so dense development is rewarded and big-box sprawl pays its true cost.
- **Two mutual equalization funds** (Municipal Monetary Fund, Regional Monetary Fund) let rich municipalities and regions subsidize poor ones — peer mutual insurance, not central handouts — modeled on the credit-union RLP/DIP system.
- **Conditionality is formula-driven, not preference-driven:** to draw sustained support, a recipient's discretionary spending may not exceed what its net contributors sustain for themselves. The fund sets the *envelope*; the recipient keeps sovereignty over the *contents*. A domestic equalization mechanism with the IMF's discipline but none of its predation.

## Section 1: Core Principles

1. **No subnational debt.** No municipality or region may borrow or carry deficit debt. The mutual funds (Section 3) are the shock absorber instead of borrowing — eliminating the moral hazard of bail-out-financed overspending.
2. **No residential land tax — ever.** No municipality or region may tax residential land or the primary home. Taxing the home turns ownership into perpetual rent to the state and undermines the homeownership keystone (Housing Act). This is an entrenched constitutional floor (Chamber Act §11, Floor 2).
3. **Cost-of-service fairness.** Taxes and fees are levied in proportion to the infrastructure burden an entity imposes — density rewarded, sprawl pays its true cost. No entity is taxed below its cost-of-service rate.
4. **Mutual equalization, not central charity.** Rich municipalities and regions subsidize poor ones through peer mutual funds, governed jointly by contributors and recipients, with conditions set by objective solvency math, not political preference.
5. **Self-funding within bounds.** Municipalities and regions raise their own revenue within the bounded base below; the funds backstop genuine deficits, not chronic overspending.

| **VAT** | Yes — **5%**, DLB zero-rated (~$166B) | Yes — own rate, harmonized | Yes — own rate, harmonized: **one base, one form, collected once** |

Two facts in that table carry weight out of proportion to their size:

- **Residential land is untaxable at every level.** Not "capped," not "restricted" — *untaxable*, by all three governments, entrenched as Chamber Act §11 Floor 2. A home cannot be lost to a bill the owner never agreed to, and no level of government has the instrument to try.
- **Profit taxation is federal only** (§2.2). The Co-op Advantage Principle compares two rates; it can only be guaranteed where one hand sets both.


### 2.1 Permitted Municipal Revenue

| Source | Notes |

| **License & permit fees** | Business permits, trade licenses, event permits |

### 2.2 Prohibited Municipal Revenue

- **Residential land or primary-home tax** — absolutely prohibited (§1.2; Chamber Act §11, Floor 2).
- **Income tax** — prohibited. The income tax is levied federally above $100,000 (Monetary Act §4) and by regions (§2.5). A municipality's base is the bounded list in §2.1 and nothing else.
- **Profit tax** — prohibited, at this level and at the regional level (§2.5). **Profit taxation is exclusively federal.**

> **Why profit taxation is federal-only, and why it is structural rather than tidy.** The **Co-op Advantage Principle** (FCL Act §3.2, entrenched as Chamber Act §11) requires that a co-op's effective burden (profit tax + commons) never exceed an identical corporation's (profit tax + SWF contribution). **That comparison is between two rates, and it can only be guaranteed where one body sets both.** If twenty regions and hundreds of municipalities could each set their own profit rates, the Principle would be unenforceable in principle — not merely unenforced, but incapable of being checked, because no one would know what "an identical-size corporation's burden" was in any given place. Concentrating profit taxation federally makes the Principle **self-enforcing: one hand sets both rates, so the comparison is arithmetic rather than litigation.**
- **Below-cost tax breaks** — a municipality may not tax any commercial entity below its cost-of-service rate to attract or retain it (§2.3).
- **Debt / borrowing** — prohibited (§1.1).
- **Development fees as disguised revenue** — fees may only recover servicing cost (§2.4), never substitute for the forbidden residential tax.

### 2.3 Cost-of-Service Assessment (The Anti-Sprawl Rule)

- **Assessment basis:** commercial and industrial property is taxed in proportion to the **infrastructure burden per acre it imposes** — the linear road, water, sewer, and service cost required to serve it — not merely its building value. Low-density, edge-of-town development pays **more** per acre; dense, infrastructure-sharing development pays **less**. Density becomes the tax advantage it deserves.
- **No below-cost rate:** a municipality **may not** tax any commercial entity below its demonstrable cost-of-service rate, and **may not** offer abatements, breaks, or special deals that shift the burden onto existing taxpayers. This bans the race-to-the-bottom giveaway that lures big-box chains at the existing downtown's expense.
- **No interregional poaching:** municipalities may not bid down taxes below cost-of-service to poach a business from another municipality — a negative-sum game only the chain wins. Detected poaching is a Municipal Fund governance violation.
- **Full-lifecycle cost recovery:** development that imposes a long-term maintenance liability must fund that liability — either through the ongoing cost-of-service rate, or a required **maintenance reserve** for the infrastructure it necessitates.
- **Not anti-business — pro-honest-pricing:** any chain is welcome; it simply pays the true cost of the infrastructure it requires. A business model that only works when the taxpayer eats its infrastructure liability is a subsidy, not a business.
- **Enforcement:** the Special Auditors (Sovereign Functions Act §3) may investigate a municipality's assessment schedule and any below-cost deal on their own initiative; an inflated cost basis or a sweetheart deal is exactly the books-cooking they are empowered to subpoena, charge, and (with judicial ratification) freeze assets over.

### 2.4 Development Fees (Capped — the Canadian Fix)

- **Cap:** development fees may recover the **actual marginal administrative and infrastructure cost of servicing the development, plus a 50% margin — capped at 150% of demonstrable cost.** Not a profit center, not general revenue.
- **Marginal cost only:** fees recover the **new** servicing the development requires — not a share of the city's existing infrastructure.
- **Demonstrable cost required:** a municipality must document the actual servicing cost; the fee cannot exceed 150% of that figure. Auditable by the Special Auditors on developer or homebuyer complaint.
- **No residential dodge:** development fees may not be inflated to recoup the forbidden residential land tax.

### 2.5 Regional Revenue & Resource Ownership

Regions fund themselves through:

| Source | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Regional income tax** | Regions levy their own, on the same base as the federal tax — **income above $100,000, formal and informal alike** (Monetary Act §4). The threshold is the CED-visibility line and is not a regional choice. |
| **Regional VAT** | Harmonized with the municipal local VAT (§2.1) on a single base and collected once — the Canadian HST pattern. Two governments, one form, one audit. |
| **Resource royalties and stumpage** | Regionally owned and levied — **primarily** (below). |
| **Regional commercial/industrial assessment coordination** | Cost-of-service basis (§2.3). |
| **Regional infrastructure access fees** | Recovering **155% of annual maintenance, annually** (Infrastructure Act §4.1) — not of construction. |

**Regions may not levy a profit tax** (§2.2), may not tax residential land, may not make below-cost deals, and may not carry debt.


**Resource ownership (the Canadian model, corrected).** Resources developed within a region are **regionally owned and managed.** Regions levy their own **royalties, stumpage, and severance/extraction fees** as primary own-source revenue — this is a constitutional revenue right, not a reimbursement from the center.

**The national cut (split at source, not reimbursement).** On the same resource activity, the sovereign takes a **defined cut at source** (Sovereign Functions Act §7.1), comprising:
- **40% of a 12% royalty** on the resource sector's ~$306.4B gross (Industry Act §3) — **~$14.7B/year**, flowing to **sovereign revenue**, and
- **Normal corporate/co-op profit tax** on resource firms' profits (~$8B; Monetary Act §4).
- **National resource cut: ~$22.7B/year.** The regional 60% share of the royalty — ~$22B — stays with the regions, on top of everything else in the table above.


The remainder of the royalty is the region's. Two levels, two streams, one activity — no reimbursement, no dependency.

**Resource wealth flows through the Regional Monetary Fund.** Resource-rich regions become natural net contributors to the RMF, and resource-poor regions the natural recipients — equalization arising organically from resource ownership, exactly as in Canada.

### 2.6 Residential Cost-of-Service & the Anti-Exclusionary Rule

The residential-land-tax prohibition (§1.2) protects the homeowner — but on its own it would make a new subdivision a pure service liability, giving municipalities a perverse incentive to block housing. This section closes that incentive **without taxing the home.** The principle: you are never taxed for owning your home; you pay for the services you use.

- **Per-household service fees (the primary offset).** A municipality may levy per-household service fees that recover the cost of services a household consumes — water, sewer, waste, road wear, streetlighting — billed **on consumption or as a flat service charge, never assessed on the value of the land or home.**

- **Residential development is not a net liability.** A subdivision's residents pay **local VAT/sales tax** on what they buy, **license and service fees**, and they are the workforce and customers that make the municipality's commercial property viable. Residential households pay their way through **service fees + local consumption taxes**, not through a lien on ownership.

- **Schools carve-out.** The single largest residential service cost — education — is **not** a municipal burden. Schooling is funded by the **$10,000 voucher and the Education Act** (a regional/national function, whose minimum is guaranteed as Chamber Act §12.2), so it does not sit on the municipal books.

- **Development fees already cover the growth cost.** The capped development fee (§2.4) makes a new subdivision fund new infrastructure up front; §2.6 covers ongoing operating cost. Together they make residential growth carry its full weight — capital and operating — with no tax on ownership.

- **Anti-exclusionary-zoning guardrail.** A municipality may not use zoning, permitting, or service-fee design to exclude residential development for revenue-protection or fiscal-liability reasons. Exclusionary zoning aimed at keeping out housing to protect a bounded budget is a Municipal Fund governance violation, investigable by the Special Auditors (Sovereign Functions Act §3). Genuine health, safety, environmental, and infrastructure-capacity zoning is unaffected — this bars fiscal exclusion, not legitimate land-use planning.

> **Net effect:** welcoming housing no longer makes a municipality poorer. Households fund their own services through use and consumption; schools are off the municipal books; growth costs are recovered at the door; and both the motive and the means to block housing are removed — all while the home itself is never taxed.

### 2.7 Rural Governance — Regional Districts and Co-op Councils

**The space between towns.** New Crossroads is 2.7M km². The 20 regions average ~135,000 km² each — roughly the size of Greece. Municipalities cover the urban cores. But the Heartland wheat farms, the Frostpeak mining roads, the west-coast fishing settlements, and the parkweb corridors between towns have no municipal government.

**There is no county layer.** The system is designed to be thin. Adding a fourth tier of government (federal → regional → county → municipal) would duplicate functions, require elections, and create bureaucracy in areas with sparse population. Instead:

**The Regional Board is the county.** Each Regional Board (Chamber Act §3 — 20 boards, 11 members each) subdivides its territory into **administrative districts** covering the full area — every square kilometre is in a district, whether urban or rural. Municipal districts are self-governing for urban functions. **Rural districts** are administered directly by the Regional Board through district offices.

**Rural district functions:**

| Function | Handled by |
|---|---|
| Rural road maintenance | Regional Board district office, funded from regional revenue |
| Land use and zoning (non-municipal) | Regional Board, subject to the anti-exclusionary rule (§2.6) and the parkweb overlay (Parks Act) |
| Rural policing | Regional constabulary (not national — the sovereign has no domestic police force, §8.5) |
| Agricultural extension and support | Regional Board + farming co-ops |
| Emergency services | Regional Board + volunteer co-op brigades |
| Parkweb management (non-municipal) | NInfraB/Parks Board, with Regional Board coordination |

**Co-op council delegation.** Where rural co-ops are concentrated — a mining district in the Frostpeak, a farming district in the Heartland, a fishing settlement on the west coast — the Regional Board may **delegate operational functions** to a **co-op council**: an assembly of the local FCLs that manages roads, coordinates services, and advises the Regional Board on land use. The co-op council is not a government; it is a **private federation performing a public function**, the same pattern as the CCIF Alliance Network (FCL Act §3.8.7). Delegation is revocable by the Regional Board (6/11).

**Revenue:** rural districts are funded from **regional revenue** (income tax, VAT, royalties, access fees — §2.5). They do not levy their own taxes. Co-op councils may levy a **maintenance fee** on member co-ops for road upkeep and shared services, capped at cost-of-service (§2.3 principles apply).

> **Why this works without a county.** A county is a general-purpose government with elected officials, taxing power, and independent authority. New Crossroads doesn't need one because the Regional Board already has the revenue (royalties alone fund substantial rural services), the administrative capacity (11-member board with district offices), and the constitutional authority (Chamber Act §3). Adding an elected county government between the region and the municipality would create a layer of government in areas that have, by definition, sparse population — meaning the county would be a large bureaucracy serving few people. The regional-district model serves those people through the Regional Board they already elect, and delegates day-to-day operations to the co-ops that are already on the ground.

## Section 3: The Mutual Equalization Funds

Two mutual funds equalize within their tiers, modeled on the credit-union RLP/DIP backstop (Credit Union Act 5.8): pay-in, equalize-within-tier, no bailout without conditions, no cross-tier raiding.

### 3.1 Structure

| Fund | Contributors | Equalizes | Analogue |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Municipal Monetary Fund (MMF)** | Every municipality pays in (% of own-source revenue) | Among municipalities | Credit-union RLP/DIP for cities |
| **Regional Monetary Fund (RMF)** | Every region pays in (% of own-source revenue) | Among regions | Credit-union RLP/DIP for regions |

- **No cross-tier raiding:** municipalities help municipalities; regions help regions.
- **No national bailout:** the national SWF (Sovereign Functions Act §5) is the lender of last resort only on cross-regional emergency certification (5/20 Regional Boards + Sovereign Council 5/7). Where the healthcare guarantee (Chamber Act §12.1) or the education guarantee (§12.2) creates an obligation the regional fund cannot satisfy, the equalization funds and, in extremis, the commons waterfall (Monetary Act §3) must close the gap — a shortfall against a guarantee is a rights violation, not a regional variation.
- **Contributions** are a defined percentage of each unit's own-source revenue, set so the funds stay solvent across the cycle; returns stay in the pool.
- **Per-capita-aware formula (MMF).** The MMF's formula accounts for residential population as a legitimate cost driver — a bedroom community is not pushed toward deficit simply for housing people.

### 3.2 Drawing From the Fund — The Conditionality Ladder

| Draw level | Condition |
|---|---|
| **Occasional / small** (one bad year, shock) | **No conditions.** Insurance, not a bailout. |
| **Sustained** (multi-year deficit) | **The solvency-band test:** per-capita discretionary spending may not exceed the median (within a defined band) of net contributors. The fund sets the envelope; the recipient chooses what to cut. |
| **Chronic / deep** (structural, repeated) | **Supervised recovery plan:** the recipient writes a plan, the fund approves it — misfortune is helped, chronic mismanagement gets oversight until solvent. |

**The formula, not the preference.** The condition is objective math, never a policy choice. The fund cannot say "axe daycare." It can only say: "Your per-capita discretionary spend exceeds the median of the regions subsidizing you — bring it within band. You choose what." Solvency conditionality without policy dictation.

### 3.3 The Three Guardrails

1. **Joint governance.** Each fund's board is contributors and recipients jointly (weighted so neither dominates), confirmed by the Territorial Chamber (Regional Boards, 11/20). Disputes resolved through the ad-hoc arbitration process (Chamber Act §6.6).
2. **Taper, don't cliff.** Support phases out as a recipient improves its own revenue — the recipient keeps a defined share of the upside. No 100% clawback, no poverty trap.
3. **Donor cap & recipient floor.** No contributor may be drawn beyond a defined percentage of its surplus; no municipality or region may fall below a basic-services floor regardless of finances.

### 3.4 Contributor Standing & Appeals (Symmetric Justice)

The contributor has standing too, so the fund is legitimate to both sides. The recipient can appeal conditions; the contributor can appeal draws — both on objective grounds only.

**A net contributor may:**
- **Challenge eligibility / gaming:** petition that a recipient is underreporting revenue, misclassifying spending, or running chronic deficits that should have escalated. A challenge **triggers a Special Auditor review** (Sovereign Functions Act §3).
- **Challenge the band calculation:** contest that the median was computed incorrectly or that its contribution exceeds the donor cap (§3.3).
- **Trigger chronic-recipient review:** force escalation to the supervised-recovery tier.

**A contributor may NOT:**
- **Dictate the recipient's line items.** A contributor may challenge *whether* a recipient qualifies and *whether* it is within band — **never what it cuts.** The fund sets the envelope; the recipient keeps sovereignty over priorities; no donor may override that.

**Mechanism:**
1. **First instance:** the contributor petitions the jointly-governed fund board.
2. **Investigation:** an eligibility/gaming challenge compels a Special Auditor review (Sovereign Functions Act §3).
3. **Appeal:** either side escalates a disputed ruling to **ad-hoc arbitration** (Chamber Act §6.6) — each side picks one arbiter from the relevant sector, the two pick a chair, the tribunal decides and dissolves. The decision is binding.
4. **Systemic:** disputes implicating many regions or the formula itself go to the **Territorial Chamber** for resolution (15/20), with ad-hoc arbitration if the chamber deadlocks.

**Transparency right.** Every contributor may see, annually, exactly what it paid in, what each recipient drew, and the band calculation that justified each draw — at the regional/municipal-government level (institutional transparency; citizen-level data stays pseudonymized). The black-box opacity that breeds grievance in real equalization systems is prohibited.

### 3.5 Anti-Gaming

- Contributions and draws are tracked (pseudonymized fiscal aggregates) via the CED; municipal/regional identity and governance via the PPK-linked bodies defined in the Chamber Act.
- The Special Auditors (Sovereign Functions Act §3) may investigate any municipality or region suspected of underreporting revenue, overstating cost-of-service, or offering below-cost deals — on their own initiative.
- Disputes over conditionality or eligibility are resolved through ad-hoc arbitration (Chamber Act §6.6); systemic multi-region crises go to the Territorial Chamber (15/20), with the SWF as lender of last resort only on cross-regional emergency certification (5/20 Regional Boards + Sovereign Council 5/7).

### 3.6 Arbitration Decision Registry

All ad-hoc arbitration decisions arising from equalization disputes, conditionality appeals, contributor-standing challenges, and inter-municipal or inter-regional deadlocks under this act are **published in the national arbitration registry** — a searchable, public archive maintained by the national legal registry (the same body that catalogues the Settled Codex, Chamber Act §6.5).

- Decisions are published in full (anonymized where privacy floors require), archived permanently, and accessible to future tribunals, the chambers, and the public.
- Decisions are **persuasive, not binding** on future tribunals. A future tribunal may consider past decisions for guidance but is not bound by them — each dispute is decided on its own merits. This prevents a shadow legal code from accumulating through ad-hoc rulings while ensuring that no decision is lost.
- The **chambers review** the accumulated arbitration log as part of the mandatory review calendar (Chamber Act §6.2). Where the chambers identify a recurring dispute pattern — the same type of deadlock arising repeatedly — they may **codify the principle into statute through ordinary lawmaking** (Chamber Act §6.1), preempting future arbitration on that question. The log feeds the legislature; it does not replace it.
- The registry is the system's institutional memory for dispute resolution: arbitrators resolve and dissolve, but their reasoning is preserved, available for both legislative learning and future tribunals' reference.

### 3.7 Capital Lending — the Revolving Function

**The MMF and RMF are not just equalization pools.** They are mutual funds, and mutual funds can lend. A municipality or rural community that needs a capital asset — a school building, a water-treatment plant, a bridge, a clinic — may draw from its tier's fund as a **revolving loan**, repaid over time, with the corpus surviving intact.

**Three functions, not one:**

| Function | Nature | Repayment |
|---|---|---|
| **Equalization** (§3.1–3.2) | Ongoing transfers from richer to poorer units | Not repaid — mutual insurance |
| **Emergency relief** | Catastrophic one-time draws (flood, mine collapse) | Repaid if possible; forgiven if not |
| **Capital lending** | Pre-approved revolving loans for infrastructure | **Repaid in full** over 10–20 years at reference + 0.5% (~4%) |

**How capital lending works:**

1. **Application in advance.** The community identifies a need (a school, a bridge, a clinic) and applies to the MMF or RMF board with a project description, cost estimate, and repayment schedule.
2. **Coordination.** The fund board evaluates all pending applications by **need** (distance to nearest facility, population served), **capacity** (the community's revenue and ability to repay), and **phasing** (oversubscribed years are prioritised by need and phased across years). This coordination is the function a bilateral credit-union loan cannot provide.
3. **Approval.** Fund board approves (simple majority) with a repayment schedule. The rate is **reference + 0.5%** (~4%) — the subsidised service-sector rate, because community infrastructure is a public good.
4. **Repayment.** The community repays over 10–20 years on top of its regular MMF/RMF contributions. Repayments flow back into the fund — **the corpus survives**, same principle as the SWF (Monetary §2.1).
5. **Default.** If a community cannot repay, the draw escalates to the conditionality ladder (§3.2). The fund does not forgive capital draws without a recovery plan, but the community is never bankrupted: the schedule adjusts, extends, or converts to a partial equalization draw. The fund absorbs the loss across all contributors.

**This is not debt.** A draw from a mutual fund the community contributes to is a **revolving claim on a pool it owns a share of** — same structure as an SWF loan, a credit-union draw on the RLP, or an FCL draw on the CCIF. The "no debt at any level" bar (§1.3) prohibits bond issuance, sovereign paper, and external borrowing. It does not prohibit a member drawing from its own mutual fund.

> **The school example.** A rural co-op council in Region 5 (Heartland Plains) — population 2,000, three farming co-ops — needs a school building. Nearest school: 40 km. Cost: $2M.
>
> The council applies to the MMF. The fund evaluates: need is high (40 km); capacity is adequate (three stable co-ops); 12 competing applications; council ranks 4th by distance. Approved.
>
> Draw: $2M at ~4%. Repayment: ~$180,000/year over 15 years. Three co-ops split it: ~$60,000 each — about $120/member/year. The school is built *now*. The fund is repaid *over time*. No bond was issued. No bank was approached. A mutual fund lent to its own member, and the member repays from revenue it was already generating. **That is the credit-union model applied to community infrastructure.**

## Section 4: Governance & Administration

- **Municipalities** set their own budgets within the bounded base; they may not run debt; they administer their own revenue and pay into the MMF. Municipal police are commanded by municipal councils, with Regional Board oversight (Chamber Act §3.3).
- **Regional Boards** (Chamber Act §3 — 20 boards, 11 members each: 7 credit-union-federated + 4 wildcard) oversee the municipalities in their region, administer regional revenue, and pay into the RMF — exercising their Territorial-Chamber / state-legislature function. They appoint regional prosecutors (Chamber Act §3.5) and coordinate emergency mutual aid (Chamber Act §3.6).
- **MMF / RMF boards** (jointly governed, §3.3) administer contributions, draws, and conditionality; confirmed by the Territorial Chamber (Regional Boards, 11/20); disputes resolved through ad-hoc arbitration (Chamber Act §6.6), with decisions logged in the arbitration registry (§3.6).
- **Special Auditors** (Sovereign Functions Act §3) enforce against gaming, below-cost deals, inflated assessments, exclusionary-zoning violations, and disguised development fees — independently and on their own initiative.
- **National SWF** (Sovereign Functions Act §5) is lender of last resort only on cross-regional emergency certification (5/20 Regional Boards + Sovereign Council 5/7) — never a routine backstop.

| Contributor standing | May challenge eligibility/gaming/band/cap — never line items; ad-hoc arbitration; annual transparency |
| Fund governance | Joint contributor/recipient; Territorial Chamber confirmation |
| Dispute resolution | Ad-hoc arbitration (Chamber §6.6); decisions logged, persuasive not binding; chambers review log and may codify patterns |
| Incentive preservation | Taper, donor cap, recipient floor |

## Design Philosophy (Informative)

This act fixes the quiet bankruptcy of the modern municipality — and it does so with principles already woven through the rest of the system.

- **You cannot tax the home.** The one tax forbidden is the one that turns an owner back into a tenant. The home is sovereign ground — constitutionally entrenched, beyond the reach of any majority, emergency, or treaty.

- **But you pay for what you use.** The home is never taxed, but the household pays for the water, waste, and roads it actually uses. Schools come off the city's books entirely, funded by the voucher — with the education guarantee ensuring every child's access regardless of regional solvency. A city may not zone out housing to protect its budget. A city grows richer by welcoming families — and the retiree who owns her home outright cannot be assessed out of it.

- **You pay what you cost.** Sprawl is not banned; it pays its own way. End the subsidy, and the market quietly chooses efficiency.

- **No debt, but never alone.** A municipality cannot borrow its way into a crisis — but is never left to drown. Cities insure cities, regions insure regions.

- **Help without dictation.** The fund enforces only solvency — you may not spend, on your subsidizers' restraint, what they will not spend on themselves. What you cut is always yours to choose.

- **Deadlocks are resolved, not institutionalized.** When two sides are stuck on an equalization question, an ad-hoc tribunal assembles, decides, and goes home. The decision is published, preserved, and available for legislative review — but it binds no future tribunal and replaces no legislative action. The log feeds the chambers; it does not govern.

- **Someone is always watching the books.** The Special Auditors can open any municipality's or region's accounts on their own initiative. The cost-of-service rules, the fee caps, the anti-exclusionary guardrail, and the fund conditions are real, not aspirational.

- **Every clinic and every school is owed.** The healthcare and education guarantees bind the equalization funds: where a region's contributions fall short, the RMF and the commons waterfall must close the gap. A child's education and a citizen's doctor are constitutional rights, not emergent outcomes of regional wealth.

No taxed homes, no subsidized sprawl, no subnational debt, no predatory conditions, no books that cannot be audited, and no region where a child's school or a citizen's clinic is contingent on the local fisc. That is how the cities and regions of New Crossroads pay their way.
