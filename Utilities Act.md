> **Cross-reference**: This act is companion legislation to the *Crossroads Energy Act of 2025 (Draft 3.0)*, *Crossroads Monetary Reform & Economic Stabilization Act of 2025 (Draft 8.0)*, *Crossroads Credit Union Act of 2025 (Draft 4.0)*, *Crossroads Federated Cooperatives Limited (FCL) Structure Act of 2025 (Draft 3.0)*, and *Crossroads Infrastructure Act of 2025 (Draft 1.0)*. All references to grid hardening, buried transmission lines, EMP-shielded substations, transformer reserves, and antifragile energy hubs are governed by the Energy Act. The asset/operator separation model follows the same pattern as the Infrastructure Act's cooperative rail system (state owns tracks, co-ops/corporations run trains).

---

## Purpose

Establishes a **competitive utilities market** built on **state-owned transmission and distribution infrastructure**, following the successful asset/operator separation model of New Crossroads' cooperative rail system. The state owns and maintains the physical grid assets (poles, wires, substations, transformers). Licensed utilities — primarily co-ops, but also corporations — compete to generate electricity and sell it to citizens and businesses, paying **transmission access fees** (like rail track access fees or freeway tolls) for use of state infrastructure.

This model prevents natural monopoly capture, ensures CME hardening is centralized and effective, generates steady revenue for the Sovereign Wealth Fund (SWF), and allows co-ops to enter the electricity market without billions in capital investment.

---

## Section 0: Interdependencies

The **Utilities Act** integrates with the **Energy Act**, **Infrastructure Act**, **Monetary Act**, **Credit Union Act**, and **FCL Act**:

- **Energy Act**: Provides the physical grid assets — 30,000 km buried high-voltage transmission lines, 150,000 km buried distribution lines, 750 EMP-shielded substations, and a strategic transformer reserve (5,000+ by 2075) — as the infrastructure base for competitive utilities. Antifragile energy hubs (2 international, 8 regional, 36 GW SMRs) serve as anchor loads and emergency islanding points.
- **Infrastructure Act**: The asset/operator separation model (state owns rail tracks, co-ops/corps run trains) is directly replicated (state owns power lines, utilities generate/sell power). Transmission access fees follow the same logic as freeway tolls (155% cost recovery over asset life).
- **Monetary Act**: Transmission access fees are paid in BWC, flow to SWF, and are subject to standard BWC transaction fees. DAI does not apply (fees are not loans). Co-op utilities pay the 5% co-op profit tax on generation profits.
- **Credit Union Act**: Utility co-ops access loans ($50,000–$5M, 5–6% nominal, DAI-adjusted) for generation plants (solar, wind, geothermal, small hydro, WTE, SMRs). Micro-loans ($500) for community solar projects.
- **FCL Act**: Generation utilities operate as Tier 2 or Tier 3 FCLs. Heavy Industry Clause applies to nuclear (SMR) and large geothermal plants. CCIF (voluntary) may fund utility co-op expansion.

## Section 1: Core Principles

### 1.1 Asset/Operator Separation

New Crossroads' utilities sector operates on the same principle as its rail system:

| Sector | Asset Owner | Operator | Access Fee |
|--------|-------------|----------|------------|
| Rail | State (70%) + Co-op (30%) | Train companies | No tolls (cooperative maintains) |
| Freeways | State | Any vehicle | Toll ($0.10/km, 155% cost recovery) |
| **Electricity Transmission** | **State (100%)** | **NEB maintains; utilities pay access fees** | **$/MWh transmitted** |
| **Electricity Distribution** | **State (100%)** | **NEB maintains; utilities pay access fees** | **$/MWh delivered** |
| **Electricity Generation** | **Co-op or Corporate** | **Utility (generator)** | **No fee (generation is competitive)** |
| **Electricity Retail** | **Co-op or Corporate** | **Utility (retailer)** | **No fee (retail is competitive)** |

**Rationale**: The state is good at building and hardening long-lived infrastructure (buried lines, substations, transformers) but bad at running customer-facing utilities efficiently. Co-ops and corporations are good at competing for customers, innovating, and controlling costs. Separation gives each sector what it does best.

### 1.2 What the State Owns (per Energy Act)

| Asset | 2025 Quantity | 2075 Quantity | Owner | Maintainer |
|-------|---------------|---------------|-------|------------|
| High-voltage transmission lines (>100kV) | 20,000 km | 30,000 km | State (NEB) | NEB |
| Medium-voltage distribution lines (10–100kV) | 100,000 km | 150,000 km | State (Regional Boards) | NEB |
| EMP-shielded substations | 500 | 750 | State (NEB) | NEB |
| Strategic transformer reserve | 1,000 | 5,000+ | State (NEB) | NEB |
| Antifragile energy hub SMRs | 18 GW | 36 GW | State (operated by NEB) | NEB |

These assets are funded by the SWF per the Energy Act ($141B for grid hardening, $200B for hubs) and maintained by the National Energy Board (NEB).

### 1.3 What Utilities Own and Operate

| Asset | Owner | License required | Notes |
|-------|-------|------------------|-------|
| Generation plants (SMRs, geothermal, solar, wind, hydro, WTE) | Co-op or Corporate | Yes (NEB) | Competitive market |
| Battery storage facilities | Co-op or Corporate | Yes (NEB) | Competitive market |
| Customer meters and billing systems | Co-op or Corporate | Yes (NEB) | Competitive market |
| Retail brand and customer contracts | Co-op or Corporate | Yes (NEB) | Competitive market |

**Co-op Preference**: Generation licenses are issued preferentially to co-ops (65% of new capacity reserved for co-ops). Corporations may apply for the remaining 35%. This maintains the 65% co-op GDP target from the Monetary Act.

## Section 2: Transmission Access Fees

### 2.1 Fee Structure (Like Rail Track Access or Freeway Tolls)

Utilities pay the NEB for access to state-owned transmission and distribution lines. Fees are set to recover 100% of grid hardening and maintenance costs plus 55% (155% total recovery over asset life), matching the freeway toll model.

| Voltage Level | Fee per MWh | Annual volume (2025) | Annual revenue (2025) | Annual revenue (2075) |
|---------------|-------------|----------------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| High-voltage (>100kV) | $5/MWh | 400 TWh | $2.0B | $8.2B |
| Medium-voltage (10–100kV) | $10/MWh | 500 TWh | $5.0B | $16.3B |
| Low-voltage (<10kV) | $15/MWh | 533 TWh | $8.0B | $24.5B |
| **Total** | | **1,433 TWh** | **$15.0B** | **$49.0B** |

*(Volume calculations: 400 TWh grid in 2025, 1,633 TWh by 2075, allocated across voltage levels based on typical grid topology.)*

### 2.2 Fee Adjustment Mechanism

- **Biennial review** by NEB (6/11 vote) with Central Council approval (6/11)
- **Inflation adjustment** tied to DLB (Daily Living Basket) annually
- **CME surcharge**: Additional $2/MWh during CME recovery (max 90 days) to fund transformer deployment
- **Co-op discount**: Co-op utilities pay 20% less than corporate utilities (co-op rate = 80% of corporate rate)

### 2.3 Revenue Allocation

| Allocation | 2025 ($B) | 2075 ($B) | Notes |
|------------|-----------|-----------|-------|
| Grid maintenance and hardening (per Energy Act) | 7.5 | 24.5 | 50% of revenue |
| SWF (per Monetary Act 8.0) | 4.5 | 14.7 | 30% of revenue |
| Residential electricity bill credit (direct to citizens) | 3.0 | 9.8 | 20% of revenue |
| **Total** | **15.0** | **49.0** | |

The residential credit is distributed equally to all citizens (cash and BWC, 50/50 split, no surveillance, matching SWF dividend mechanics).

## Section 3: Utility Licensing and Market Access

### 3.1 License Categories

| License Type | Duration | Fee | Requirements | Issued by |
|--------------|----------|-----|--------------|-----------|
| Generation license | 20 years | $10,000/year | Technical competence, financial stability, co-op preference | NEB |
| Retail license | 10 years | $5,000/year | Customer service standards, billing systems, co-op preference | Regional Board |
| Combined (gen + retail) | 20 years | $12,000/year | Both requirements | NEB + Regional Board |

### 3.2 Co-op Preference in Licensing

- **65% of new generation capacity** (by MW) reserved for co-ops
- **65% of retail customer accounts** (by region) reserved for co-ops
- Corporations may apply for remaining 35%
- If co-op applications do not fill the 65% reservation, capacity reverts to corporations after 90 days

### 3.3 License Revocation

- Failure to maintain service standards (outages >4 hours/year per customer average)
- Fraud or billing violations (per CWD audit)
- Bankruptcy (license transfers to another utility automatically)

### 3.4 Open Access for Small Generators (Net Metering)

| Generator size | Access | Fee | Notes |
|---------------|--------|-----|-------|
| <10 kW (residential solar) | Free | $0 | Net metering, no transmission fee |
| 10–100 kW (small business solar) | Reduced | 50% of standard fee | Simplified application |
| 100 kW–1 MW (community solar) | Standard | 100% of standard fee | Co-op preferred |
| >1 MW (utility-scale) | Standard | 100% of standard fee | Full license required |

This mirrors the Informal Economy Guarantee's philosophy: small-scale activity is friction-free and private; large-scale activity faces full scrutiny.

## Section 5: Retail Market

### 5.1 Consumer Choice

Every citizen and business may choose their retail electricity provider from any licensed utility operating in their region.

| Customer type | Choice | Default provider if no choice made |
|---------------|--------|-------------------------------------|
| Residential | Any licensed retail utility | Regional co-op utility |
| Small business | Any licensed retail utility | Regional co-op utility |
| Industrial | Direct access (PPA) or utility | Competitive bidding |

### 5.2 Retail Price Structure

| Component | Paid to | Notes |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| Generation cost | Utility (generator) | Competitive market |
| Transmission access fee | NEB (state) | $/MWh (Section 2) |
| Retail margin | Utility (retailer) | Competitive market, capped at 10% for residential |

### 5.3 Consumer Protections

- **Bill transparency**: Must show generation cost, transmission fee, retail margin separately
- **No disconnection for non-payment during CME (30 days)**
- **Low-income assistance**: Automatic enrollment for citizens earning <$20,000/year (per Monetary Act personal income tax threshold)
- **Informal sector access**: Cash payments accepted at credit union kiosks (no fee, no surveillance, per Informal Economy Guarantee)

### 5.4 Switching

- Customers may switch providers **once per month** at no cost
- Switching processed within 24 hours (CLS-managed customer database via CWD)
- **Asymmetric scrutiny**: Residential switching data is anonymized and aggregated; industrial switching is fully tracked

### 6.3 Example: Rural Electric Co-op Startup

A Tier 2 FCL in Region 5 (Heartland Plains) wants to build a 10 MW solar farm and serve 2,000 residential customers.

| Step | Action | Cost | Financing |
|------|--------|------|-----------|
| 1 | Form utility co-op (under FCL Act) | $1,000 | Member base shares |
| 2 | Obtain generation license from NEB | $10,000/year | Operating budget |
| 3 | Obtain retail license from Regional Board | $5,000/year | Operating budget |

The co-op pays 80% of the standard transmission access fee ($8/MWh instead of $10/MWh), has priority during shortages, and qualifies for the 0.5% DAI floor on its community solar micro-loans for member households.

Per Energy Act Section 2, the NEB (11 members) now also:

## Design Philosophy (Informative)

The Utilities Act completes the asset/operator separation pattern that began with the Infrastructure Act's rail system.

**Why state-owned infrastructure?**
- Natural monopolies (transmission lines, substations) are not competitive markets
- The state can harden the grid against CMEs once, centrally, efficiently
- Buried lines, EMP shields, and transformer reserves are strategic national assets
- Private utilities would under-invest in hardening (they don't capture the full social benefit)

**Why competitive generation and retail?**
- Co-ops and corporations compete on cost, service, and innovation
- Citizens choose their provider — if a co-op is bad, they switch
- No state monopoly on customer service (which is usually terrible)
- Co-ops can start small (community solar) and grow to utility-scale

**Why transmission access fees?**
- Like rail track access or freeway tolls, users pay for what they use
- Revenue funds maintenance (50%), SWF (30%), and direct citizen credits (20%)
- 155% cost recovery matches the freeway model
- Co-op discount (20%) gives co-ops a structural advantage without a subsidy

**Why co-op preference?**
- 65% of generation licenses reserved for co-ops (matches Monetary Act 65% co-op GDP target)
- Co-ops pay 20% less in transmission fees
- Co-ops get priority grid access during shortages (after antifragile hubs)
- This is not a subsidy — it's a structural design choice favoring distributed, member-owned generation

**Why informal sector access?**
- Cash payments accepted at credit union kiosks (no fee, no surveillance)
- Low-income assistance automatic (no application, no stigma)
- Community solar micro-loans at 0.5% DAI floor (self-attested income)
- The grid serves everyone, regardless of how they earn or spend

The Utilities Act is not privatization. It is not nationalization. It is **asset-state, operations-market** — the same pattern that makes New Crossroads' rail system the envy of the continent. The state owns the poles and wires. Co-ops and corporations compete to keep the lights on. And citizens choose who gets their business.

That is the Utilities Act.
