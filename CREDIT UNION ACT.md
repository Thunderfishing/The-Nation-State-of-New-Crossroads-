## The Inheritance

**What the revolutionaries walk into on Day 1:**

A socialist island nation of 112 million people, near Point Nemo, with:

- **Sovereign debt at ~120% of GDP** — recently defaulted. The old currency is collapsing. Foreign creditors are furious but distant.
- **State ownership of everything** — every mine, every factory, every farm, every power plant, every apartment block, every port, every rail line. All of it in government hands, most of it poorly maintained, some of it genuinely good (the nuclear plants, the mines, the rail backbone).
- **A massive informal economy** — perhaps 25–30% of real economic activity operating as a black market. The state has spent decades trying to suppress it and failing. These are the people who actually keep the country fed and functioning.
- **A disarmed, surveilled, censored population** — state media, no private firearms, informant networks in the markets, housing assigned by committee. The population trusts nobody with power, and they're right not to.
- **Deep resource endowments** — the Frostpeak's uranium, coal, gold, silver, copper, and industrial metals. The Heartland's agricultural capacity. The Ember Range's geothermal. ~400 TWh of nuclear and hydro generation capacity that still works.
- **Strategic isolation** — no land neighbours, nearest continent thousands of kilometres away. The default will not trigger a military invasion. It will trigger a trade-credit squeeze.
- **Legacy infrastructure that mostly works** — 56 GW of nuclear (aging but operational), a rail backbone, ports, a road network, a power grid, submarine cables to foreign continents. None of it CME-hardened, none of it optimised, but none of it rubble. This is not a war zone — it's a bureaucratic collapse.

**What they don't have:** foreign reserves, international credit, a working currency, a tax collection apparatus, a legal framework for private ownership, or any reason for the population to trust them.

## The Man and the Plan

**Durant B. Cooper** led the revolution with a coalition of military defectors, informal-economy leaders, mining engineers, and constitutionalist intellectuals who had been planning the new order for years — probably decades — in exile or underground. The seventeen founding acts are ready before the revolution happens. The design philosophy — *discretion corrupts; replace it with published rules* — exists before the revolution, not after it.

Cooper is not a democrat. He is a constitutionalist who intends to build a democracy and then leave. The distinction matters: a democrat would hold elections immediately and hope the system survives the result. Cooper holds power long enough to make the system unkillable, then walks away.

**The Cooper Doctrine (stated on Day 1):**

> *"We govern for five years because the institutions need time to root. We share power for five more because the chambers need time to learn. We leave one at a time so the stagger works. I leave last because someone has to turn the lights off. And for twenty years I hold a veto — not because I don't trust the system, but because the system hasn't been tested yet. After twenty years, it will have been. Then the veto dies, and nobody gets another one."*

## The Transition Architecture

### The Transitional Council (Years 0–10)

Cooper and six revolutionary colleagues — **seven total** — govern as the **Transitional Council**, structured like an NEB-type independent board: a chairman (Cooper) and six members with defined sector responsibilities. The Council is not a junta — it operates under the founding acts from Day 1, it publishes its decisions, and the Special Auditors have jurisdiction over it from the moment they're appointed.

| Seat | Responsibility | Occupant profile |
|---|---|---|
| **Chairman** | Overall authority; commander-in-chief; veto power | **Durant B. Cooper** |
| Member 1 | Economic transition (co-op conversion, credit unions, CLS) | Economist / informal-economy leader |
| Member 2 | Military and intelligence | Military defector / senior officer |
| Member 3 | Energy and infrastructure | Mining engineer / nuclear specialist |
| Member 4 | Justice and civil rights | Constitutionalist / legal scholar |
| Member 5 | Education and healthcare | Educator / medical professional |
| Member 6 | Foreign affairs and trade | Diplomat / trade negotiator |

**The Council's authority is extraordinary but bounded:**

- It governs by decree during Years 0–5, but decrees must be consistent with the founding acts. A decree that contradicts an act is void — the judiciary can strike it from the day the courts are seated.
- The Special Auditors may investigate any Council member from Month 3 onward.
- The Council publishes all decisions. No secret decrees, no classified domestic policy.
- The Council may not amend the founding acts — only the chambers can, once seated, at the prescribed bars.

### The Cooper Sunset Veto

**Durant B. Cooper holds absolute veto power over any legislation, decree, regulation, or chamber resolution.** The veto is personal, non-delegable, and non-transferable. No Council member other than Cooper holds it.

**The veto sunsets constitutionally.** It expires on the **later of:**
- **20 years after the revolution** (Year 20, ~2045), or
- **The date Cooper vacates the Transitional Council or the Sovereign Council** (whichever comes last)

If Cooper leaves office at Year 12 (see below), the veto continues until Year 20 — Cooper as a private citizen with constitutional veto authority over the chambers. If Cooper remains in office past Year 20 (impossible under the transition schedule, but the clause is written for completeness), the veto expires on his departure.

**After the sunset date, the veto power is void.** It may not be revived, extended, renewed, or granted to any other person. The entrenching provision is set at the **highest amendment bar** (Chamber Act §10.5 — supermajorities of every chamber plus a 75% national referendum). Cooper himself cannot extend it unilaterally.

**The veto's purpose, stated honestly:** the founding acts are untested. A hostile chamber majority in Year 8 could hollow out the co-op advantage, gut the informal-economy guarantee, or raid the SWF — all through ordinary lawmaking. The veto exists to give the system 20 years of constitutional protection while the institutions prove themselves. It is not a gift to Cooper; it is a shield for the architecture. It dies because shields should not outlive the threat they were built for.

## Year 0: The Revolution (2025)

### Week 1 — The Constitutional Moment

**Day 1 announcements, in order of psychological impact:**

1. **The right to bear arms, including concealed carry.** The old regime disarmed you. We just armed you. This is the single loudest signal that the new government is not the old one. *(Exceptions: courthouses, airports, government buildings — named and narrow.)*
2. **Near-absolute press freedom and free speech.** The old regime ran every newspaper and every broadcast. As of now, anyone can publish anything. Local AM radio stations begin forming immediately.
3. **Your home is yours — untaxable, at every level, forever.** Entrenched as Floor 2. The old regime assigned housing and could reassign it. That is over.
4. **Your business is legal.** Every black-market operation in the country is legitimate as of this morning. No registration required below $100,000. Cash commerce constitutionally protected. Nobody is coming to inspect, audit, or prosecute.
5. **The sovereign default is confirmed and the old currency is retired.** The debt belongs to the regime that incurred it. The new state does not recognise it as its own. **The new state will never borrow, at any level, and has constitutionally barred itself from doing so.**

**What the population experiences on Day 1:** they wake up armed, free, in a home nobody can take or tax, with a business nobody can shut down, under a government that just banned itself from borrowing. Every one of those is the *exact opposite* of the old regime. The message is not subtle.

### Month 1 — The BWC and the Tax

**The BWC launches on conventional paper stock.** Emergency issue — secure enough, plain, functional. Distributed through the post office, the old state banks (temporarily repurposed), and the 5,000 credit union charters that begin forming immediately. The exchange rate to the old currency is set by a one-time conversion — generous to small holders, haircut on large ones.

**The tax system goes live — and it is shockingly simple:**

- **6% VAT on consumption. The Daily Living Basket is zero-rated.** Collected at point of sale. No filing, no returns, no tax office visits.
- **No income tax below $100,000.** The first $100,000 of every citizen's personal income is exempt. Marginal rates on the excess: 20% / 30% / 40%. For the vast majority of the population — and the entirety of the informal economy — there is no direct tax relationship with the state whatsoever.
- **No residential property tax. No business registration tax. No payroll tax.**
- **Tariffs at the border** — the ports collect these; they don't require domestic compliance infrastructure.

The tax system asks almost nothing of the population and raises ~$495B because it taxes *transactions* not *people*. The informal economy participates automatically through the VAT and receives more back through the dividend than it pays.

### Months 1–6 — The Great Transfer

**State-owned enterprises begin converting to co-ops.** This is the core economic event of the revolution and it takes months, not days.

- Every state enterprise is chartered as an FCL. The workers become members — one share, one vote. Management is elected from within or recruited.
- The enterprise's assets are valued at **post-collapse prices** — fire-sale, not book value.
- Workers receive their membership interest for **labour equity** — years of service under the old regime convert to founding capital.
- The **5,000 credit unions** begin forming simultaneously (Credit Union Act). Each takes the deposits of the old state savings system and recapitalises them as member-owned institutions. Every citizen receives 1 free base share at their CU — membership, voting rights, and the start of patient capital accumulation.
- **The CLS corps (~40,000 founding agents)** begin standing up, embedded in the credit unions, processing co-op formations, loan applications, the first $50 BWC age-20 wallets, and the student-venture liaison programme (Credit Union Act §5.3).

**What is NOT privatised:** the grid, the rail and freeway network, the buried fiber backbone, the AM emergency broadcast system, the parkweb, the ports, the submarine cables. These remain **state-owned sovereign infrastructure** — operated by co-op concessions under sector-board oversight (Utilities Act §1.4).

### Months 3–6 — The Boards Stand Up

**The seven sector boards** are initially **appointed by the Transitional Council with an 18-month sunset** — they must be re-confirmed by the elected chambers within 18 months or they dissolve.

| Board | Stands up | First task |
|---|---|---|
| NEB (Energy) | Month 2 | Assess the legacy nuclear fleet; begin grid-hardening planning; tender first co-op maintenance concessions |
| NInfraB (Infrastructure) | Month 2 | Inventory the rail and road network; set founding access-fee rates at 155% of maintenance |
| NCB (Communications) | Month 3 | Declare the fiber backbone and submarine cables state-owned; assess CME vulnerability |
| NIB (Industry) | Month 3 | Begin processing co-op conversions for the industrial sector; launch the foreign-factory partnership programme (Industry Act §2.5) |
| NHA (Housing) | Month 4 | Launch the Durahome programme; establish the forced-savings accounts |
| NHB (Healthcare) | Month 4 | Convert the old state hospitals to the co-op premium model; stand up the bottom-tier subsidy |
| NLC (Education) | Month 5 | Launch the voucher system for the next school year; begin mandatory-service programme (Education Act §4) |

### Month 6 — The SWF Is Seeded

**The Sovereign Wealth Fund receives its ~$230B seed.** Sources:

- **Freed debt service.** The old regime was paying ~$300–400B/year servicing its debt. That line is now zero. Even a fraction of one year's freed service exceeds the seed.
- **Asset-sale proceeds.** The co-op conversions are not giveaways — labour equity covers the workers' share, but the residual enterprise value flows to the state as founding capital.
- **Foreign reserves.** Whatever the old central bank held — gold, foreign currency, SDRs — transfers to the SWF.
- **First resource royalties.** The Frostpeak mines never stopped producing. At 12% on even half the founding resource base, royalties are flowing from Month 1.

The SWF is seeded once, then grows from contributions (~$143B/year). No second injection. The seed is the revolution's one-time fiscal event; everything after is the system running on its own.

## Years 1–2: The System Boots (2026–2027)

**The Transitional Council governs directly.** Cooper chairs weekly meetings, sector responsibilities are divided, and the seven members function as the executive while the constitutional institutions take shape beneath them.

**Year 1 milestones:**
- BWC circulating. Paper stock, functional.
- VAT collecting. The ~$495B revenue base is building — it won't hit the full annual figure until the formal economy stabilises, but the informal economy's VAT is flowing from Day 1.
- ~80% of state enterprises converted to co-ops. Complex conversions (mining, heavy industry) take longer.
- Credit unions operational in all 20 regions. Deposits growing. Base shares accumulating.
- SWF accumulating: ~$230B seed + ~$143B first-year contributions = ~$373B by year-end.
- The seven sector boards functioning under Transitional Council appointment, awaiting chamber confirmation.

**Year 2 milestones:**
- **Chambers begin forming.** The Territorial Chamber (20 Regional Boards) elects first — credit-union-federated seats through the tiered cross-seconding process (Chamber Act §3.1), wildcard seats through citizen petition and universal vote. The two Productive Chambers follow as co-ops and corporations register and the sector-proportional seats fill through cross-regional approval voting (Chamber Act §4.1).
- **The Exchange opens.** Co-op Capital Board, Special-Share Board, Metals ETF Board, and the new Investment Fund Board all begin trading. GIC Ladder Fund ETFs launch — the system's flight-to-safety instrument.
- **The metals streaming pipeline goes live.** First contracts with Frostpeak mining co-ops signed. Metal flows to the ETF vault.
- **The Special Auditors complete their first investigations.** The new system's enforcement arm has teeth.
- **First polymer notes (copper flecks) enter limited circulation.**
- **Chambers confirm all seven sector boards** — the convention appointments become constitutional. The Transitional Council's authority begins to overlap with the chambers' legislative power.
- **Trade-credit squeeze real but survivable.** The nuclear fleet generates the nation's own electricity. The Heartland feeds the nation. The Frostpeak exports commodities counterparties want badly enough to trade for. Nobody is hungry, nobody is cold, the lights are on.
- **SWF at year-end:** ~$516B. Approaching the ~$720B cap.

## Years 3–5: The Transition Begins (2028–2030)

### Year 3 — The Dividend Arrives

**The SWF crosses the 12% cap around mid-year.** The §2.3 stabilisers fire automatically. The first dividend payments go out: **~$1,400 per citizen per year**, paid monthly or quarterly, in cash, no filing required. For a household of four: ~$5,600/year.

**This is the moment the revolution becomes irreversible.** Not because the dividend is large, but because 112 million people just received money *from* a government for the first time in their lives. The old regime took. This one gave. And it did it without asking for a form.

The informal economy — 25–30% of the working population — receives the dividend and pays less back in VAT than it gets. Under 6% VAT + the ~$1,400 citizen dividend, the informal sector nets positive: it pays ~$30–40B of VAT (DLB zero-rated) and receives ~$35B of dividend. **These are the revolution's most important constituents**, and the system was designed to reach them first.

**Other Year 3 milestones:**
- Full polymer currency circulation. Silver and gold denominations join copper.
- Trade-credit squeeze easing. Two years of reliable commodity exports have rebuilt minimal trade reputation.
- Housing Act Durahome programme reaching scale (~50,000 homes/year).
- Infrastructure Phase 1 underway. Grid hardening, rail expansion, first hub SMR construction.

### Year 4 — Steady State

**The fiscal architecture runs unsupervised:**

| Component | Status |
|---|---|
| Revenue (~$495B) | Collecting — VAT 6%, income, profit taxes, tariffs, resources, property, excise, Parks Index |
| Spending (~$424.5B) | Disbursing — vouchers, subsidies, infrastructure, military, parks, R&D, four functions, mandatory-service stipends |
| SWF (~$720B+, capped) | Lending and backstopping — not spending |
| Citizen dividend (~$157B/yr) | Paying — ~$1,400/citizen, cash, universal |
| Commons (~$48B) | Flowing — healthcare $19B, education $10B, charity $19B |
| Healthcare (~$448B) | Self-funding on premiums + commons + subsidy |
| Credit-union network | Self-funding on NIM (~3%) |

**Nobody is running this.** The sovereign operates at 3.3% of GDP. The chambers oversee. The boards regulate their sectors. The Special Auditors investigate. The formulas publish rates. The stabilisers fire or don't. There is no economic tsar.

### Year 5 — The Power Transfer Begins

**Cooper announces the transition schedule.** The Transitional Council will dissolve itself into the Sovereign Council over the next seven years. One member resigns per year, replaced through the constitutional process (Territorial Chamber nominates, Productive Chambers confirm). Cooper goes last.

**The Transitional Council formally cedes legislative authority to the chambers.** From Year 5 onward, the Council governs only through the Sovereign Council's §6.3 powers (the closed list — military command, treaty negotiation, auditor/judicial nominations, Tier-2 emergency key). All domestic legislative authority belongs to the chambers.

**What changes for citizens:** nothing visible. The system was already running on the acts, not on the Council. The transition is institutional, not experiential.

## Years 6–12: The Staggered Handover (2031–2037)

One Transitional Council member resigns per year. Each departing seat is filled through the Sovereign Council's constitutional nomination and confirmation process (Chamber Act §6.2), creating the stagger the constitution requires.

| Year | Event | Sovereign Council status |
|---|---|---|
| **Year 6 (2031)** | Member 1 resigns (Economic transition). **Class A, Seat 1** filled by Territorial nomination, Productive confirmation. Full 5-year term begins. | 6 transitional + 1 constitutional |
| **Year 7 (2032)** | Member 2 resigns (Military). **Class A, Seat 2** filled. | 5 + 2 |
| **Year 8 (2033)** | Member 3 resigns (Energy/Infrastructure). **Class A, Seat 3** filled. *Class A is now complete — 3 seats on full 5-year terms, staggered by 1 year each.* | 4 + 3 |
| **Year 9 (2034)** | Member 4 resigns (Justice). **Class B, Seat 1** filled with a 30-month initial term (to establish the Class B offset). | 3 + 4 (constitutional majority) |
| **Year 10 (2035)** | Member 5 resigns (Education/Healthcare). **Class B, Seat 2** filled with 30-month initial term. | 2 + 5 |
| **Year 11 (2036)** | Member 6 resigns (Foreign Affairs). **Class B, Seat 3** filled with 30-month initial term. *Class B is now complete.* | 1 + 6 |
| **Year 12 (2037)** | **Cooper resigns.** The **Wildcard seat** is filled by **universal citizen vote** — the only directly elected seat on the Sovereign Council. *The stagger is complete. The Transitional Council no longer exists.* | 0 + 7 (fully constitutional) |

**Why this sequence works:**

- **Class A (Years 6–8):** three seats on full 5-year terms, each staggered by one year. By Year 11, the first Class A seat is up for re-election — the replacement cycle has begun before the transition even ends.
- **Class B (Years 9–11):** three seats on shortened initial terms (30 months), creating the half-term offset from Class A. After the initial terms, all Class B seats carry full 5-year terms on their own stagger.
- **The Wildcard (Year 12):** Cooper's seat becomes the universal-vote wildcard — the one seat the citizens elect directly. Cooper leaves last, and the last seat he vacates is the most democratic one.

**The continuity floor (§6.2) holds throughout.** At no point do fewer than four Council members serve — the minimum for quorum. The transition never creates a governance gap.

## Years 12–20: Cooper as Private Citizen (2037–2045)

**Cooper is no longer in government.** He holds no office, commands no troops, and chairs no board. He is a private citizen of New Crossroads.

**He retains one extraordinary power: the sunset veto.** Until Year 20 (2045), Cooper may veto any legislation, regulation, or chamber resolution. The power is personal, non-delegable, and non-transferable.

**How the veto works in practice (Years 12–20):**

- The chambers pass legislation normally. Cooper has no seat, no vote, and no right to attend sessions.
- Any enacted law is published in the national legal registry. Cooper may read it like any citizen.
- Within 30 days of enactment, Cooper may file a **written veto** with the Supreme Court, stating which provision he vetoes and why. The veto is absolute — no override, no appeal, no supermajority exception.
- A vetoed provision is struck. The chambers may redraft and re-enact — Cooper may veto again if the redraft offends the same principle.
- **Cooper may NOT veto retroactively.** A law older than 30 days is beyond the veto window. This prevents Cooper from holding old laws hostage.
- **Cooper may NOT propose legislation.** He can block; he cannot build. The asymmetry is deliberate — the veto is a shield, not a sword.

**What Cooper uses it for (likely):** vetoing attempts to weaken the entrenched floors, to raid the SWF, to create a government bond market, to tax residential property, to merge the PPK and CED, to build a surveillance capability inside CMIS, or to extend any person's power beyond constitutional limits — including his own.

**What Cooper does NOT use it for (likely):** day-to-day policy disagreements. A veto on a tariff schedule or a park boundary would be absurd — and would erode the veto's legitimacy for the fights that matter. Cooper's incentive is to use the veto rarely and only on constitutional-architecture questions, because every frivolous use weakens the norm that his successors cannot have this power.

## Year 20: The Sunset (2045)

**The veto expires.** Automatically, constitutionally, with no ceremony required. The national legal registry records the date. The Supreme Court acknowledges it. The chambers note it.

**No person in New Crossroads will ever hold this power again.** The entrenching provision (highest bar — Chamber Act §10.5) requires supermajorities of every chamber plus a 75% national referendum to revive it. That bar has never been cleared for anything, and it will not be cleared for this.

**What remains of Cooper's legacy:**

- The seventeen founding acts — most now in the Settled Codex, having passed their 15-year unchallenged threshold.
- The five-pot architecture, the reference-rate anchor, the privacy wall, the co-op advantage, the informal-economy guarantee, the CME-proof grid, the dividend, the Durahome, the parkweb, the nuclear deterrent — all running on rules, not on Cooper.
- The staggered Sovereign Council, seeded by seven revolutionaries who resigned one at a time so the machinery would work.
- And a precedent: **extraordinary power, voluntarily surrendered, on a published schedule, with a constitutional expiry date.** The next crisis that tempts someone to claim extraordinary authority will be measured against the man who held it, used it sparingly, and let it die.

**The system does not need Cooper anymore.** That was the point.

## Design Philosophy (Informative)

**The revolution is not the founding.** The revolution seizes power; the founding builds institutions. Cooper understood that revolutions are romantic and institutions are boring — and that the boring part is what survives.

**Extraordinary power is safest when it has a published death date.** A veto that expires in 2045 is a shield with a shelf life. Everyone — including Cooper — knows the date. The chambers can wait it out. The population can count the years. There is no ambiguity, no renewal clause, no "emergency extension." The power dies on schedule because dying on schedule is what makes it trustworthy while it lives.

**The stagger is the institution's immune system.** Seven revolutionaries could have resigned together and handed the Sovereign Council to seven strangers on the same day. Instead, one left per year for seven years. Each departing seat was replaced through the constitutional process, and each new member overlapped with the remaining revolutionaries. Institutional knowledge transferred in layers, not in a single dump. By Year 12, the Council had been half-revolutionary and half-constitutional for six years — long enough for the constitutional members to carry the culture without needing the founders.

**The system was ready before the revolution.** The seventeen acts were not improvised. They were written in exile, debated underground, revised for years. The revolution was the *delivery mechanism* for a constitutional order that already existed on paper. Cooper's contribution was not the design — it was the credibility to implement it, the discipline to restrain himself while doing so, and the integrity to leave when it was done.

**The transition timeline is the soil. The sector timelines are the crop.** Everything the founding acts describe — the dividend, the grid, the housing, the education, the parks, the military, the Exchange — grows from the institutional soil laid in Years 0–5 and fertilised by the power transfer of Years 6–12. Cooper planted. The nation harvests. And no one person will ever hold the watering can again.
