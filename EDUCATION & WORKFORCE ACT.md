# Crossroads Education and Workforce Act of 2025: Draft 2.0

**Posted to r/Bulwarkomics**  
**Draft**: 2.0 Detailed | **Date**: April 13, 2026  
**Author**: Thunderfish2  

> **Cross-reference**: This act is companion legislation to the *Crossroads Federated Cooperatives Limited (FCL) Structure Act of 2025 (Draft 3.0)*, the *Crossroads Monetary Reform & Economic Stabilization Act of 2025 (Draft 8.0)*, and the *Crossroads Credit Union Act of 2025 (Draft 4.0)*. School governance follows the asset/operator separation model from the *Crossroads Utilities Act of 2025 (Draft 1.0)*: Local School Boards own facilities and long-term assets; Teacher Professional FCLs operate instructional delivery.

---

## Overview

This act establishes a debt-free education and workforce system for 18 million students (ages 5–20) and 13 million journeymen within a $14.5 trillion GDP (65% co-ops/$9.425 trillion, 15% corporate/$2.175 trillion, 20% informal/$2.9 trillion), scaling to $38.94 trillion GDP (65% co-ops/$25.311 trillion) by 2075.

**Governance Innovation:** Each public school is organized as a hybrid FCL — 70% owned by a Local School Board (parents, PTA, military liaison, sports representative) and 30% owned by a Teacher Professional FCL (all licensed teachers at the school).

The act preserves the **voucher system** ($5,000/year per student, following the student to any licensed school), the **mandatory 24-month service program** (ages 19–20, integrated as work experience and placement networking). A $94.35 billion Sovereign Wealth Fund (SWF) (part of $550 billion SWF, to $3.082 trillion) supports operations, managed by the National Learning Council (NLC) with 5,000 Credit Unions, 27,100 FCLs, and $601.58 billion revenue.

## Section 1: Objectives

- Deliver debt-free education for 18 million students (ages 5–20), targeting $75,000 journeyman earnings by age 20.
- Fund education with **$5,000/year vouchers**, enabling competitive market choice among co-op and corporate schools.
- Organize every public school as a **hybrid FCL**: 70% Local School Board / 30% Teacher Professional FCL.
- Support high-potential student ventures with loans and educational flexibility.
- Mandate **24-month service (ages 19–20)** as integrated work experience, with stipends ($12,000/year vocational, $15,000/year professional), credentialing 1 million journeymen annually.
- Support 13 million journeymen, 2 million masters, 100,000 grandmasters, and 15 million immigrants.
- Establish **apprenticeship pathways** to master/grandmaster status with bonus shares for project oversight.
- Create a **Teacher Professional Pathway** (journeyman → master → grandmaster teacher) distinct from other professions.
- Integrate **co-op clubs** (sports, arts, recreation) into school governance with board representation.
- Align with healthcare workforce needs (1.8 million students, 100,000 service graduates).
- Enhance skills via CLS Academy, Governance Training Program, Credit Union Academy, Alliance Network, and Media and Mentorship Program, tracked by CWD.

### 3.2 School Governance: Hybrid FCL Model

#### 3.2.1 Asset/Operator Separation

Each public school separates asset ownership from instructional operation:

| Role | Entity | Ownership | Responsibilities |
|------|--------|-----------|------------------|
| **Asset Owner** | Local School Board | 70% of school FCL | Facilities, maintenance, budget, long-term planning, principal hiring (joint) |
| **Instructional Operator** | Teacher Professional FCL | 30% of school FCL | Pedagogy, curriculum delivery, teacher hiring/firing, schedule, instructional spending |
| **Chartering & Oversight** | Regional Board | External | License schools, audit compliance, enforce 65% co-op target, dispute resolution |

#### 3.2.2 Local School Board (70% Owner)

Each public school has a **7-member Local School Board** with the following composition:

| Seat | Constituency | Election Method | Term |
|------|--------------|----------------|------|
| Seat 1 | Parents/guardians (at-large) | Elected by parents/guardians in attendance zone | 4 years |
| Seat 2 | Parents/guardians (at-large) | Elected by parents/guardians in attendance zone | 4 years |
| Seat 3 | Parents/guardians (at-large) | Elected by parents/guardians in attendance zone | 4 years |
| Seat 4 | Parents/guardians (at-large) | Elected by parents/guardians in attendance zone | 4 years |
| Seat 5 | PTA representative | Elected by PTA membership | 2 years |
| Seat 6 | Military liaison | Appointed by Regional Board from list of veterans/JROTC instructors | 3 years |
| Seat 7 | Sports/Activities representative | Elected by co-op club members in the school zone | 2 years |

**Staggered terms:** Seats 1–4 elected in presidential-style cycles (two seats per election every 2 years). Seats 5–7 elected in off-cycle years.

**Quorum:** 4 members. **Voting:** Simple majority, except as modified by joint management committee below.

**Powers of Local School Board:**
- Approve annual budget (subject to Teacher Co-op veto on instructional spending)
- Own and maintain school facilities (buildings, grounds, equipment)
- Approve capital improvements (loans from Regional Board or CCIF)
- Participate in joint hiring/firing of principal (60% board / 40% teacher co-op vote)
- Approve non-instructional staff hiring (custodians, cafeteria, security)
- Set school calendar (start/end dates, holidays) within NLC guidelines

#### 3.2.3 Teacher Professional FCL (30% Owner)

All licensed teachers at the school are **automatically members** of the school's Teacher Professional FCL, organized as a Professional FCL with the following specifications:

| Provision | Teacher Professional FCL |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| Ownership | 30% of school FCL (Local Board owns 70%) |
| Voting | 1 teacher, 1 vote (internal); 30% voting power on joint management committee |
| Profit-sharing | **Cost-share model**: each teacher's "revenue" = share of voucher funding based on class size × student load |
| Retainer shares | Parents may hold non-voting retainer shares (2–8% patronage on fees paid), capped at 20% of Teacher Co-op equity |
| Investor capital | **Not permitted** (capital provided by Local Board via Regional Board loans) |

**Internal Teacher Co-op Governance:**
- All licensed teachers hold equal base shares ($1 par value, up to 1,000)
- Teachers elect a **Teacher Council** (3–7 members, depending on school size) to represent them on the joint management committee
- Internal decisions (e.g., hiring new teachers, professional development, peer review) made by simple majority of all teachers
- **Cost-share formula:** Each teacher's compensation = (their student load ÷ total school student load) × (voucher revenue allocated to instruction) + base stipend

**Powers of Teacher Professional FCL (exercised through joint management committee):**
- Hire and fire teachers (internal peer review process, appealable to Regional Board)
- Set curriculum delivery methods (within NLC standards)
- Manage instructional schedule (class periods, teacher assignments)
- **Veto power** on instructional spending
- Participate in joint hiring/firing of principal (40% vote)
- Set professional development requirements (within NLC guidelines)

#### 3.2.4 Joint Management Committee

The Local School Board (70% voting power) and Teacher Professional FCL (30% voting power) operate through a **Joint Management Committee** for decisions requiring both parties.

| Decision Type | Local Board Vote % | Teacher Co-op Vote % | Threshold | Notes |
|---------------|-------------------|---------------------|-----------|-------|
| Principal hiring/firing | 60% | 40% | 60% of combined | Prevents unilateral action |
| Budget (total) | 100% | 0% | Simple majority | Board owns budget |
| Instructional spending (within budget) | 0% | **Veto** | Teacher Co-op can veto | Blocks cuts to classrooms |
| Non-instructional spending | 100% | 0% | Simple majority | Facilities, admin, etc. |
| Curriculum changes (within NLC) | 50% | 50% | 60% of combined | Requires both parties |
| School closure | 100% | 0% | 6/7 board + Regional Board approval | High bar |

**Deadlock resolution:** If the Joint Management Committee cannot reach agreement after 30 days, either party may appeal to the **Regional Board**, which appoints a mediator from the Co-op Department. If mediation fails, the Regional Board decides by 6/11 vote, with preference for maintaining instructional continuity.

#### 3.2.5 School FCL Tiers

Schools are organized under the FCL Act's tier system:

| Tier | Enrollment | Governance | DAI Floor | Regional Board Oversight |
|------|-----------|------------|-----------|--------------------------|
| Tier 1 (Micro) | <75 students | No board required (principal manages with teacher input) | 0.5% | Light (annual report only) |
| Tier 2 (Mid) | 75–500 students | 5-member Joint Committee (3 board + 2 teacher) | 1.0% | Standard (biannual audit) |
| Tier 3 (Full) | 500–2,000 students | 7-member Local Board + Teacher Council (as above) | 1.0% | Full (quarterly review) |
| Heavy Industry | >2,000 students or >$25M budget | 9–11 member board, 51% member ownership minimum | 1.0% | Central Council annual review |

**Heavy Industry Clause for large schools:** Requires Community Benefit Agreement, Labor Peace Agreement (if >1,000 employees), and Central Council approval.

#### 3.2.6 Chartering and Oversight by Regional Boards

Per the Government Act, 20 Regional Boards (11 members each) are responsible for:

- Issuing **10-year renewable charters** to School FCLs
- Enforcing the **65% co-op enrollment target** (65% of students in co-op schools, which School FCLs count as)
- Auditing compliance with governance rules (biannually for Tier 3, annually for Heavy Industry)
- Approving **tier upgrades/downgrades** (simple majority)
- Appointing the **military liaison** to each Local School Board (from a list of qualified veterans/JROTC instructors)
- Resolving deadlocks
- Revoking charters for persistent fraud, underperformance (2 years below proficiency thresholds), or governance failure (6/11 vote, appealable to Central Council)

### 3.3 Voucher System

- **$5,000/year per student** ($90 billion, $4.5 billion/region), adjusted biennially for 3% GDP growth ($13,500/year by 2075)
- Voucher follows the student to any licensed school (co-op or corporate)
- Paid via $94 billion credit union checking, subject to 1% BWC Transaction Fee and 1% Checking Account Fee (covered by SWF)
- Tracked via CWD
- Blockchain-based school performance data published on CWD (graduation rates, journeyman earnings, teacher retention)
- Annual voucher audits (20% of schools, $10 million/year) cap $50 million/year fraud. Fraudulent schools face 1-year funding suspension (6/11 NLC vote)

### 3.4 Co-op Clubs (Sports, Arts, Recreation)

Per FCL Act Section 2.2, schools shall host or sponsor **co-op clubs** for extracurricular activities:

| Club Type | Organization | Dues | Governance | Board Representation |
|-----------|--------------|------|------------|----------------------|
| Sports (soccer, basketball, etc.) | Tier 2 FCL | $50/year per member | Members elect club captain | Sports/Activities seat (Seat 7) |
| Arts (theater, band, choir) | Tier 2 FCL | $50/year per member | Members elect club captain | Sports/Activities seat (Seat 7) |
| Recreation (chess, debate, hiking) | Tier 1 or Tier 2 FCL | $25–$50/year | Members elect club captain | Sports/Activities seat (Seat 7) |

**Club rights:**
- Priority booking for school facilities after instructional hours
- May generate revenue through events, concessions, sponsorships
- Profit split: 50% to club, 50% to School FCL general fund
- Clubs elect the **Sports/Activities representative** to the Local School Board (Seat 7) via ranked-choice voting among club captains

### 3.5 Dropout Support

- $6 billion/year apprenticeships for 900,000–2 million dropouts (45,000–100,000/region) in sectors (e.g., healthcare, construction), with voting rights for wildcards/FCL boards at age 20.
- 50,000 dropout volunteers/year (2,500/region) support education (e.g., teaching aides), trained via $6 billion/year apprenticeship program.
- 25,000/year transition to journeyman apprenticeships after 2 years via $750 million/year reintegration ($500 million apprenticeships, $200 million transition, $50 million retention bonus, SWF-funded).

### 3.6 Junior Integration and Student Ventures

- **$500 venture loans** (0%, 5-year repayment, $2 million/year, $100,000/region) for ages 12–15, issued by CLS agents based on lenient business plan criteria (e.g., basic revenue projections, market feasibility), subject to 1% BWC Transaction Fee and 0.5% Liquidity Fee (~$20,000/year), covered by SWF. Students receive loan advice and debt management, with operational mentorship from masters or FCL board members.
- **Supplemental Loans**: $5,000 (0%, 5-year repayment, $10 million/year, $500,000/region) for successful ventures ($10,000+ annual revenue), or $50,000 ($5 million/year, $250,000/region) for wildly successful ventures (altering educational paths, e.g., scalable innovation, regional economic impact), issued by CLS agents with loan advice.
- **Educational Flexibility**: Part-time schooling (50% curriculum), independent study, or accelerated journeyman credentialing (age 18+ with master endorsement), approved by Regional Boards (6/11 vote).
- **Mentorship**: CLS agents provide loan-related mentorship (e.g., funding scalability, repayment plans). Masters or grandmasters offer operational mentorship.
- **Oversight**: Annual audits ($1 million/year, $50,000/region) via blockchain-based registry, with metrics (revenue, jobs) on CWD. NLC reviews biennially (8/11 vote).

- ### 3.7 Police Service Vocational Pathway

Policing shall be organized as a standard blue-collar vocational trade under the journeyman–master–grandmaster ladder identical to all skilled trades (cross-reference: Government Act §2.1.2). No college degree or external professional certification may be required or weighted in hiring, promotion, credentialing, or funding decisions at any level.

#### 3.7.1 Secondary Vocational Track (Ages 16–18)
Municipal police departments shall partner with local Tier 2 and Tier 3 School FCLs to offer an optional **police vocational module** as part of the career-path specialization curriculum (see §3.1).  
- 3 weeks per year of hands-on apprenticeship hours.  
- Curriculum: patrol procedures, de-escalation and community mediation, basic forensics, informal-economy protection (Monetary Act §1A), public safety co-op club leadership, and neighborhood problem-solving.  
- Students earn journeyman-eligible credit hours tracked via CWD.  
- Municipal departments sponsor **Public Safety Co-op Clubs** (parallel to §3.4 Co-op Clubs) for ages 12–18. These Tier 1 or Tier 2 FCLs (dues $25–$50/year) give youth direct exposure and elect a representative to the Local School Board (parallel to the Sports/Activities seat).

#### 3.7.2 Mandatory 24-Month Service Apprenticeship (Ages 19–20)
Police Service counts as a full vocational apprenticeship option within the Mandatory Service Program (§4).  
- Participants (those who selected the police track) complete their 24 months in their **home municipality**.  
- Structure: 3-month boot-camp phase (weapons, fitness, survival — identical to the male service track) followed by 21 months of street apprenticeship under a Master Sergeant.  
- Stipend paid at the vocational rate ($12,000/year) via credit-union checking.  
- Successful completion grants automatic **Journeyman Patrol Officer** credential at age 20, with full powers, $50 BWC wallet, and 1 base share.

#### 3.7.3 Journeyman → Master → Grandmaster Progression
Progression follows the identical merit-based ladder used by teachers (§5.2) and all other trades (§5.3), with metrics tracked via CWD.

| Rank                  | Minimum Time | Requirements (CWD-tracked)                                      | Promotion Method                          | Pay Bump (relative to journeyman rate) |
|-----------------------|--------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Journeyman Patrol Officer | Age 20      | Completion of 24-month service apprenticeship                   | Automatic                                | Base (matches journeyman electrician/plumber) |
| Master Sergeant       | 5 years     | 5+ apprentices mentored + community metrics (repeat calls ↓, resident satisfaction ↑) | 51 % peer vote of officers in the department | +25 %                                 |
| Chief of Police (municipal) | 10 years   | Master status + 80 % apprentice retention + demonstrated leadership | Municipal council + local wildcard representative | +40 %                                 |

- **Residency Rule**: Every officer must live within 10 km of their assigned patrol zone (waiver only by 6/11 Regional Board vote for proven hardship).  
- **Community Integration**: 20 % of sworn officer time must be non-enforcement (neighborhood walks, co-op security workshops, youth mentorship). Measured and tied to regional funding bonuses via CWD.  
- **Oversight**: Regional Boards retain funding and standards authority (Government Act §2.1); municipal governments handle day-to-day operations. Biannual CWD audits enforce the blue-collar apprenticeship model and prevent credential creep.

This pathway ensures police officers are recruited, trained, and promoted from the same local working-class communities they serve, preserving the worker-centric meritocracy of the entire education and workforce system.

## Section 5: Workforce Development

### 5.1 Composition

- **Journeymen**: 13 million (650,000/region), credentialed at age 20 with $75,000 earnings, accessing $2,600 wallet ($8,449 by 2075). Credentialed via education (ages 16–18) and service (ages 19–20) in vocational or professional fields.
- **Masters**: 2 million (100,000/region), 5+ years post-service or 3+ years with exceptional project impact, 5+ apprentices, $150,000 revenue, oversee complex projects (e.g., high-rise construction, open-heart surgery). Receive 2% per-share bonus ($20/BWC/year for $1,000/BWC, $40 million/year, paid via $94 billion savings).
- **Grandmasters**: 100,000 (5,000/region), 10+ apprentices, 80% retention, $1.5 million impact, oversee critical infrastructure (e.g., highway interchanges, advanced surgeries). Receive 4% per-share bonus ($40/BWC/year for $1,000/BWC, $4 million/year, paid via $94 billion savings).
- **Apprentices**: 20 million cumulative (1 million/region), mentored by masters during service and journeyman phase.
- **Immigrants**: 15 million (750,000/region, 300,000/year), integrated via $5 billion/region apprenticeships, $600 million/year language/co-op training and credential assessment (6-month timeline, $50 million/year, Credit Union-funded, 25% prioritized for healthcare: 37,500/year, $12.5 million/year), points-based system.
- **High Earners**: 100,000 (5,000/region, $100,000–$150,000/year).

### 5.2 Teacher Professional Pathway

Teachers are licensed professionals with a distinct pathway within the Education Act.

| Level | Criteria | Compensation | Bonus |
|-------|----------|--------------|-------|
| **Journeyman Teacher** (years 1–3) | Licensed, teaching credential, 80% student proficiency | $50,000–$60,000 | None |
| **Master Teacher** | 5+ years teaching, 5+ apprentice teachers mentored, 80% student proficiency, $150,000 instructional impact (measured by value-added assessment) | $80,000–$100,000 | 2% per-share bonus ($20/year for $1,000 base shares) |
| **Grandmaster Teacher** | 10+ apprentice teachers mentored, 80% retention of mentees, $1.5 million instructional impact (e.g., school-wide gains, curriculum innovation) | $120,000–$150,000 | 4% per-share bonus ($40/year for $1,000 base shares) |

**Master and Grandmaster Teacher privileges:**
- May serve as mentors to journeyman teachers and apprentice teachers (ages 16–18 in service program)
- Eligible for Teacher Council leadership
- Receive priority for professional development funding
- Grandmaster teachers may be appointed to NLC technical expert positions

**Certification:** Master and Grandmaster Teacher status certified by the Joint Management Committee (Local Board + Teacher Co-op), confirmed by Regional Board, tracked via CWD.

### 5.3 Apprenticeship Progression

- **Journeyman Credentialing**: At age 20, students graduate as journeymen after 4 years of education (ages 16–18 curriculum, 3 weeks/year apprenticeships) and 2 years of service (full-time apprenticeship). Earnings: $45,000 from vouchers ($5,000/year x 9 years, ages 12–20), $24,000 (vocational) or $30,000 (professional) from stipends, totaling $69,000–$75,000, paid via $94 billion checking/savings. Safety training ($50 million/year, SWF-funded) ensures compliance for small projects.
- **Journeyman Oversight**: Journeymen manage small-scale projects (e.g., house construction, simple surgeries), mentored by masters at FCLs or institutions.
- **Master Status**: Achieved after 5+ years or 3+ years with 80% project success, 5+ apprentices, $150,000 revenue. Masters oversee complex projects, receiving 2% per-share bonus. $200 million/year mentorship program (SWF-funded) trains 100,000 additional masters by 2035, prioritizing healthcare and construction.
- **Grandmaster Status**: Achieved after 10+ apprentices, 80% retention, $1.5 million impact, overseeing critical infrastructure, receiving 4% per-share bonus.
- **Progression Pathway**: Journeymen apprentice under masters for 5–10 years. Boards (5–7 members, $25,000–$75,000/year) certify master/grandmaster status via CWD ($15 million/year), standardized with regional flexibility (6/11 Regional Board vote).

## Section 6: Post-Educational Training Programs

### 6.1 CLS Academy

- **Purpose**: Trains 60,000 CLS agents (3,000/region) to manage $452.5 billion loans ($294.125 billion co-op, $67.875 billion corporate, $90.5 billion informal), issue student venture loans, support bankruptcy recovery, and ensure 65% co-op GDP ($9.425 trillion).
- **Structure**: In Crossroads City with a cyber-focused campus in New Tech City, offering 1-year training ($1.5375 billion). Agents (44,000 at $75,000/year, 3,666 seniors at $120,000/year, 18 regional at $200,000/year) manage loans for 27,100 FCLs, students, and informal ventures, with 6-week updates every 3 years. $50 million/year rural training hubs ensure 50% rural participation (1,500 agents/region).
- **Curriculum**: Loan management ($452.5 billion), debt management, client portfolio development, bankruptcy recovery plans ($94 billion savings), student venture loan issuance (lenient criteria, e.g., basic revenue projections), mentorship for successful ventures ($10,000+ revenue or altering educational paths).
- **Integration**: CLS cadets (journeymen, masters) spend 2-year national service at the Academy (males post-boot camp, females directly), mentoring up to 3 trainees/year. Agents issue $500 venture loans, provide loan advice, and support bankruptcy recovery, tracked via CWD.
- **Cost**: $1.5375 billion/year ($76.875 million/region), funded by $1.2 billion credit union revenue and $337.5 million SWF.
- **Oversight**: CWD tracks agent performance, citations (5–10% salary bumps for 10/50/100 loans), disciplinary actions (5–15% fines, suspensions, expulsion).

### 6.2 Governance Training Program

- **Purpose**: Trains members and staff of government and regional boards (NEB, Regional Boards, NLC, etc.), Central Council staff (510), auditors (50), and administrative workers (16,000, 800/district) for governance roles supporting education, workforce, and economic development.
- **Structure**: $2.5 billion/year ($125 million/region, $100 million/year curriculum, including $5 billion voter training), offering 1-year in-person/online training, with 6-week updates every 3 years. $50 million/year rural hubs ensure 50% rural participation.

- Capacity: 5,000 officers annually nationwide (250/region), with 50 % rural participation guaranteed via satellite training hubs ($50 million/year).

**Curriculum** (80 % hands-on, 20 % classroom):
- Advanced de-escalation, crisis negotiation, and community mediation.

- Forensics, evidence handling, and informal-economy protection protocols (Monetary Act §1A).
- Regional sustainability and public safety co-op club integration.
- Leadership and mentorship training (prepares officers to take apprentices in municipal departments).
- All modules delivered by Master Sergeants; no outside consultants or academic lecturers permitted.

**Eligibility and Progression** (CWD-tracked):
| Level | Entry Requirement | Outcome on Graduation | Promotion Path |
|-------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------------|
| **Journeyman Patrol Officer** | Mandatory service + 2 years street time | Certified for regional coordination duty | Eligible for Master Sergeant track |
| **Master Sergeant (Regional)** | 5+ years + Academy graduation + 5 apprentices | Leads regional units or trains municipal departments | +25 % pay bump; peer-vote promotion |

This Academy ensures the regional police coordination function remains worker-led, merit-based, and community-rooted while giving Regional Boards the trained personnel they need to fulfill their oversight role under the Government Act.

## Section 8: Governance

### 8.1 National Learning Council (NLC)

- **Composition**: 11 members (6 regional representatives elected by 20 Regional Boards, 4 technical experts [including at least 2 Grandmaster Teachers], 1 chairman), appointed by Central Council (6/11 vote, 11/20 Boards confirm).
- **Voting**:
  - Routine (e.g., budgets, standards): 6/11 vote, 7 days.
  - Strategic (e.g., curriculum changes, venture support): 8/11 vote, 14 days with Regional Board input.
  - Emergency (e.g., pandemics, enrollment spikes): 5/11 vote, 72 hours with 3/20 EGA trigger.
- **Operations**: 10,000 staff (500/district), $100 million/year, funded by $94.35 billion SWF. Public minutes on CWD, $1 million/region forums, annual town halls ($10 million/year, SWF-funded). District structure reviewed every 10 years (8/11 vote, $5 million/year).

### 8.2 Regional Boards

Per the Government Act, 20 Regional Boards (11 members each) have the following education-specific responsibilities:

- Issue **10-year renewable charters** to School FCLs
- Enforce the **65% co-op enrollment target** (65% of students in co-op schools)
- Audit compliance with governance rules (biannually for Tier 3, annually for Heavy Industry)
- Approve **tier upgrades/downgrades** (simple majority)
- Appoint the **military liaison** to each Local School Board (from a list of qualified veterans/JROTC instructors)
- Resolve Joint Management Committee deadlocks (6/11 vote after mediation)
- Revoke charters for cause (6/11 vote, appealable to Central Council)
- Oversee co-op club registration and dispute resolution
- Confirm Master/Grandmaster Teacher certifications

### 8.3 Management

- **NLC**: Oversees $94.35 billion SWF, $452.5 billion loans, 20 regions, sets system-wide standards.
- **Regional Boards**: Charter, audit, and oversee School FCLs.
- **Local School Boards + Teacher FCLs**: Manage individual schools.

## Appendix B: Glossary

- **NLC**: National Learning Council, 11-member body managing education and workforce standards.
- **Regional Board**: 20 boards (11 members each) that charter, audit, and oversee School FCLs.
- **Local School Board**: 7-member board (4 parents, 1 PTA, 1 military liaison, 1 sports rep) that owns school facilities and holds 70% of school FCL voting power.
- **Teacher Professional FCL**: All licensed teachers at a school, organized as a Professional FCL, holding 30% of school FCL voting power.
- **Joint Management Committee**: Committee of Local Board + Teacher Co-op representatives that makes joint decisions (principal hiring, curriculum changes).
- **Co-op Club**: Recreation-focused FCL (sports, arts, etc.) with $25–$50/year dues.
- **FCLs**: Federated Cooperatives Limited, worker/customer-owned co-ops.
- **SWF**: Sovereign Wealth Fund, $550 billion (2025), scaling to $3.082 trillion (2075).
- **BWC**: Bulwark Coin, blockchain-based currency backed by 0.025 oz precious metals.
- **CWD**: Crossroads Workforce Database, $150 million/year, tracking merit, vouchers, workforce, engagement.
- **DAI**: Deflation Adjustment Index (from Monetary Act), with floor of 0.5% for Tier 1 schools, 1.0% for Tiers 2–3.
- **CCIF**: Co-operative Capital Investment Fund, $2.395 trillion voluntary liquidity pool.
