# Crossroads Federated Cooperatives Limited (FCL) Structure Act of 2025: Draft 3.0

**Posted to r/Bulwarkomics**
**Draft**: 3.0 | **Date**: April 10, 2026
**Author**: Thunderfish2

> **Cross-reference**: This act is companion legislation to the *Crossroads Monetary Reform & Economic Stabilization Act of 2025 (Draft 8.0)* and the *Crossroads Credit Union Act of 2025 (Draft 4.0)*. All references to the Deflation Adjustment Index (DAI), BWC Appreciation Rate (BAR), Informal Economy Guarantee, and tax rates are governed by those acts. FCL profit tax is 5% on profit over $100,000 per Monetary Act Section 4. FCLs make no direct contribution to the Sovereign Wealth Fund (SWF).

## Purpose

Establishes the structure, governance, and financial operations of **195,100 Federated Cooperatives Limited (FCLs)** across 20 regions in New Crossroads, plus **27,600 recreation-focused co-op clubs**, for a total of **222,700 cooperative entities**. FCLs drive 65% of the **$14.5 trillion GDP** ($9.425 trillion co-op sector) in 2025, scaling to **$25.311 trillion** by 2075, using Bulwark Coin (BWC). FCLs produce goods and services in retail, manufacturing, agriculture, energy, technology, professional services, and recreation — distinct from credit unions, which manage financial services.

## Section 1: Purpose and Scope

- **Objective**: Establish 195,100 worker/customer-owned FCLs (9,755 per region) plus 27,600 co-op clubs (1,380 per region) to drive 65% of $14.5 trillion GDP ($9.425 trillion co-op sector) in 2025, scaling to $25.311 trillion (65% of $38.94 trillion) by 2075, contributing $2.036 trillion exports and utilizing $274 billion resources ($112.5 billion minerals, $45.375 billion agriculture, $26.625 billion fuels, $9 billion forestry/water, $31.18 billion parks, $49.32 billion other).
- **Role**: Produce goods and services in retail (e.g., grocery stores), manufacturing (e.g., steel mills), agriculture, energy, technology, professional services (e.g., law firms), and recreation (e.g., co-op clubs), empowering workers, customers, and investors via cooperative ownership, patronage returns, and profit-sharing.
- **Distinction**:
  - FCLs produce goods and services, unlike credit unions managing financial services.
  - Unlike corporations (15% of GDP, $2.175 trillion), FCLs prioritize member ownership with investor participation.
  - FCLs pay a **5% profit tax on profit over $100,000** (per Monetary Act Section 4) and **no SWF contribution**.
- **Regional Alignment**: Supports regional roles (e.g., Region 1: retail, $150 billion; Region 2: nuclear, $6.6 trillion; Region 3: mining, $470 million profit).
- **Amendments**: Require 75% Central Council vote.

## Section 2: FCL Structure and Ownership

### 2.1 FCL Network

- **Structure**: 195,100 core FCLs, plus 27,600 recreation-focused co-op clubs. All are worker/customer-owned and produce goods and services across retail, manufacturing, agriculture, energy, technology, professional services, and recreation. **FCLs operate under three maturity tiers (see Section 2.4), with only Tier 3 requiring the full governance structure described in this Act.**
- **Revenue**: $9.425 trillion (65% GDP), funded by $294.125 billion loans (per Credit Union Act), capital investments, and profits.

### 2.2 Membership

- **Citizens (112 million)**:
  - **Ages 20+ (94 million Corporate Citizens)**: Auto-incorporated, join FCLs with $1 base share (or $50/year for co-op clubs). Access business liability insurance externally ($10,000–$100,000, $50–$1,000/year).
  - **Ages 12–19**: Join voluntarily via worker/customer accounts (1 base share, parental permission). Eligible for voting and patronage with oversight.
  - **Non-Members (Under 12, 12–19 Opt-Outs)**: Use cash ($1.45 trillion pool), no FCL ownership or benefits.
- **Co-op Clubs**: 40 million members (within 94 million FCL members) by 2075, paying $50/year dues ($1 base share equivalent), generating $2 billion/year. **Co-op club members receive priority booking for recreation slots (7-day advance window) and exclusive voting rights on recreation policy.**

### 2.3 Ownership Models

| Type | Ownership Ratio | Details |
|------|----------------|---------|
| Standard FCLs | 70% member / 30% investor (default) | Used for typical businesses such as grocery stores. Workers/customers hold base shares ($1, 4–8% dividends); investors contribute $50,000–$5 million, voting capped at 5% per investor. |
| Capital-Intensive FCLs | 60% member / 40% investor | Used for high-capital projects such as steel mills or oil refineries (investors need a larger stake to fund expensive equipment and infrastructure). |
| Community-Focused FCLs | 80% member / 20% investor | Used when higher employee/member ownership makes sense (e.g., co-op clubs). |
| Professional FCLs | 100% worker-owned | Used for professional services such as law firms, engineering firms, or groups of doctors. Licensed professionals hold equal shares (e.g., 10% per lawyer, $1,000). **Professional FCLs may also operate under a cost-share model where each member keeps their own revenue and shares expenses, with no cross-member profit-sharing.** Non-equity investor capital via loans ($100,000–$1 million, 5–6% DAI-adjusted) or profit-sharing (10–20%, 5–10 years). **Professional FCLs may issue non-voting retainer shares to clients for $1,000–$25,000 each. These shares carry no governance rights, entitle holders to patronage returns (2–8% on fees paid), and are refundable upon retainer termination. Total client share capital cannot exceed 20% of firm equity. Voting control remains 100% with worker-owners.** Hybrid ratios allowed with 75% board approval. |

- **Shares**:
  - **Base Shares**: $1 par value, up to 1,000 ($1,000), 4–8% dividends. Upon exit, members receive par value ($1 per share) plus unpaid dividends. The $1,500 maximum refers to the value of a member's accumulated account (shares × dividends), not share price appreciation.
  - **Investor Shares**: $50,000–$5 million (or up to $500 million under Heavy Industry Clause, Section 2.4.3), tied to 30% ownership (or 40%/20% or 49% under Heavy Industry Clause), voting capped at 5% per investor (or 15% under Heavy Industry Clause), non-sellable except via Section 2.5, estate-transferrable.
- **Mechanics**: Members (70% voting power in standard FCLs); investors (30% voting power, 5% cap per investor). Example: 10 investors (3% stake each) collectively exercise 30% voting power.
- **Investor Eligibility**: Must demonstrate co-op initiation/scaling, approved by FCL and Regional Boards. Secondary buyers must meet same criteria.

### 2.4 FCL Maturity Tiers

**Rationale:** To balance democratic ownership with operational agility, FCLs operate under three tiers based on size, revenue, and loan exposure. All tiers pay the **5% co-op profit tax on profit over $100,000** (per Monetary Act Section 4) and **no SWF contribution**. This creates a natural ladder from informal sector startups to full-scale FCLs, mirroring how corporations scale from sole proprietorship to public company.

#### Tier 1 – Micro-Co-op (1–15 workers, <$2M revenue, <$500k loans)

- **Governance:** No board required. Founder(s) act as management team. Members hold shares but do not elect a board. Decisions made by founders with simple majority worker input.
- **Investor Ownership:** Permitted (small stakes). Founders scaling from informal sector retain control.
- **Patronage:** Voluntary, not required. May be offered at founder's discretion.
- **CCIF:** Fully exempt (no contributions, no participation required).
- **DAI Application:** Mandatory for all loans. Floor: 0.5% (service-sector/micro rate per Monetary Act 8.0).
- **Exit & Funding:** See Sections 2.5, 2.6, and 3.6.
- **Transition:** Must upgrade to Tier 2 upon exceeding any threshold (employees, revenue, OR loans).

#### Tier 2 – Mid-Size Co-op (15–75 workers, $2M–$25M revenue, <$5M loans)

- **Governance:** 3-member board. Composition: 2 worker representatives + 1 investor representative (if investors exist). If no investors, 3 worker representatives. No customer representative. Weekly meetings, simple majority decisions. Emergency decisions delegated to manager/CEO.
- **Optional Expansion:** 5-member board permitted if revenue exceeds $10M (4 worker representatives + 1 investor representative).
- **Patronage:** Required per Section 3.1.
- **CCIF:** Fully optional. May deposit unspent capital allocations and earn returns (5–7%) with no mandatory contribution.
- **DAI Application:** Mandatory for all loans. Floor: 1.0% (standard rate).
- **Exit & Funding:** See Sections 2.5, 2.6, and 3.6.
- **Transition:** Must upgrade to Tier 3 upon exceeding any threshold.

#### Tier 3 – Full FCL (75+ workers or $25M+ revenue)

**Base Tier 3 Rules:**
- **Governance:** Full structure as described in Section 4. 5–7 member board with 70/30 member/investor voting. Patronage, profit-sharing, full audits.
- **Patronage:** Required per Section 3.1.
- **CCIF:** Fully optional. May deposit unspent capital allocations and earn returns (5–7%) with no mandatory contribution.
- **DAI Application:** Mandatory for all loans. Floor: 1.0% (standard rate).
- **Investor Share Cap (Base):** $50,000–$5 million per investor
- **Investor Voting Cap (Base):** 5% per investor
- **Minimum Member Ownership:** 70%
- **Exit & Funding:** See Sections 2.5, 2.6, and 3.6

**Heavy Industry Clause (Optional Rider for Tier 3 FCLs only)**

An FCL may apply to the Regional Board for designation under the Heavy Industry Clause if it meets **one or more** of the following:
- Projected capital expenditure >$500 million
- Operates in: nuclear generation, vehicle manufacturing, aerospace, semiconductor fabrication, heavy steel, port/rail infrastructure, or telecommunications backbone
- Employs >2,000 workers

**If approved, the following modified rules apply:**

| Provision | Base Tier 3 | With Heavy Industry Clause |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------------|
| Investor share cap (individual) | $5 million | **$500 million** |
| Investor voting cap (individual) | 5% | **15%** |
| Minimum member ownership | 70% | **51%** |
| Maximum investor ownership | 30% | **49%** |
| Board size | 5–7 | **7–11** (minimum 5 worker/customer seats) |
| Central Council oversight | No | **Yes (annual review)** |
| Community Benefit Agreement | Recommended | **Required** |
| Labor peace agreement | No | **Required for projects >1,000 workers** |
| CCIF | Fully optional | Fully optional (same as base) |
| DAI floor | 1.0% | 1.0% (same as base) |

**Approval process:** Regional Board → Co-op Department → Central Council (simple majority). Renewal every 5 years.

#### Downgrade

An FCL may request downgrade to a lower tier if it remains below all applicable thresholds for two consecutive years, approved by Regional Board (simple majority). Prevents yo-yo transitions.

### 2.5 Exit Mechanisms for Founders and Investors

**2.5.1 Hierarchy of Exit Rights**
When a founder or investor seeks to sell their ownership stake in an FCL, the following hierarchy applies by tier:

| Tier | Layer 1: Sell to Co-op | Layer 2: Sell to Existing Investors | Layer 3: Co-op Capital Index |
|------|------------------------|--------------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Tier 1 (Micro) | Optional | Permitted (no board vote required) | Not eligible |
| Tier 2 (Mid) | Required first right | Board vote required (simple majority) | Eligible with board approval |
| Tier 3 (Base + Heavy Industry) | Required first right | Board vote required (60% member vote) | Eligible with Regional Board approval |

**2.5.2 Redemption Reserve**
- Tier 2 and Tier 3 FCLs must allocate **5% of annual net profit** to a **Redemption Reserve** until the reserve equals 10% of total member/investor equity.
- Redemption Reserve funds may only be used for Layer 1 buybacks (sell to co-op), worker exits under Section 2.6, or as a liquidity backstop for the Co-op Capital Index.
- Tier 1 FCLs are exempt.

**2.5.3 Secondary Investor Eligibility**
Any buyer in a Layer 2 or Layer 3 transaction must meet the same investor eligibility criteria as a primary investor (Section 2.3): demonstrated co-op initiation/scaling experience, approved by the FCL board and Regional Board. Failure to verify renders the sale void and shares forfeit to the co-op at appraised value.

**2.5.4 Valuation Method**
All exit valuations shall use the **CWD audited book value** as of the most recent fiscal quarter. No premium based on speculative market pricing is permitted for Layers 1 or 2.

**2.5.5 Co-op Capital Index**
- A **Crossroads Co-op Capital Index (CCI)** is established, operated by the CCIF Alliance Network under oversight of the Co-op Department and Central Council.
- Only **non-voting investor shares** (including converted founder shares) and **retainer client shares** (Section 2.3) may be listed. Worker base shares are non-tradable.
- Listing criteria: FCL must be Tier 3 (Base or Heavy Industry), audited by CWD, and have Regional Board approval.
- Trading limited to **Corporate Citizens (20+)** and **existing FCL investors** who meet eligibility criteria. No open market access.
- Pricing: Anchored to CWD audited book value. Daily price movement capped at ±10%. Minimum hold period: 12 months after purchase.
- CCIF acts as liquidity backstop, authorized to purchase unlisted shares at 90% of book value if no buyer found within 90 days.

### 2.6 Member Exit and Share Redemption (Workers & Customers)

**2.6.1 Triggering Events**
A member (worker or customer) is entitled to redemption of their base shares and any accrued but undistributed patronage equity upon:
- (a) Voluntary resignation or retirement from the FCL (for worker-members);
- (b) Permanent move outside the member's region (for customer-members);
- (c) Death (paid to estate);
- (d) Age 70+ mandatory retirement conversion to customer-only membership (worker-members may retain shares but voting rights transfer).

**2.6.2 Payout Amount**
The departing member receives:
- Full par value of base shares ($1 each, no depreciation);
- Any **unpaid patronage returns** credited to their account;
- A pro-rata share of the **Redemption Reserve** (Tiers 2–3 only) if the reserve exceeds 10% of equity (excess distributed equally among departing members that year).

Not included: Profit-sharing from future periods, investor-style capital gains, or voting rights after exit.

**2.6.3 Payout Schedule (Tiered)**

| Tier | Payout Speed | Interest on Delay | Notes |
|------|--------------|-------------------|-------|
| Tier 1 (Micro) | Lump sum within 90 days | 0% | Small amounts (<$5k typical) |
| Tier 2 (Mid) | 20% upfront, balance over 3 years | 3% simple interest | Preserves cash flow |
| Tier 3 (Base + Heavy Industry) | 10% upfront, balance over 5 years | 5% simple interest | Large co-ops can amortize |

**2.6.4 Termination for Cause (Firing)**
A worker-member who leaves the FCL for any reason — including voluntary resignation, retirement, layoff, or termination for cause — is entitled to the full redemption value of their base shares and accumulated patronage equity under the payout schedule in Section 2.6.3.

If the worker is terminated for cause involving financial loss to the FCL (theft, embezzlement, fraud), the FCL may seek damages through the Co-op Department arbitration process. Damages awarded may be offset against the redemption payment, but no offset may reduce the payment below 50% of the total accrued equity without a court order.

**2.6.5 Relation to Investor Exits**
Member redemption under this section is **senior** to investor exits under Section 2.5. If an FCL lacks liquidity, member redemptions are paid before any Layer 2 or Layer 3 investor sale is approved.

## Section 3: Financial Operations

### 3.1 Patronage Returns

- **Eligibility**: Customers buy $1 base share ($50/year for co-op clubs) for 2–8% returns on FCL purchases (e.g., groceries, recreation). Patronage returns apply to consumer-facing FCLs (retail, recreation, agriculture, energy, manufacturing). **For Tier 1 Micro-Co-ops, patronage is voluntary. For Tiers 2 and 3, patronage is required per this section.** Professional FCLs may opt out of patronage returns and instead pass tax savings to clients via reduced rates, or offer patronage only to retainer client share holders as described in Section 2.3.
- **Tiers** (inflation-adjusted):

| Tier | Spending Range | Return Rate | Return (2025) | Adjusted Rate (2035/2045) |
|------|----------------|-------------|---------------|---------------------------|
| 1 | $1,000–$5,000 | 2% | $20–$100 | 2.01% (2045) |
| 2 | $5,001–$10,000 | 5% | $250–$500 | 5.1% (2035) |
| 3 | >$10,000 | 8% | $800+ | 8.16% (2035) |

- **Allocation**: 5–10% net profit, 50% cash/50% BWC credits, $862.5 million/year. Example: $6,000 spending earns $300 (5%), with $270 refunded ($135 cash, $135 credits).
- **Applicability**: Standard for retail and co-op clubs, optional for manufacturing/professional FCLs with 75% board approval.

### 3.2 Profit-Sharing

- **Process**:
  - Deduct expenses (e.g., wages, utilities).
  - Pay **5% co-op profit tax on profit over $100,000** (per Monetary Act Section 4).
  - Allocate 5–10% for patronage.
  - Allocate 10–25% for capital investments.
  - Allocate 0–15% to the Co-operative Capital Investment Fund (CCIF) — **entirely voluntary** (see Section 3.8 for details).
  - Split remaining profit as follows (boards may adjust ±5% with 75% approval):

| Allocation | Required % of Remaining Profit |
|------------|-------------------------------|
| Healthcare Contribution | 10% |
| Education Contribution | 5% |
| Charity/Social Good | 10% |
| Members/Employees | 40% |
| Investors/Founders | 35% |

- **Distribution**:
  - **Standard FCLs**: 70% to workers/customers, 30% to investors.
  - **Professional FCLs**: 100% to worker-partners; investors get non-equity profit-sharing (10–20%) or loan interest (5–6% DAI-adjusted). Under the cost-share model, no firm-level profit exists; each member keeps their own surplus after expenses.
  - **Tier 1 Micro-Co-ops**: Profit-sharing is voluntary. Founders may retain profits for reinvestment.
- **Co-op Clubs**: Profits follow the same split; excess flows to member dividends or dues reduction. System-wide co-op club profits reach $40 billion/year by 2075.
- **Governance**: Profit allocations approved by FCL boards (51% majority, 70% member/30% investor voting). Tier 1 Micro-Co-ops have no board requirement; founder approval suffices.

### 3.3 Funding

- **Loans**: $294.125 billion ($14.70625 billion/region) from credit unions per Credit Union Act:
  - Business Loans: $50,000–$5 million, 5–6% nominal (DAI-adjusted to ~2.4–3.4% effective with 1.0% floor).
  - Energy FCLs: $12.65 billion/district.
- **Capital Investments**: $50,000–$5 million (standard FCLs), non-equity loans ($100,000–$1 million, professional FCLs), 10–20% profit reinvestment.
- **Revenue Context**: Total government revenue per Monetary Act 8.0 is $497.5 billion (excluding co-op SWF recharge). FCLs contribute via the 5% co-op profit tax.

### 3.4 Corporate Structure

- **Role**: Corporations drive 15% GDP ($2.175 trillion), scaling to $5.841 trillion by 2075, in technology, manufacturing, energy.
- **Structure**:
  - Shareholder-owned, equity shares via Crossroads Global Co-op Index ($329 billion/year, $1 trillion cap).
  - Corporate boards appoint CEOs, overseen by Trade & Corporate Department.
  - Dividends: 3–5%.
  - **Taxes per Monetary Act 8.0**: Solo corporations: 0% under $100,000 profit, 5% $100k–$500k, 15% over $500k. Non-solo corporations: 0% under $100,000 profit, 15% $100k–$1M, 20% over $1M. Total corporate tax revenue: ~$25 billion/year.
- **Loans**: $67.875 billion ($3.39375 billion/region) from credit unions.

### 3.5 Master/Grandmaster Bonuses

- **Masters**: 3+ years post-service, 5+ apprentices, $100,000 revenue, 2% per-share bonus ($20/year for $1,000). **Apprenticeships primarily occur in Tier 1 Micro-Co-ops.**
- **Grandmasters**: 10+ apprentices, 80% retention, $1.5 million contribution, 4% per-share bonus ($40/year). **Journeyman status typically achieved in Tier 2 Mid-Size Co-ops. Master and Grandmaster status reserved for Tier 3 Full FCLs.**

### 3.6 Tiered Funding Access

**3.6.1 Tier 1 (Micro)**
- Access to microloans: $1,000–$50,000 via Regional Co-op Stabilization Fund (0–2% interest, unsecured, DAI-adjusted with 0.5% floor).
- No CCIF contribution required (fully exempt).
- Eligible for informal sector transition grants ($5,000–$25,000, match required).

**3.6.2 Tier 2 (Mid)**
- Access to business loans: $50,000–$2 million via credit unions (5–6% nominal, DAI-adjusted ~2.4–3.4% effective with 1.0% floor) or Regional Co-op Stabilization Fund (3–4%).
- CCIF: Fully optional. May deposit unspent capital allocations.
- Eligible for Redemption Reserve matching grants (up to $50,000/year from Regional Board).

**3.6.3 Tier 3 (Base + Heavy Industry)**
- Access to full loan range: $50,000–$5 million via credit unions (5–6% nominal, DAI-adjusted). Heavy Industry FCLs may access project finance up to $2 billion via CCIF or private placement.
- CCIF: Fully optional. May deposit unspent capital allocations.
- Eligible to list on Co-op Capital Index (Section 2.5.5).
- May issue co-op bonds (up to 200% of equity) with Co-op Department approval.

### 3.7 Co-op/Corporate Joint Ventures (CCJV)

**3.7.1 Authorization**
FCLs may enter into Joint Ventures with corporations registered under Section 3.4 for specific projects, technology licensing, or time-limited production arrangements. CCJVs are separate legal entities from both parent organizations.

**3.7.2 Ownership Tiers**

| CCJV Type | Co-op Ownership | Corporate Ownership | Max Term | Approval Body |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------------|----------|---------------|
| Co-op Led | 51–99% | 1–49% | 20 years | Regional Board |
| Corporate Led | 30–49% | 51–70% | 15 years | Central Council |
| Strategic Infrastructure | 40% (min) | 60% (max) | 25 years | Central Council (75%) |

**3.7.3 Governance Protections**
- Corporate partners have **no voting rights** in FCL internal affairs outside the JV.
- FCL worker-members in a CCJV retain full base share rights in their home FCL.
- JV board seats proportional to ownership; co-op must hold at least one seat.
- Deadlocked disputes resolved by Co-op Department arbitration.

**3.7.4 Financial Rules**
- JV profits taxed at corporate rate (Section 3.4) unless co-op-owned portion >50%, then co-op tax rate (5% on profit over $100,000) applies to that portion.
- Patronage returns apply only to co-op-owned share of JV profits.
- JV may opt into CCIF deposits if co-op-owned portion >50% (voluntary, requires board vote).

**3.7.5 Exit and Buyout**
- Upon JV dissolution, assets distributed by ownership percentage.
- Co-op has **right of first refusal** to buy corporate partner's share at appraised value.
- Corporate partner may sell to another corporation only with co-op's written consent.
- If co-op declines purchase, Regional Board may approve alternative corporate buyer.

**3.7.6 Prohibitions**
- CCJVs may not be used to circumvent investor voting caps (Section 2.3).
- CCJVs may not hold base shares in other FCLs.
- No CCJV may exist for more than 25 years without Central Council renewal.

### 3.8 Co-operative Capital Investment Fund (CCIF)

**3.8.1 Nature and Purpose**
The Co-operative Capital Investment Fund (CCIF) is a **voluntary liquidity pool** that enables FCLs and corporate participants to deposit unspent capital allocations, earn returns (5–7%), and fund national-scale projects aligned with the Sovereign Wealth Fund (SWF). The CCIF is **not a tax or mandatory contribution** — participation is entirely optional.

**3.8.2 Voluntary Participation**
- **Tier 1 Micro-Co-ops:** Fully exempt. May not participate.
- **Tiers 2 and 3:** Fully optional. May deposit any portion of unspent capital allocation at any time.
- **Corporations:** May participate subject to approval by Co-op Department and Central Council.

**3.8.3 Projected Scale**
Through voluntary participation, the CCIF is projected to reach:

| Source | Projected Deposit Balance |
|--------|---------------------------|
| FCLs (Tiers 2–3) | $1.96 trillion |
| Corporate participants | $435 billion |
| **Total system-wide** | **$2.395 trillion** |

These projections assume 5–7% annual returns and typical capital allocation patterns. Actual balances depend on voluntary participation.

**3.8.4 Returns**
- Depositing FCLs earn **5–7% annual returns** on their CCIF balance.
- Returns are paid in BWC (50% cash, 50% BWC credits by default; FCLs may elect 100% cash with 30-day notice).
- Interest accrues daily, paid quarterly.

**3.8.5 Withdrawals**
- Deposits may be withdrawn at any time with **30 days' notice** for amounts up to 20% of an FCL's total deposit.
- Larger withdrawals (over 20%) require **90 days' notice** to preserve pool liquidity.
- No penalties for withdrawal. No lock-up periods.

**3.8.6 Use of Funds**
CCIF funds enable **$50 billion/year in national SWF-linked projects** including:
- Greenhouses ($1 billion/year)
- Recycling hubs ($500 million/year)
- CO2 pipelines ($500 million/year)
- Renewables, housing, eco-zones ($47.5 billion/year)

Project selection follows the SWF framework under the Monetary Act, with CCIF receiving proportional returns from completed projects.

**3.8.7 Governance**
- Operated by the **CCIF Alliance Network**, an independent body under Co-op Department oversight.
- Annual audits by CWD.
- Depositing FCLs elect 5 of 9 CCIF board seats proportionally (1 vote per $1 million deposited, capped at 10% of total voting power).

**3.8.8 Relation to Other Provisions**
- CCIF deposits are **separate from** Redemption Reserve (Section 2.5.2) and may not be encumbered for member exits.
- CCIF may act as liquidity backstop for Co-op Capital Index (Section 2.5.5).

## Section 4: Governance and Oversight

- **FCL Boards** (Tier 3 Full FCLs only; Tiers 1–2 follow Section 2.4):
  - **Composition**: 5–7 members (7–11 for Heavy Industry Clause FCLs), elected by workers/customers (70% voting power, or 51% minimum for Heavy Industry) and investors (30% voting power, up to 15% per investor for Heavy Industry), 3-year terms. Includes co-op clubs.
  - **Board Structure**: 4 member representatives (2 workers, 2 customers), 1–2 investor representatives (Heavy Industry: minimum 5 worker/customer seats).
  - **Salaries**: Small (<$5 million): $10,000–$25,000/year; Medium ($5–50 million): $25,000–$50,000/year; Large (>$50 million): $50,000–$75,000/year.
  - **Role**: Manage operations, profit allocation, patronage, ownership ratios, **decide CCIF participation (voluntary)**. Decisions require 51% majority.
- **Regional Boards**:
  - **Composition**: 20 boards, 11 members each (10 masters, 1 wildcard, 220 total), elected by ~4.7 million voters/region (94 million total, 12+ with base/investor shares), 5-year terms (masters), 1-year terms (wildcards).
  - **Salaries**: $50,000–$75,000/year, $11–$16.5 million/year total.
  - **Role**: Approve loans, manage $500 million/region Co-op Stabilization Fund, oversee FCL compliance, verify investor eligibility, **approve tier upgrade/downgrade requests**, **approve Heavy Industry Clause applications**, **oversee CCJVs**.
- **Oversight**:
  - **Co-op Department**: Approves professional FCLs, arbitrates disputes, enforces investor voting caps via blockchain/AI audits, **reviews Heavy Industry Clause renewals**, **oversees CCIF Alliance Network**.
  - **Central Council**: Aligns system-wide policies, **approves Heavy Industry Clause (final step)**, **approves Corporate Led CCJVs**.
  - **Audits**: 50–75 auditors audit 5% of FCLs biannually, capping $2.5 billion fraud via $1 billion/year blockchain/AI, aligned with Parks and Monetary Acts. **Tier 1 Micro-Co-ops are exempt from biannual audits unless flagged for irregularities.**

## Section 5: Sustainability and Regional Integration

## Design Philosophy (Informative)

The FCL Structure Act is the productive engine of the New Crossroads economy. Where the Monetary Act provides the currency and the Credit Union Act provides the financial plumbing, the FCL Act provides the businesses that citizens own and operate together.

- **Tiers are not hierarchies — they are ladders.** A micro-co-op in Tier 1 can grow to Tier 2 without a governance cliff. A Tier 3 co-op can downsize without penalty. The system is designed for real businesses, not theoretical ideals.

- **Voluntary CCIF, not mandatory extraction.** Unlike the old system where co-ops paid 2% of GDP into the SWF, the CCIF is a voluntary liquidity pool. FCLs deposit when it suits them, withdraw when they need capital, and earn market returns. This is cooperation, not taxation.

- **The 5% profit tax (over $100k) keeps co-ops advantaged.** Corporations pay 15–20%. Co-ops pay 5%. That gap is intentional. It rewards cooperative ownership and keeps wealth distributed.

- **The 0.5% DAI floor for Tier 1 micro-co-ops recognizes their service-sector role.** Many micro-co-ops emerge from the informal economy. They face higher volatility and thinner margins. A lower DAI floor (matching healthcare, education, and infrastructure) keeps credit affordable for the smallest enterprises.

- **Heavy Industry Clause balances scale and democracy.** A nuclear plant or auto factory cannot be financed with $5 million investor caps. The clause permits larger investor participation (49% ownership, 15% voting) while preserving member control (51% minimum, 5 worker/customer board seats). Community Benefit and Labor Peace Agreements ensure that scale does not come at the cost of co-op values.

- **The Co-op Capital Index provides liquidity without exit.** Non-voting investor shares can trade. Worker base shares cannot. This preserves the cooperative core while giving investors a path to realize value.

FCLs are not corporations with a co-op sticker. They are member-owned, member-governed, and member-benefiting. The tax code favors them. The credit unions serve them. The monetary system stabilizes their borrowing costs through DAI. And the informal economy feeds into Tier 1 as startups grow into Tier 2 and Tier 3.

That is the FCL Act.
