## The Inheritance

**What the revolutionaries walk into on Day 1:**

A socialist island nation of 112 million people, near Point Nemo, with:

- **Sovereign debt at ~120% of GDP** — recently defaulted. The old currency is collapsing. Foreign creditors are furious but distant.
- **State ownership of everything** — every mine, every factory, every farm, every power plant, every apartment block, every port, every rail line. All of it in government hands, most of it poorly maintained, some of it genuinely good (the nuclear plants, the mines, the rail backbone).
- **A massive informal economy** — perhaps 25–30% of real economic activity operating as a black market. The state has spent decades trying to suppress it and failing. These are the people who actually keep the country fed and functioning.
- **A disarmed, surveilled, censored population** — state media, no private firearms, informant networks in the markets, housing assigned by committee. The population trusts nobody with power, and they're right not to.
- **Deep resource endowments** — the Frostpeak's uranium, coal, gold, silver, copper, and industrial metals. The Heartland's agricultural capacity. The Ember Range's geothermal. ~400 TWh of nuclear and hydro generation capacity that still works.
- **Strategic isolation** — no land neighbours, nearest continent thousands of kilometres away. The default will not trigger a military invasion. It will trigger a trade-credit squeeze.
- **Legacy infrastructure that mostly works** — 56 GW of nuclear (aging but operational), a rail backbone, ports, a road network, a power grid. None of it CME-hardened, none of it optimised, but none of it rubble. This is not a war zone — it's a bureaucratic collapse.

**What they don't have:** foreign reserves, international credit, a working currency, a tax collection apparatus, a legal framework for private ownership, or any reason for the population to trust them.

## Year 0: The Revolution (2025)

### Week 1 — The Constitutional Moment

**The constitutional convention sits and produces the founding acts.** This is not improvised — the revolutionaries have been planning this for years, probably decades, in exile or underground. The seventeen acts are ready. The design philosophy — discretion corrupts, replace with published rules — exists before the revolution, not after it.

**Day 1 announcements, in order of psychological impact:**

1. **The right to bear arms, including concealed carry.** The old regime disarmed you. We just armed you. This is the single loudest signal that the new government is not the old one. *(Exceptions: courthouses, airports, government buildings — named and narrow.)*
2. **Near-absolute press freedom and free speech.** The old regime ran every newspaper and every broadcast. As of now, anyone can publish anything. Local AM radio stations begin forming immediately.
3. **Your home is yours — untaxable, at every level, forever.** Entrenched as Floor 2. The old regime assigned housing and could reassign it. That is over.
4. **Your business is legal.** Every black-market operation in the country is legitimate as of this morning. No registration required below $100,000. Cash commerce constitutionally protected. Nobody is coming to inspect, audit, or prosecute.
5. **The sovereign default is confirmed and the old currency is retired.** The debt belongs to the regime that incurred it. The new state does not recognise it as its own. **The new state will never borrow, at any level, and has constitutionally barred itself from doing so.**

**What the population experiences on Day 1:** they wake up armed, free, in a home nobody can take or tax, with a business nobody can shut down, under a government that just banned itself from borrowing. Every one of those is the *exact opposite* of the old regime. The message is not subtle.

### Month 1 — The BWC and the Tax

**The BWC launches on conventional paper stock.** Emergency issue — secure enough, plain, functional. Distributed through the post office, the old state banks (temporarily repurposed), and the 5,000 credit union charters that begin forming immediately. The exchange rate to the old currency is set by a one-time conversion — generous to small holders, haircut on large ones.

**The tax system goes live — and it is shockingly simple:**

- **6% VAT on consumption. The Daily Living Basket is zero-rated.** Collected at point of sale. No filing, no returns, no tax office visits.
- **No income tax below $100,000.** For the vast majority of the population — and the entirety of the informal economy — there is no direct tax relationship with the state whatsoever.
- **No residential property tax. No business registration tax. No payroll tax.**
- **Tariffs at the border** — the ports collect these; they don't require domestic compliance infrastructure.

The tax system asks almost nothing of the population and raises ~$460B because it taxes *transactions* not *people*. The informal economy participates automatically through the VAT and receives more back through the dividend than it pays.

### Months 1–6 — The Great Transfer

**State-owned enterprises begin converting to co-ops.** This is the core economic event of the revolution and it takes months, not days.

**The mechanism:**

- Every state enterprise is chartered as an FCL. The workers become members — one share, one vote. Management is elected from within or recruited.
- The enterprise's assets (plant, equipment, inventory) are valued at **post-collapse prices** — fire-sale, not book value. The old regime's book values are fiction. What matters is what the assets produce *now*.
- Workers receive their membership interest for **labour equity** — years of service under the old regime convert to founding capital. Nobody pays cash to join a co-op they've been running for twenty years.
- The **5,000 credit unions** begin forming simultaneously (Credit Union Act). Each takes the deposits of the old state savings system — the one thing the population had that was real money — and recapitalises them as member-owned institutions.
- **The CLS corps (~40,000 founding agents)** begin standing up, embedded in the credit unions, processing co-op formations, loan applications, and the first wave of the $50 BWC age-20 wallets.

**What is NOT privatised:**

- The grid (state-owned, operated by the NEB — Energy Act)
- The rail and freeway network (state-owned, operated by the NInfraB — Infrastructure Act)
- The buried fiber backbone (state-owned, operated by the NCB — Communications Act)
- The AM emergency broadcast system (state-owned — Communications Act)
- The parkweb (state-owned — Parks Act)
- The ports (state-owned, co-op-operated — Infrastructure Act)

These remain public infrastructure. The state *owns* the rails; co-ops *run trains on them* and pay access fees. The state *owns* the grid; co-ops *generate power* and pay transmission fees. The 155% fee model begins from day one on legacy infrastructure — it doesn't wait for the new builds.

### Months 3–6 — The Boards Stand Up

**The seven sector boards** are nominated by the Territorial Chamber and confirmed by both Productive Chambers (Chamber Act §7.1). In the first months, the chambers themselves are being elected, so the boards are initially **appointed by the constitutional convention with a sunset** — they must be re-confirmed by the elected chambers within 18 months or they dissolve.

| Board | Stands up | First task |
|---|---|---|
| NEB (Energy) | Month 2 | Assess the legacy nuclear fleet; begin grid-hardening planning |
| NInfraB (Infrastructure) | Month 2 | Inventory the rail and road network; set founding toll/access-fee rates |
| NCB (Communications) | Month 3 | Declare the fiber backbone state-owned; assess CME vulnerability |
| NIB (Industry) | Month 3 | Begin processing co-op conversions for the industrial sector |
| NHA (Housing) | Month 4 | Launch the Durahome programme; establish the forced-savings accounts |
| NHB (Healthcare) | Month 4 | Convert the old state hospitals to the co-op premium model; stand up the bottom-tier subsidy |
| NLC (Education) | Month 5 | Launch the voucher system for the next school year |

### Month 6 — The SWF Is Seeded

**The Sovereign Wealth Fund receives its ~$230B seed.** Sources:

- **Freed debt service.** The old regime was paying ~$300–400B/year servicing its debt. That line is now zero. Even a fraction of one year's freed service exceeds the seed.
- **Asset-sale proceeds.** The co-op conversions are not giveaways — labour equity covers the workers' share, but the residual enterprise value flows to the state as founding capital.
- **Foreign reserves.** Whatever the old central bank held — gold, foreign currency, SDRs — transfers to the SWF.
- **First resource royalties.** The Frostpeak mines never stopped producing. At 12% on even half the founding resource base, royalties are flowing from Month 1.

**The SWF is seeded once, and then it grows from contributions (~$138B/year).** It does not receive another injection. The seed is the revolution's one-time fiscal event; everything after is the system running on its own.

## Year 1: The System Boots (2026)

**What's running:**
- The BWC is circulating. Paper stock, functional.
- VAT is collecting. The $460B revenue base is building — it won't hit the full annual figure until the formal economy stabilises, but the informal economy's VAT is flowing from Day 1.
- ~80% of state enterprises have converted to co-ops. The remainder are complex (mining, heavy industry) and take longer.
- Credit unions are operational in all 20 regions. Deposits are growing.
- The SWF is accumulating. ~$230B seed + ~$138B first-year contributions = ~$368B by year-end.
- The seven sector boards are functioning under convention appointment, awaiting chamber confirmation.
- The first elected chambers are seated. The Territorial Chamber (20 Regional Boards) forms first; the two Productive Chambers follow as co-ops and corporations register.

**What's NOT running yet:**
- The polymer currency facility (commissioned, not built).
- The CME-hardened grid (planning phase — Energy Act Phase 1 runs 2025–2035).
- The freeway buildout (planning/surveying — Infrastructure Act Phase 1).
- The full CLS corps (at ~40,000 of an eventual ~127,000; scaling with co-op formation).
- The citizen dividend (SWF below cap; no payout yet).
- The CCIF (co-ops need to accumulate profit before they can deposit into it).
- The metals streaming pipeline (contracts being negotiated with the newly formed mining co-ops).

**What citizens experience in Year 1:**
- Their home is theirs. Nobody has challenged it.
- Their business is running legally, probably doing better than under the old regime because the suppression risk is gone.
- They're paying 4% on non-essential purchases and nothing else.
- The credit union gave them an account and their old savings transferred.
- Their kids are in school on a voucher.
- Healthcare premiums have replaced the old state system — they're paying $200–600/month depending on tier, but the bottom 20% is subsidised.
- The AM radio has local stations for the first time. The press is chaotic and free.
- They can buy a gun.

**What they're still waiting for:** the dividend, the beautiful currency, and proof that all of this holds.

## Year 2: Credibility (2027)

**The trade-credit squeeze is real but survivable.**

Foreign suppliers want payment on delivery, not net-30. Import costs are elevated. Some goods are scarce. But:

- The nuclear fleet generates the nation's own electricity. No energy imports needed.
- The Heartland feeds the nation. No food crisis.
- The Frostpeak exports uranium, metals, and coal — counterparties want these badly enough to trade, default or no default.
- The merchant marine is operating. Ports are functioning. The physical trade infrastructure survived the revolution intact.

**The squeeze hurts at the margins** — consumer goods variety drops, some industrial inputs are delayed, prices on imported items spike. But nobody is hungry, nobody is cold, and the lights are on. The isolation that makes the squeeze possible also limits what the squeeze can take — because the nation doesn't depend on imports for anything existential.

**Institutional milestones:**

- **Chambers confirm all seven sector boards** — the convention appointments become permanent. The constitutional machinery is now self-sustaining; no transitional authority remains.
- **The Exchange opens.** Co-op Capital Index shares begin trading. The Metals ETF launches with its founding allocation — every adult citizen holds shares.
- **The metals streaming pipeline goes live.** The first contracts with Frostpeak mining co-ops are signed. Metal begins flowing to the ETF vault.
- **The Special Auditors complete their first investigations.** The new system's enforcement arm has teeth, and the population sees it used — probably against a few former-regime officials who tried to pocket assets during the Great Transfer.
- **First polymer notes (copper flecks) enter limited circulation.** Small denominations, regional pilot, but the public sees them. The notes are as beautiful as promised.

**SWF at year-end:** ~$659B. Still below the ~$749B cap. No dividend yet.

## Year 3: The Dividend Approaches (2028)

**The SWF crosses the 12% cap around mid-year.**

The §2.3 stabilisers fire automatically. Contributions above the cap begin redirecting to general revenue, and the sovereign surplus — which the state constitutionally may not accumulate — flows to the citizen dividend.

**The first dividend payments go out.** ~$1,380 per citizen per year, paid monthly or quarterly, in cash, no filing required. For a household of four that's ~$5,520/year — real money in a $53,600 per-capita economy.

**This is the moment the revolution becomes irreversible.** Not because the dividend is large, but because 112 million people just received money *from* a government for the first time in their lives. The old regime took. This one gave. And it did it without asking for a form, a registration, an account, or a declaration.

The informal economy — 25–30% of the working population — receives the dividend and pays less back in VAT than it gets. **These are the revolution's most important constituents**, and the system was designed to reach them first.

**Other milestones:**

- **Full polymer currency circulation.** Silver and gold denominations join copper. Paper-to-polymer swap underway — one for one, no haircut, no deadline panic. The queues are the revolution made visible.
- **The polymer facility is itself a co-op.** ~2,000 workers in a precision manufacturing operation near the Frostpeak, producing the most beautiful currency in circulation anywhere, with constitutional job security (the nation will always need banknotes).
- **Trade-credit squeeze easing.** Two years of reliable commodity exports, settled on delivery, have rebuilt a minimal trade reputation. Terms are improving — not to pre-default levels, but functional. The nation doesn't need bond-market access because it doesn't borrow, so the remaining reputational overhang has nowhere to attach.
- **Housing Act Durahome programme reaching scale.** ~50,000 homes/year, $180,000 each, co-op-built, geothermal-heated. The housing stock is physically transforming.
- **Infrastructure Phase 1 underway.** Rail expansion, freeway surveying, grid-hardening contracts let. The ~$20B/year infrastructure line in general revenue is funding it.

## Year 4: Steady State Begins (2029)

**The SWF is capped.** Contributions redirect to general revenue and onward to the dividend automatically. The fund's job narrows to its three permanent functions: crisis liquidity, revolving lending (industry and housing construction), and the Defense Fund.

**The fiscal architecture is running unsupervised:**

| Component | Status |
|---|---|
| Revenue (~$493B) | Collecting — VAT 6%, income, profit taxes, tariffs, resources, property, excise |
| Spending (~$424.5B) | Disbursing — vouchers, subsidies, infrastructure, military, parks, R&D, four functions, mandatory-service stipends |
| SWF (~$764B, capped) | Lending and backstopping — not spending |
| Citizen dividend (~$155B/yr) | Paying — $1,380/citizen, cash, universal |
| Commons (~$48B) | Flowing — healthcare $19B, education $10B, charity $19B |
| Healthcare (~$448B) | Self-funding on premiums + commons + subsidy |
| Credit-union network | Self-funding on NIM (~3%) |

**Nobody is running this.** The sovereign operates at 3.3% of GDP. The chambers oversee. The boards regulate their sectors. The Special Auditors investigate. The formulas publish rates. The stabilisers fire or don't. There is no economic tsar, no planning committee, no five-year plan. The system runs because the rules run.

**The CLS corps is scaling toward its mature ~127,000.** Co-op formation is accelerating as the formal economy grows around a functioning credit system. The informal economy holds its share — not because people can't formalise, but because many choose not to. The system lets them.

## Year 5: The Revolution Is Over (2030)

**Five years in, every mechanism the founding acts describe is operational:**

- 17 acts, functioning.
- 7 sector boards, confirmed and independent.
- 3 chambers, elected and legislating.
- ~$780B SWF, capped, paying dividends.
- ~$1,380/citizen/year flowing to every citizen including the informal majority.
- Polymer BWC with metallic flecks in every wallet.
- Metals ETF growing, shares trading on the Exchange.
- 50,000 Durahomes/year under construction.
- Grid hardening underway.
- Rail expanding.
- Freeway buildout beginning.
- AM emergency network operational.
- Parkweb intact, Dark Corridor Fund collecting.
- Trade-credit squeeze functionally over.
- No sovereign debt. No sovereign debt instruments. No bond market. No credit rating. No need for one.

**The transition is complete. Phase 1 (2025–2035) continues — the infrastructure buildout, the energy expansion, the industrial scaling — but those are growth, not recovery.** The constitutional machinery is self-sustaining. The fiscal model balances. The population is armed, free, housed, employed, insured, and receiving a dividend from a fund that runs on rules they can read.

**What remains to build is the future, not the foundation.**
