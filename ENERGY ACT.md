## Purpose

The New Crossroads Energy Act establishes a **CME-resilient, fully electrified energy framework** for 112 million citizens (2025), scaling to 130 million and a ~$19T GDP by 2075. It delivers the **highest per-capita electricity generation of any major nation** (~21.6 MWh/capita by 2075), powered by a nuclear-and-geothermal backbone with no fossil transport fuel.

The system is built on the asset/operator separation model:
- **State owns:** Transmission lines, distribution lines, substations, transformer reserve, antifragile hub SMRs, the repository SMR, hydro dams, WTE plants, and geo-coal/geo-WTE plants (sovereign infrastructure assets)
- **Co-ops and corporations compete on:** Non-hub nuclear generation, standalone geothermal, renewables, and retail electricity sales
- **Co-op concessions maintain:** All state-owned grid assets under performance concessions (Utilities Act §1.4), measured against the Asset Condition Index
- **NEB oversees:** Standards, licensing, concession tendering, fee-setting, and CME response. **The NEB does not perform hands-on maintenance** — that is contracted out to co-op concessionaires (Utilities Act §1.4)

The grid is hardened against CME over a **70-year phased program (2025–2095)**, built outward from the 21 antifragile hubs — the survivable core first, the last rural mile last. Generation reaches full capacity by 2075; grid hardening completes by 2095.

Managed by the **National Energy Board (NEB)** under Sovereign Council oversight. No binding sector quotas apply. Co-op generation receives license priority and fee discounts.

Grid construction is funded by **general revenue** (Sovereign Functions Act §7.3). State-owned generation assets (hub SMRs, repository SMR, hydro, WTE, geo-coal, geo-WTE) are sovereign infrastructure. The NEB **owns and sets standards**; hands-on maintenance and operations are contracted to co-op concessions or licensed to corporations.

**Why WTE, geo-coal, and geo-WTE are state-owned.** These plants handle waste streams and fossil fuels — environmental liabilities that belong with the sovereign. The state owns the dirty plant; a corporation runs it under license and pays access fees. The state captures the liability; the corporation captures the operational efficiency. Same logic as the grid: the state owns what must be hardened and regulated; the market operates what must be efficient.

### 3.4 Hydro — Limited by Design

New Crossroads is not a nation of dams. Hydro is constrained to **18 GW** across two categories:

- **2 major dams on the Westflow River** (~8 GW combined): large-scale impoundment dams, existing at founding, providing baseload power and water storage
- **10 wind-sited reservoir dams on Corridon tributaries** (~10 GW combined): approved only where the prevailing-wind-and-catchment geometry demonstrates that evaporated reservoir moisture returns to the glacial catchment as snowfall (Parks Act §4.3). One dam yields three returns — power, storage, and glacial recharge — with no net evaporation loss

No other rivers are dammed. The Corridon mainstem flows freely — flooding its floodplains, nourishing its wetlands, and recharging the system (Parks Act §2.4). The minimum environmental flow (Parks Act §2.5) protects in-stream ecology: in a drought year, withdrawals are curtailed before the protected flow is breached.

### 4.2 Phased Hardening — Hubs First, Last Mile Last

The grid is hardened over **70 years** in three concentric phases, prioritized by criticality. At any point in the program, a CME destroys only what has not yet been buried — and the hub-first phasing ensures the nation survives from year one.

| Phase | Years | Scope | Investment | CME resilience at phase end |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **1: The Survivable Core** | 2025–2040 | 21 hub SMRs and their trunk transmission to each other and to the nearest substations; EMP shielding on hub-connected substations; the repository SMR. Core buried transmission (20,000 km) | ~$140B | A CME hits and the hubs island: every hospital, airport, water plant stays lit. The rest of the grid goes dark but the nation survives |
| **2: The Regional Skeleton** | 2040–2065 | Bury the high-voltage backbone connecting all 21 hubs into a national loop; harden remaining substations; extend buried distribution to major cities and industrial corridors | ~$120B | A CME knocks out rural distribution but cities, corridors, and inter-hub links stay running |
| **3: The Last Mile** | 2065–2095 | Bury remaining rural distribution to full 150,000 km; complete transformer reserve (5,000+); full 750 substations hardened | ~$62B | Grid is fully CME-proof to the last farmhouse |

**Why 70 years, not 50.** Burying 150,000 km of distribution line in 50 years is ~3,000 km/year — an aggressive sustained rate competing for trenching crews, cable, and conduit during the decades when the nation is also building hubs, reactors, housing, and rail. At 70 years the rate drops to ~2,140 km/year, matching labor supply and avoiding boom-bust construction cycles. The mandatory-service apprentice pipeline (Education Act §4) provides a steady flow of trained electrical and construction labor for three generations rather than a surge that runs out of work.

| Event | Action | Timeline | Responsible |

## Section 4A: Nuclear Fuel Cycle — Reprocessing, Repository, and Stewardship

*Cross-reference: Military Act §8 (Nuclear Directorate), Sovereign Functions Act §7.3 (sovereign obligations), Parks Act §4.3 (Frostpeak siting).*

### 4A.1 The Circular Fuel Cycle

New Crossroads operates 170 GW of civilian SMRs and 24 nuclear submarine reactors, all fuelled by domestically mined Frostpeak uranium. The fuel cycle is **circular, not linear** — spent fuel is reprocessed to recover ~96% of its material as reusable fuel, with only ~4% going to permanent storage.

| Stage | What happens | Output |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Mining** | Uranium ore from the Frostpeak | Yellowcake |
| **Enrichment** | Low-enriched (civilian, <5%) and highly enriched (naval, >90%) — Military Act §8.1 hard wall | Fuel-grade uranium |
| **Fabrication** | Fuel rods (civilian) and reactor cores (naval) | Ready fuel |
| **Burn** | 3–5 years in a reactor (civilian) or 10+ years (naval) | Spent fuel |
| **Reprocessing** | ~95% uranium + ~1% plutonium recovered; vitrified into MOX fuel | Reusable fuel + 4% high-level waste |
| **Re-burn** | MOX fuel returns to the reactor (3–4 reprocessing cycles possible) | Spent MOX |
| **Repository** | The 4% that cannot be recycled — vitrified in borosilicate glass, copper-overpacked, emplaced in the geological repository | Permanent storage |

### 4A.2 The Repository SMR

A **dedicated 2 GW state-owned SMR** at the Frostpeak repository site provides grid-independent power for the reprocessing facility and the repository's triple-redundant pumping, monitoring, and ventilation systems. The repository requires continuous power for 50–75 years of operations — pumps that cannot lose power because a flooded repository with incompletely sealed waste packages is the one scenario the barrier design exists to prevent.

The repository SMR is fueled from ore mined next door, operated by the Nuclear Stewardship Consortium (§4A.7) under NEB oversight, and subject to the Military Act §8.1 hard wall for any weapons-grade material recovery at the co-located reprocessing facility.

### 4A.3 The Reprocessing Facility — Phased to Match the Grid

The national reprocessing complex, sited in the Frostpeak near the enrichment facility (Military Act §8.2), grows with the reactor fleet:

| Phase | Years | Capacity | Covers | Capital | Funded by |
|:---|:---|---:|:---|---:|:---|
| **1** | 2030–2040 | 1,000 t/yr | 58 GW hub + repository SMRs + 24 submarines | ~$18B | General revenue |
| **2** | 2045–2055 | 2,500 t/yr | ~130 GW (hubs + early competitive SMRs) | ~$14B | $5/MWh fuel-cycle surcharge |
| **3** | 2060–2070 | 4,000 t/yr | Full 170 GW + submarines | ~$12B | Surcharge (~$7.5B/yr at full grid) |
| **Total** | | **4,000 t/yr** | **170 GW + 24 subs** | **~$44B** | General revenue (Phase 1) → self-funding (Phases 2–3) |

**Total spent fuel at 170 GW:** ~3,060 tonnes/year (at ~18 t/GW for high-burnup SMRs) plus ~25 tonnes/year from submarines = ~3,085 t/yr. The 4,000 t/yr Phase 3 capacity provides ~30% margin.

**Dry-cask bridging.** Non-hub SMRs that come online before Phase 2 store spent fuel in on-site dry casks — safe for 50–100 years. Casks are retrieved when reprocessing capacity reaches them.

**Technology:** adapted from the French PUREX process. Each expansion is cheaper than greenfield (existing site, workforce, infrastructure, and the Consortium's distributed expertise).

**The $5/MWh fuel-cycle surcharge** is added to the access-fee model as a named line. At 170 GW it generates ~$7.5B/year, covering operating costs and building the Stewardship Endowment. The surcharge is ~4% of the average electricity rate, transparent and separate from base rates.

### 4A.4 The Geological Repository

When a Frostpeak uranium mine exhausts its ore body, it becomes the nation's permanent high-level waste repository. The mine's existing tunnels, known geology (20–30 years of data), ventilation infrastructure, and experienced workforce convert from extraction to storage.

| | |
|:---|:---|
| Conversion cost | ~$15B (vs ~$25–30B greenfield) |
| Engineered barriers | ~$7.1B — copper/steel overpacks (~12,000 packages), bentonite clay backfill, tunnel sealing, drainage galleries, redundant pump systems, groundwater diversion tunnels |
| Operational phase | 50–75 years (while waste is loaded) |
| Post-closure | Passive — the barriers contain without active intervention |

**Engineered barriers (designed to be safe wet):**

| Barrier | Function |
|:---|:---|
| **Borosilicate glass matrix** | Waste vitrified into glass. Dissolves at ~0.001 mm/year in groundwater. A 10 cm block lasts 100,000 years |
| **Copper overpack** | Each package sealed in thick copper. Corrodes at ~1 μm/year in anaerobic groundwater. A 5 cm wall lasts 50,000+ years |
| **Bentonite clay backfill** | Swells when wet — self-sealing. Water moves at millimetres per century |
| **Host rock** | Frostpeak crystalline rock, supplemented by groundwater diversion tunnels |

**Water management (operational phase, 50–75 years):**

| System | ~Annual cost |
|:---|---:|
| Active pumping (24/7, triple-redundant) | ~$50M |
| Monitoring (seismic, hydrological, thermal) | ~$20M |
| Ventilation & atmosphere management | ~$15M |
| **Total** | **~$85M/year** |

### 4A.6 Repository Legislation — Five Entrenched Provisions

**1. Anti-abandonment.** The repository is a **sovereign obligation equal in priority to the military and the judiciary** (Sovereign Functions Act §7.3). No organ of the state may abandon or defund it. The pumping, monitoring, and ventilation systems operate continuously. The repository SMR (§4A.2) provides grid-independent power.

**2. The Stewardship Endowment.** A dedicated perpetual fund — not the SWF, not the MMF — funded from the surcharge during operations. Target: ~$600M at 5% real return = ~$30M/year for post-closure monitoring in perpetuity. Entrenched — cannot be raided, redirected, or borrowed against.

**3. Closure Protocol.** Requires: all waste emplaced and barriers verified; Stewardship Endowment at or above the funded level; a 10-year pre-closure monitoring period; and **Territorial Chamber ratification (15/20)**.

**4. Perpetual Land Restriction.** Surface above the repository and a buffer zone permanently restricted. Marked with durable surface monuments and subsurface markers. Parks Act conservation-easement model applies.

**5. Knowledge Preservation.** Repository location, contents, and hazards recorded in multiple durable formats (granite markers, multilingual warnings, geological survey records, AM emergency broadcast permanent data store) — designed to survive institutional collapse.

### 4A.7 The Nuclear Stewardship Consortium

A consortium of **all Frostpeak uranium mining enterprises** — co-op and corporate alike. Membership mandatory for any enterprise holding a uranium mining charter.

| Element | Detail |
|:---|:---|
| Workforce | ~3,000–4,000 at any time, from a roster of ~8,000–10,000 across all members |
| Revenue split | Proportional to workforce contributed, rebalanced annually |
| Crew rotation | Every member rotates personnel — no single enterprise holds all institutional knowledge |
| Governance | Member-elected board, Productive Chamber oversight, Special Auditor enforcement |
| Survivor clause | If a member dissolves, remaining members inherit its share and personnel |

## Section 7: Governance

### 7.1 National Energy Board (NEB)

The NEB (11 members) is an **independent sector board** on the Chamber Act §7 registry. It **oversees and sets standards** — it does not perform hands-on maintenance, generation, or retail operations.

- **Composition:** 6 representatives from 20 Regional Energy Districts, 4 experts (Chamber Act §8 regional advisory layer), 1 chairman
- **Appointment:** Territorial Chamber nominates (11/20), both Productive Chambers confirm — the uniform rule for sector boards (Chamber Act §7.1)
- **Removal:** by the nominating and confirming bodies jointly, or on cause by a Special-Auditor investigation
- **Voting:** 6/11 operational; 8/11 regulations; strategic shifts require Territorial Chamber ratification (11/20)

**Functions:** Grid standards and the Asset Condition Index; generation and retail licensing; concession tendering and oversight (Utilities Act §1.4); Asset Renewal Fund administration (Utilities Act §2.4); transmission access-fee rates; CME response coordination; annual asset-condition and utility-performance reporting.

**The NEB does not:** maintain the grid (co-op concessions do, per Utilities Act §1.4), operate generation (utilities do, under license), or set retail prices (the market does, subject to the residential cap).

### 7.2 CLS Functions for Energy

~22,000 industry-focused CLS officers (demand-distributed per Credit Union Act §5.1) manage energy loans — solar/wind, geothermal, community solar micro-loans, and CME recovery loans. All reference-rate-anchored at the service-sector band.

### 8.2 Grid Hardening Capital

~$322B over 70 years = ~$4.6B/year from general revenue (inside the ~$20B infrastructure line).

### 8.3 Mandatory-Service Labor Savings

The mandatory-service apprenticeship program (Education Act §4) produces ~1M journeymen/year. Of these, an estimated **50,000–75,000 per year** work in energy-related trades during their 21-month service — electricians, welders, construction workers, cable-pullers, concrete crews, nuclear technicians. They are paid by general revenue (the mandatory-service stipend, ~$12–15k/yr); **employers pay nothing for their labor.**

**Value to the buildout:**

| Metric | Conservative | Moderate |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Apprentices/year in energy trades | 50,000 | 75,000 |
| Labor-years per apprentice (21 months) | 1.75 | 1.75 |
| Market-equivalent labor value | $30k/yr | $35k/yr |
| **Annual labor value to employers** | **~$2.6B** | **~$4.6B** |
| Over 70 years (extended buildout) | ~$184B | ~$322B |

**Competitive-bid pass-through.** In standard infrastructure procurement, labor is ~40% of civil-project cost. When a cost input drops uniformly across all bidders (every contractor has access to the same apprentice pool), competitive bidding forces ~80% of the savings to the buyer. The state sees an estimated **8–11% reduction in bid prices** — equivalent to **~$50–75B in savings** on the ~$583B state generation program plus the ~$322B grid hardening, over the full buildout.

This advantage is invisible in the Energy Act's budget tables because it is an Education Act cost producing an Energy Act benefit. The market transfers it automatically through price competition. It also explains why per-km burial costs may sit below international benchmarks: no real-world comparison has a million state-funded apprentices a year flowing into the trades.

## Design Philosophy (Informative)

The Energy Act provides the hardware — the buried lines, EMP-shielded substations, transformer reserves, and antifragile hubs that make New Crossroads the only nation on Earth with a CME-proof grid.

The Utilities Act provides the software — the market structure, concession model, access fees, anti-rot guarantees, and competition rules that keep the hardware alive and the market efficient.

Together, they complete the asset/operator separation pattern:

- Rail: State owns tracks → co-ops/corps run trains
- Energy: State owns grid → co-ops/corps generate and sell power → co-op concessions maintain the grid
- Communications: State owns fiber and towers → co-ops/corps provide internet and media

**Why state-owned grid, but not state-maintained.** Governments build and then forget. The state here owns the grid but hands its upkeep to co-ops paid to keep it in good shape and penalized when it slips (Utilities Act §1.4). Ring-fenced renewal funds prevent budget-raiding; the Special Auditors can prosecute neglect. The maintenance fight is won before it starts.

**Why competitive generation on a state-owned backbone.** Citizens choose their provider. Co-ops get the first call and the cheaper rate. The monopoly wires stay public and hardened. The dirty plants (WTE, coal, geo-coal, geo-WTE) are sovereign assets operated under license — the state captures the environmental liability, the corporation captures the operational efficiency.

**Why 21 hubs — and why the repository gets its own.** Each hub is a survivable island: reactor, hospital, airport, water treatment. When the grid goes dark, the hubs stay lit. The repository hub is different — no hospital, no airport, just continuous power for a facility that must never lose it, running on fuel mined from the same mountain.

**Why 70 years for the grid, not 50.** Because burying 150,000 km of cable is a multi-generational project, and a steady build rate over 70 years matches the apprentice pipeline, avoids boom-bust labor cycles, and doesn't compete with itself for materials. The hubs come first — year-one survival. The last farmhouse comes last. At every point, what matters most is already hardened.

**Why the highest per-capita generation in the world.** Because this is a fully electrified economy with no fossil transport fuel, a steel industry, a shipbuilding industry, a nuclear-technology industry, 45,000 hectares of heated greenhouses, 500,000 homes a year, 75,000 km of electrified rail, and 130 million people who all cook, heat, cool, and commute on electrons. 21.6 MWh per citizen is not a boast — it is what honest physics says this economy needs.

**Why the waste burns and the landfill shrinks.** A nation that recycles 79%, burns 15%, and landfills only 6% of inert residual — with zero-tax recyclers, at-cost utilities, and WTE plants that generate power from what's left — produces almost no organic waste in a hole in the ground. The recycling industry is the first line; WTE is the last; the landfill is for rubble and ash that cannot burn and is not worth sorting.

And when the next Carrington-level CME hits — and it will — New Crossroads will be the only nation with the lights still on.

That is the Energy Act.
