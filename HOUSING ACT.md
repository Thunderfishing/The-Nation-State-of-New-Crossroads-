## Overview

The **New Crossroads Housing Act of 2025 (Draft 2.2)** establishes a framework to deliver *affordable*, *durable*, and *energy-efficient* housing for 112 million citizens (94M Corporate Citizens, 67M middle class), supporting a $14.5T GDP (2025 baseline: 65% co-ops/$9.425T, 15% corporate/$2.175T, 20% informal/$2.9T). The system is designed to foster a **co-op dominant housing sector through incentives**, while supporting corporate builders and protecting universal access regardless of payment method.

It promotes **Durahomes**—modular homes built with hempcrete and low-carbon concrete, featuring earthship-inspired passive design (e.g., overhangs)—to achieve **86% homeownership** (96M citizens) by 2085.

Funded by **$150B/year from the Housing SWF** (part of the total $1.5–2.0T SWF capped at 12% of GDP per Monetary Act 9.1), **$94B Forced Savings** (scaling with population), and **DAI-adjusted loans** (0.5% floor for service-sector housing), it facilitates co-op/corporate production of materials and greenhouses (leased to co-ops), transitioning to a *free market* by 2035.

**Tax Framework (per Monetary Act 9.1):**
- Co-op housing FCLs: **5% profit tax on profit over $100,000** (no SWF contribution)
- Corporate housing builders: 0% under $100k, 15% $100k–$1M, 20% over $1M
- No co-op SWF recharge — eliminated

Regulations and incentives ensure 80% Durahomes, with **geothermal heating** incentives in feasible regions (e.g., Region 4, Ember Range) amplifying savings to **$3,000/year** ($30,000/10 years), while allowing individual choice (e.g., timber lodges). The act aligns with Energy Act 4.1, Government Act 4.17, Monetary Act 9.1, and Infrastructure Act 2.2 (DCALS dark corridor compliance for housing near insect corridors).

No binding sector quotas apply. The 65/15/20 figures in 2025 are **descriptive baselines**, not enforcement targets. Co-op housing production receives preference (first right of refusal).

## Section 1: Core Principles (New - Harmonized)

### 1.1 Asymmetric Scrutiny for Housing (Per Monetary Act 9.1)

| Level | Transaction Type | CWD Tracking | Reporting |
|-------|------------------|--------------|-----------|
| **Informal** | Cash rent payments, micro-mortgage payments, housing co-op fees below DLB threshold | Never collected | None. Constitutionally protected. |
| **Citizen** | BWC housing payments under $10,000/year | No tracking | None |
| **Middle** | BWC housing payments $10k–$100k/year | Aggregate totals only | Annual summary |
| **Elite** | Corporate housing transactions above $100k | Full tracking | Quarterly audits |
| **Institutional** | NHB operations, major housing loans | Full transparency | Public annual reports |

**Cash housing payments** are accepted at all credit union kiosks with no fee and no surveillance, per Monetary Act Section 1A (Informal Economy Guarantee).

### 1.2 DAI-Adjusted Loans for Housing (Per Monetary Act 1.6)

All loans for housing (construction, mortgages, geothermal retrofits, material production) receive DAI adjustment with **0.5% floor** (service-sector housing).

| Loan Type | Nominal Rate | DAI-Adjusted Effective (2025, BAR=2.6%) | Floor |
|-----------|--------------|------------------------------------------|-------|
| Durahome construction (co-op) | 4% | 1.4% | 0.5% |
| Geothermal retrofit | 4% | 1.4% | 0.5% |
| Hempcrete plant (co-op) | 4% | 1.4% | 0.5% |
| Mortgage (primary residence) | 4% | 1.4% | 0.5% |
| Non-Durahome construction | 5% | 2.4% | 1.0% |

### 1.3 Informal Sector Housing Access (Per Credit Union Act 5.1)

Informal workers and cash-based citizens have automatic access to:
- **Micro-loans** ($500, 4% nominal/~1.4% effective) for housing expenses: Self-attested income, no documentation, 24hr approval via CLS
- **Basic Wallet** (BWC Layer 2 access without formal income history)
- Credit unions may not require disclosure of cash transaction history for housing loans under $500,000

### 1.4 Co-op Housing Preference

Housing construction contracts, material production licenses, and greenhouse leases are issued preferentially to co-ops. If a co-op applies and meets all requirements, the contract or license shall be granted before any corporate application is considered. This is a structural preference (first right of refusal), not a binding quota.

### 1.5 Dark Corridor Compliance for Housing (Per Infrastructure Act 2.2)

Housing developments, geothermal compounds, and material production facilities located within 500m of elevated freeway insect corridors must comply with **DCALS**:
- Indirect red-spectrum motion lighting only (scaled by square footage per Infrastructure Act 2.4.1.3)
- Retroreflective markers (no powered lighting for edges)
- Retroreflective building paint as alternative compliance pathway
- No horizon-visible emission

**DCLTI tax incentive**: Certified housing developments receive property tax reduction (0.75% → 0.70%, or 0.68% for full retroreflective compliance).

## Section 2: Durahome Specifications (Updated with DAI and DCALS)

**Durahomes** are modular, hempcrete/low-carbon concrete homes with passive design and optional geothermal heating in feasible regions.

### 2.1 Materials (Unchanged, but DAI applies to loans)

| Material | Specification | DAI Floor |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| Hempcrete | 12" walls, R-30, $10/ft², sequesters 5 tons CO2 | 0.5% |
| Low-carbon concrete | 6" floors/frames, R-20, $12/ft², CO2-cured | 0.5% |
| Hemp fiberboards | 2x4-like framing, $1/board-foot | 0.5% |

### 2.2 Geothermal Heating (Feasibility Clarified)

- **Feasible regions**: Regions 4 (Geothermal City), 9 (Emberfall), 16 (Emberheart) — shallow subsurface heat (<200m depth)
- **Infeasible regions**: Regions 3 (Frostpeak), 5 (Heartland Plains), 7 (Desert Port) — use solar or electric heating
- **System**: Ground-source heat pumps (3–5 kW, $10,000/home), 400–600% efficient
- **Savings**: $2,000/year with geothermal (feasible regions); $1,200/year standard
- **Total savings**: $2,200–$3,000/year ($22,000–$30,000/10 years)

### 2.3 DCALS Compliance for Geothermal Compounds

Geothermal drilling sites and heat pump manufacturing facilities within 500m of elevated freeway insect corridors must comply with DCALS (Infrastructure Act 2.2). Eligible facilities receive DCLTI property tax reduction (0.75% → 0.70%, or 0.68% for full retroreflective compliance).

## Design Philosophy (Informative)

The Housing Act is the **shelter foundation** of New Crossroads. A home is not a commodity — it is the place where families raise children, where communities form, where the informal economy operates in cash without surveillance.

**Why DAI 0.5% floor for housing?**
- Housing is a service-sector necessity. The people who build and maintain homes receive substantial non-monetary compensation through the Education Act's mandatory service programs.
- A lower DAI floor keeps mortgages affordable as BWC appreciates. A 4% nominal mortgage becomes ~1.4% effective in 2025 — and stays affordable even as BWC rises to $300 by 2075.

**Why no co-op SWF recharge?**
- Housing co-ops already pay 5% profit tax over $100k. They do not pay an additional 2% of GDP into the SWF. That burden shifted to financial transactions, corporate profits, and resource extraction (per Monetary Act 9.1). This keeps capital available for building homes.

**Why co-op housing preference (not quotas)?**
- Co-ops receive first right of refusal for housing construction contracts, material production licenses, and greenhouse leases.
- If no qualified co-op applies, a corporation may take the contract.
- No binding quota means no enforcement action if co-op share fluctuates.
- The 65% figure from 2025 is a descriptive baseline, not a target.

**Why informal protection for cash housing payments?**
- A family paying rent in cash should not need a BWC wallet, a CWD profile, or a tax filing. The transaction is below the DLB threshold. It is protected. The state has no interest in tracking who pays rent to whom.
- Micro-mortgages for informal workers (self-attested income, 24hr approval) ensure that cash-based citizens are not excluded from homeownership.

**Why DCALS for housing near insect corridors?**
- Housing developments adjacent to elevated freeway insect corridors cannot use standard lighting. The red-spectrum, motion-activated, indirect systems described in DCALS keep residents safe without creating insect traps.
- Retroreflective building paint provides a passive alternative — zero energy, zero light emission.
- The DCLTI tax incentive (0.75% → 0.70% for DCALS, 0.68% for full retroreflective) makes compliance profitable.

**Why geothermal only in feasible regions?**
- Drilling 200m in the Ember Range costs $5,000–$7,000. Drilling 500m through Frostpeak bedrock costs $15,000–$20,000. Forcing geothermal on infeasible regions would make Durahomes unaffordable. The act is honest about limits.

The Housing Act is not about forcing everyone into identical boxes. It is about ensuring that every citizen — whether they pay in cash or BWC, whether they live in the Heartland Plains or the Ember Range — has access to a durable, efficient, affordable home. The Durahome with geothermal in Region 4, the timber lodge in Region 7, the cash-rented apartment in Region 1 — all are valid. All are protected.

When a family moves into a Durahome with red-trimmed pathways (DCALS-compliant, of course), pays their mortgage with DAI-adjusted BWC, and watches their energy bills drop to $400/year — they will know that New Crossroads built a housing system that respected both their wallet and the world outside their window.

That is the Housing Act.
